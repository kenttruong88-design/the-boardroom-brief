# The Waiting Room Where Everyone's Your Friend and the One Where You Wait for Everything

🇮🇪 Ireland vs 🇧🇷 Brazil: Healthcare and Expat Medical Culture

*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Ireland has a public healthcare system beloved in principle and dreaded in practice, a country where "I'll just pop into A&E" is a sentence nobody says without first clearing their entire evening and packing snacks. Brazil, despite its reputation abroad as chaotic, runs one of the most genuinely ambitious universal healthcare systems on the planet alongside a private sector so efficient it makes European private care look sluggish by comparison. Neither system matches its stereotype cleanly, and both will surprise you, usually while you're mildly unwell and in no mood for surprises.

I have sat in a Dublin A&E waiting room for nine hours with a suspected broken wrist, becoming genuinely emotionally attached to the strangers around me by hour six, and I have walked into a private clinic in São Paulo, been seen by a specialist within twenty minutes, and paid less out of pocket than a round of drinks in central London. These are both true. Neither cancels the other out.

## Do's & Don'ts

### 🇮🇪 Ireland

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Register with a GP immediately on arrival, waiting lists start from day one | Don't expect free GP visits without a medical card, most people pay each time |
| Consider private health insurance, it dramatically cuts specialist wait times | Don't treat A&E as a quick option, non-emergencies can mean an all-day wait |
| Apply for a medical card if eligible, income thresholds are more generous than assumed | Don't skip travel insurance if visiting, EHIC/GHIC covers EU citizens, not everyone |
| Ask your GP directly about public vs private referral routes, timelines differ hugely | Don't assume urgency will be obvious to triage staff, describe symptoms clearly and firmly |

### 🇧🇷 Brazil

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Get a private convênio (health plan) if you can afford one, it's standard for expats | Don't assume SUS quality is poor everywhere, it excels at emergency and vaccine care |
| Carry your SUS or convênio card at all times, it speeds up every registration | Don't skip travel or private insurance thinking SUS alone will cover elective needs |
| Use SUS for emergencies without hesitation, it's free and constitutionally guaranteed | Don't expect short waits for elective SUS procedures, those queues can run for months |
| Research pharmacies (farmácias) for minor issues, pharmacists diagnose informally and well | Don't assume every private clinic accepts every convênio, coverage networks vary |

## Ireland: Free in Theory, Slow in Practice

Irish healthcare operates on a hybrid public-private model that confuses even long-term residents, let alone newly arrived expats trying to figure out whether a GP visit is free, discounted, or full price, which depends entirely on whether you hold a medical card, a GP visit card, or neither. Most working adults without a medical card pay out of pocket for GP visits, typically €50 to €70, a detail that genuinely shocks visitors from countries with fully free primary care, who assumed "public healthcare" meant something closer to universally free at the point of use.

The public system's real reputation problem is wait times, and it's earned. Hospital waiting lists for non-urgent specialist appointments and elective procedures routinely stretch into many months, sometimes years, for certain specialties, a chronic pressure point that dominates Irish political discourse and news cycles with grim regularity. A&E departments bear the brunt of this bottleneck, with patients frequently spending the better part of a day, occasionally longer, on trolleys in corridors waiting to be seen, admitted, or discharged.

Private health insurance has consequently become close to a practical necessity rather than a luxury for anyone who can afford it, cutting specialist referral wait times from many months down to weeks, sometimes days. A significant majority of the population carries some form of private cover, a striking statistic for a country with an ostensibly universal public system, and one that tells you plainly which route locals actually trust for anything beyond genuine emergencies.

What redeems the system, and what expats consistently mention once they've been through it, is the quality of care once you're actually being seen. Irish medical staff are highly trained, the emergency care itself is genuinely excellent once triage processes you, and GPs tend to build real, ongoing relationships with patients that many more "efficient" systems abroad simply don't offer. The wait is brutal. The care, once it arrives, generally isn't.

## Brazil: Constitutional Universal Care Meets a Thriving Private Sector

Brazil's SUS, the Sistema Único de Saúde, is a constitutionally guaranteed universal healthcare system available free at the point of use to literally anyone within Brazil's borders, citizen or not, a detail that surprises expats who assumed access required residency paperwork or insurance. It's genuinely one of the more ambitious universal healthcare commitments globally, and its emergency care, vaccination programs, and infectious disease response have drawn real international recognition, most visibly during coordinated national vaccination campaigns.

The catch, and it's a significant one, is that SUS's elective and specialist care queues can be extraordinarily long, stretching months for certain procedures, particularly outside major metropolitan hospitals. This gap has produced Brazil's enormous private healthcare sector, accessed via a convênio, an employer-sponsored or privately purchased health plan that a substantial share of the urban middle class and virtually all expats carry alongside, or instead of, relying on SUS.

Private Brazilian healthcare, once you're inside a convênio network, is often startlingly fast and high quality relative to cost — same-day specialist appointments, modern private hospitals in São Paulo and Rio that rival anything in Western Europe, and out-of-pocket costs for procedures that would bankrupt an uninsured American patient. The system's real complexity lies in the network fragmentation: not every convênio covers every clinic or hospital, and expats routinely get caught out discovering, mid-appointment, that their plan doesn't actually cover the specialist they've just seen.

Pharmacies deserve particular mention as an underrated pillar of Brazilian healthcare culture — farmácias are staffed by knowledgeable pharmacists who routinely diagnose and treat minor ailments informally, dispensing advice and medication for everyday complaints without requiring a formal doctor's visit, a genuinely efficient first line of care that visitors from more tightly regulated pharmaceutical markets find refreshingly practical.

## The Verdict

Ireland wins on consistency of care quality once you're actually seen, and on the reassurance of a fully public safety net that, however slow, genuinely won't leave anyone without emergency treatment. Brazil wins on speed and cost-effectiveness within its private tier, and on the sheer ambition of SUS's universal, no-questions-asked emergency access. If forced to be seriously unwell in either country tomorrow, I'd take Brazil's private convênio system for anything non-emergency, and Ireland's public A&E, trolley and all, is still preferable to no system whatsoever for the genuinely urgent.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *r/ireland — paraphrased: "Nine hours in A&E with a suspected fracture, made three new friends and learned an entire family's life story before I was even X-rayed. The care itself, once you're in, is excellent."*

> *Internations São Paulo — paraphrased: "Got a convênio through my employer and honestly it's better than the insurance I had back home. Saw a dermatologist within a week for something that would've taken three months in my home country."*

> *expat.com Brazil — paraphrased: "SUS saved my life during a genuine emergency, completely free, no paperwork drama. But I still use my private plan for anything that isn't urgent, because the SUS queue for that was eight months."*

## Conclusion

Ireland's public system is a genuine safety net weighed down by chronic underinvestment, delivering excellent care to those patient enough, or desperate enough, to wait for it. Brazil's dual system proves that universal access and private efficiency can coexist, provided you understand which door to walk through for which problem. Expats in either country who skip researching the system before they need it will find out the hard way, usually mid-crisis, which queue they're actually standing in. Sort your healthcare paperwork in month one. Your future self, inevitably unwell at the least convenient moment, will thank you.
