# Haggling Is a Sport in One Country and a Faux Pas in the Other

🇹🇿 Tanzania vs 🇭🇷 Croatia

*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Shopping tells you everything about how a country feels about money, time, and confrontation, and few pairings illustrate this better than Tanzania and Croatia. In Dar es Salaam's Kariakoo market, the asking price is a conversational opening gambit, not a final offer, and refusing to haggle is practically an insult to the vendor's craft. In Croatia's open-air tržnice, the price is the price, stated once, calmly, and you will look faintly ridiculous attempting to negotiate it down over a kilo of tomatoes.

I have embarrassed myself in both directions — haggling pointlessly in Split, and paying full asking price on my first week in Kariakoo like the obvious tourist mark I was. Neither mistake was cheap, financially or socially.

## Do's & Don'ts: Tanzania

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Haggle on everything except food staples and fixed-price shops | Accept the first price offered — it's assumed you'll counter |
| Build a relationship with one or two regular vendors for better long-term prices | Haggle aggressively or rudely — it should feel like banter, not a fight |
| Bring small notes; large bills slow every transaction down | Expect fixed opening hours everywhere — many stalls run on their own schedule |
| Learn basic Swahili numbers — it changes the whole negotiation dynamic | Rush the process; a good haggle takes real, unhurried time |

## Do's & Don'ts: Croatia

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Arrive early — the best produce at the tržnica is gone by 9am | Attempt to haggle over food; it's simply not how the market works |
| Bring exact change where possible — vendors appreciate it | Touch produce without asking first at some stalls, especially with older sellers |
| Respect the posted market hours — most close by early afternoon | Assume every seller speaks English; a few Croatian pleasantries go a long way |
| Ask sellers directly which produce is local and in season — they love being asked | Confuse the daily tržnica with tourist souvenir stalls — prices and etiquette differ hugely |

## Tanzania: The Price Is Just the Opening Line

Kariakoo market in Dar es Salaam is less a shopping trip than a full-contact social performance, and the first price any vendor gives you is understood by everyone, instantly, as a starting bid rather than a figure anyone actually expects you to pay. Haggling here isn't rude — refusing to haggle is rude, a kind of disengagement that vendors read as either naivety or, worse, disrespect for the ritual itself.

The skill is real and it's learnable. You counter low but not insultingly low. You feign walking away, and about a third of the time it works. You build rapport, ask about the vendor's day, comment on the quality of the fabric or the freshness of the fish, and only then circle back to the number. It is, at its best, genuinely enjoyable — theatre with a financial stake, conducted with humour rather than hostility.

Where foreigners consistently blunder is in either direction: haggling too aggressively, which reads as genuinely disrespectful rather than sporting, or not haggling at all, which marks you instantly as a tourist mark and sets a bad precedent for every future visit to that stall. The market rewards regulars — vendors remember faces, and a customer who returns weekly, greets by name, and negotiates fairly earns noticeably better prices over time than any one-off tourist ever will, however skilled their haggling.

## Croatia: The Price Is the Price, Full Stop

Croatian tržnice — the open-air markets that anchor daily life in nearly every town — run on an entirely different social contract: fixed, fair, take-it-or-leave-it pricing, stated plainly, with zero expectation of negotiation. Attempt to haggle over a punnet of strawberries in Split's Pazar market and you will be met with a polite but unmistakable confusion, the kind reserved for someone who has clearly misunderstood which country they're in.

This isn't rigidity for its own sake — it reflects a broader Croatian retail culture built on trust, consistency, and a certain quiet pride in fair dealing. The vendor grew the tomatoes, or knows exactly who did; the price reflects the season, the quality, and the actual cost of production, not a starting position designed to be talked down. Bargaining implies the seller was trying to overcharge you in the first place, and that assumption itself is the insult.

What Croatian markets do reward is punctuality and familiarity rather than negotiation skill. Arrive at 7am and the produce is extraordinary — that morning's catch, that morning's harvest. Arrive at 11am and you're picking through what's left. Regulars get better cuts, better recommendations, and occasionally a small unprompted discount offered as genuine goodwill rather than the result of any bargaining at all — the inverse transaction of Kariakoo, where goodwill is earned through negotiation rather than despite it.

## The Verdict

Tanzania wins if you enjoy shopping as sport, relationship, and performance rolled into one transaction. Croatia wins if you value transparency, trust the price on the ticket, and would rather spend your morning drinking coffee than negotiating over strawberries.

I've come to prefer Kariakoo, if I'm honest — there's something alive about a transaction that's also a conversation. But I've never once felt cheated in a Croatian tržnica, and that particular peace of mind has its own quiet value.

## Conclusion

Tanzania and Croatia have built opposite economies of trust around the same basic act of buying vegetables. Kariakoo trusts negotiation to arrive at a fair price through relationship and performance. The Croatian tržnica trusts the vendor to have set a fair price already, and expects you to trust it too. Get the etiquette backwards in either direction and you'll either overpay for months or cause genuine, if mild, offence over a bag of strawberries. Learn the local rules before you learn the local language — in both places, they'll get you further, faster.
