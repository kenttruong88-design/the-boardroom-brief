# Two Countries Where the Altitude Checks You In Before the Hotel Does

🇧🇴 Bolivia 🇳🇵 Nepal
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

I arrived in La Paz feeling smug about a well-reviewed hotel booking, only to spend my first night gasping slightly at 3,640 metres while the front desk staff, entirely unbothered, handed me a cup of coca leaf tea like it was the most routine amenity in the world, which, there, it is. In Nepal, checking into a trekking teahouse in the Annapurna region meant accepting that "hotel" was a generous word for a wooden room with a shared bathroom down a freezing corridor, and that the real amenity was the dal bhat served communally by a lodge family who'd clearly done this ten thousand times before. Bolivia and Nepal both make you negotiate with altitude before you negotiate with a hotel receptionist. Neither apologises for it.

If your idea of hotel culture is thread count and turndown service, both of these countries would like to introduce you to a slightly different definition of hospitality, one measured more in oxygen than amenities.

## Do's & Don'ts

**Bolivia**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Accept coca leaf tea (mate de coca) on arrival in high-altitude cities — it genuinely helps | Book strenuous activities for your first day or two in La Paz — acclimatise first |
| Expect variable heating; many mid-range hotels aren't built for how cold nights get | Assume star ratings translate directly — a "4-star" here may differ from Western expectations |
| Ask staff for altitude advice; most are well practised in helping guests adjust | Overpack fancy attire; Bolivian accommodation culture, even upscale, trends practical |

**Nepal**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Expect teahouse lodges on trekking routes rather than hotels — shared bathrooms are common | Expect reliable hot showers or Wi-Fi much above 3,000 metres — both are inconsistent at best |
| Order dal bhat at teahouses — it's the standard, filling, and usually the best option | Assume trekking lodge prices are fixed; they can rise the higher and more remote you go |
| Bring your own sleeping bag liner for teahouses — bedding warmth varies widely | Expect to book trekking lodges far in advance outside peak season — it's often first-come |

## Bolivia: Hospitality at 3,600 Metres and Rising

Bolivian accommodation culture cannot be separated from the country's staggering altitude, which shapes everything from how hotels are built to what's offered at check-in. La Paz itself sits at an elevation most world capitals would consider unlivable, and reputable hotels have adapted with a set of quiet, practised rituals — coca leaf tea offered on arrival, oxygen available on request in some properties, staff genuinely well-versed in advising new arrivals to take the first day slow rather than push straight into sightseeing.

What surprises visitors used to more standardised international hotel grading is how loosely star ratings translate locally. A "4-star" Bolivian hotel may deliver wonderful hospitality and genuinely charming architecture while still having inconsistent heating or water pressure that would raise eyebrows elsewhere — this isn't negligence, it's a reflection of infrastructure genuinely built for a different set of environmental challenges than, say, a European city hotel. Heating in particular catches people off guard: nights in La Paz or Potosí can drop sharply, and not every property, even reputable ones, has fully solved for it.

Beyond the practical altitude adaptations, Bolivian hospitality carries a warmth and unhurried quality that rewards patience — check-in conversations tend to be personal rather than transactional, staff often take genuine pride in offering local advice beyond their formal job description, and the overall accommodation culture leans practical and welcoming over polished and corporate. Expats who arrive expecting slick, internationally uniform hotel service will find Bolivia's version rougher around the edges but considerably more human.

## Nepal: The Teahouse Is the Real Hotel Industry

Nepal's accommodation culture splits cleanly into two worlds: city hotels in Kathmandu or Pokhara, which function reasonably close to international norms, and the teahouse lodges that dominate the trekking routes, which are an entirely different institution altogether. On routes like the Annapurna Circuit or Everest Base Camp trek, these family-run lodges provide simple rooms, shared bathrooms, and a communal dining hall built around the dal bhat set meal, which functions as fuel, social hub, and cultural centrepiece all at once.

What trekkers consistently underestimate is how sharply amenities decline with altitude and remoteness — hot showers become a paid, inconsistent luxury above certain elevations, Wi-Fi turns spotty to nonexistent, and heating is often limited to a single communal stove around which the entire lodge gathers each evening, less out of charm and more out of genuine cold-driven necessity. Pricing for food and lodging also rises the higher and more remote the teahouse, a logical consequence of everything needing to be carried in by porter or mule, though it still occasionally startles trekkers expecting flat, predictable rates.

The relational warmth of Nepali teahouse culture, though, is where it distinguishes itself — lodge families often know their regular trekking guides personally, offer genuine local knowledge about conditions ahead, and create a communal dining atmosphere where trekkers from wildly different countries end up sharing a table and a pot of milk tea by necessity rather than choice. It's accommodation reduced to its most functional, human essentials: a bed, a hot meal, and a stove to huddle around, delivered with a warmth that no five-star lobby could really replicate.

## The Verdict

Nepal wins, marginally, on the strength of its teahouse culture's sheer necessity-driven warmth — there's something about a communal stove and a shared plate of dal bhat at 4,000 metres that produces a more genuine hospitality than any hotel amenity list could manufacture. Bolivia's altitude hospitality, the coca tea and unhurried check-ins, is charming but operates within a more conventional hotel framework that occasionally over-promises relative to what it delivers. Nepal's teahouses never oversell themselves; they simply are what they are, and that honesty, at altitude, is worth more than a star rating.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *Reddit r/Bolivia — paraphrased: a visitor described booking a well-reviewed La Paz hotel and being surprised the heating was essentially a single space heater, standard for the altitude and building style.*

> *Reddit r/Nepal — paraphrased: a trekker recounted paying triple the lowland price for a hot shower at a high-altitude teahouse and being told, cheerfully, "everything comes up by mule, that's the price of hot water here."*

> *Internations La Paz — paraphrased: an expat noted that hotel staff routinely and unprompted offer altitude sickness advice to new arrivals, treating it as a standard part of check-in rather than an optional extra.*

## Conclusion

Bolivia and Nepal both make you reckon with elevation before you reckon with anything resembling standard hotel service, and both, in their own way, turn that limitation into genuine warmth rather than apology. Bring a sleeping bag liner, drink the coca tea when offered, and lower your expectations for consistent hot water in both places. What you'll get instead, at altitude, is hospitality stripped of pretence — and honestly, a stove to huddle around beats a turndown chocolate most nights.
