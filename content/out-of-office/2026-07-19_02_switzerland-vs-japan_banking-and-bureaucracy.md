# Two Nations That Turned Paperwork Into a Load-Bearing Wall of Society

🇨🇭 Switzerland 🇯🇵 Japan
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

There is a specific kind of despair reserved for the moment you realise a country's entire bureaucratic machine was built before you were born and has no intention of updating itself for your convenience. I have felt it in a marble-floored Zurich bank lobby, waiting for a advisor to finish a coffee before acknowledging my existence, and I have felt it in a Tokyo ward office, watching a clerk stamp a form four separate times with four separate rubber seals for a task that required, structurally, zero seals. Switzerland and Japan are both extraordinarily competent countries that have decided competence and convenience are unrelated concepts. Everything works. Nothing is quick. And in both places, you will, at some point, need a fax machine.

If you think "developed country" means "frictionless admin," these two will correct you within a week, politely, in triplicate.

## Do's & Don'ts

**Switzerland**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Bring every original document, plus a photocopy, plus your residency permit, to any bank meeting | Expect an account without a confirmed address and often a job contract |
| Get comfortable with postal payment slips — Switzerland still loves them | Assume online banking replaces the in-person relationship manager entirely |
| Book an appointment for basic account setup — walk-ins get politely stonewalled | Be late; punctuality is the one bureaucratic currency that actually buys goodwill |

**Japan**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Get a hanko (personal stamp) — some institutions still expect one over a signature | Assume a smartphone can replace a physical bankbook for older institutions |
| Bring a Japanese-speaking colleague or a translation app to your first bank visit | Expect any government office to email you — plan on registered mail and visits |
| Register your address change immediately — everything downstream depends on it | Lose your inkan or bankbook; replacing either is its own bureaucratic saga |

## Switzerland: Efficient, Except for the Part That Involves You

Switzerland has a reputation for precision that is, broadly, deserved — the trains really do run on time, the water really is drinkable from public fountains, and the bureaucracy really does grind with the same watchmaker's patience the country is famous for. What that reputation omits is how much of that precision is aimed at institutional stability rather than your personal convenience. Opening a bank account as a newcomer without a permanent employment contract is a documented minor ordeal — banks want proof of address, proof of residency permit, sometimes proof of employment, and they want it presented in person, at an appointment, during business hours that assume you have nothing else going on.

Then there's the postal payment slip, the orange or red slip that Swiss households still use for everything from rent to dentist bills, which coexists with a mobile banking app in a way that suggests the country simply couldn't bring itself to retire it. Expats routinely report the peculiar experience of being fully digital in every other aspect of Swiss life while manually entering a 27-digit reference number into an app because the underlying document is, culturally, still a piece of paper.

Swiss bureaucracy rewards patience and punishes improvisation. Show up prepared, with the right stack of documents, at the right appointment time, and the system works beautifully. Show up hoping to "figure it out" and you will be sent home to book an appointment for the privilege of figuring it out later. The compensation is that once you're in the system, it is genuinely reliable — Swiss institutions rarely lose your paperwork, rarely make errors, and will honour a commitment made twenty years ago without blinking. It's bureaucracy as a long-term relationship, not a transaction.

## Japan: The Stamp Is the System

Japan's bureaucracy runs on a parallel logic that looks, to the uninitiated, almost pre-digital — and in many pockets of it, genuinely is. The hanko, a small carved personal stamp, still carries legal weight that a signature alone sometimes doesn't, and expats are routinely startled to learn they need to register one to open certain accounts or sign certain leases. Fax machines remain in active professional use, not as a nostalgic quirk but as a functioning part of how offices actually communicate, and government correspondence still frequently arrives by registered post requiring an in-person signature at your door.

What makes Japanese bureaucracy distinct from Switzerland's is the sheer procedural layering — one task often requires three separate offices, each with their own form, each requiring the previous office's stamped confirmation before proceeding. Setting up a bank account, a phone plan, and a residence registration in your first month is less a checklist than an escort mission, where each item depends on having completed the last in the correct sequence, at the correct window, with the correct stamp.

And yet, once inside the system, Japanese bureaucracy is astonishingly precise and almost never adversarial — clerks are unfailingly polite, procedures are followed exactly as written with no favouritism and no shortcuts either way, and the eventual outcome is reliable to the point of monotony. The frustration isn't corruption or incompetence; it's volume. Everything requires more steps than seems necessary, executed flawlessly, by people who will not skip a single one regardless of how much you'd like them to.

## The Verdict

Switzerland wins this one, marginally, because its bureaucracy at least resolves into genuine long-term stability — open the account once, endure the appointment, and you're set for years. Japan's system asks you to keep feeding it stamps and registered mail indefinitely, a bureaucracy that never quite lets you stop performing the ritual. Both countries could unionise paperwork into an Olympic sport. Switzerland would win gold for precision. Japan would win gold for endurance. Neither would win anything for speed, and neither cares that you noticed.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *Reddit r/Switzerland — paraphrased: a newcomer was rejected for a basic account by three banks before landing one, because none would open without a signed permanent contract, which they didn't yet have because they needed the account to receive their first paycheck.*

> *Reddit r/japanlife — paraphrased: someone described losing their hanko and discovering that replacing it required a notarized process nearly as involved as the original registration.*

> *Internations Zurich — paraphrased: an expat recounted needing an actual in-person appointment just to ask a clarifying question about a form, because the bank did not accept clarifying questions by phone.*

## Conclusion

Neither Switzerland nor Japan will apologise for the paperwork, because as far as either is concerned, the paperwork is the point — proof that things are done properly, thoroughly, without shortcuts for anyone. Arrive with photocopies, patience, and low expectations for speed, and both systems will eventually reward you with something rare: bureaucracy that actually works exactly as promised, once you've suffered through learning how it wants to be approached. Just don't expect either of them to text you back.
