# Nobody Knocks on Your Door in Ljubljana, and Everybody Does in Turrialba

🇨🇷 Costa Rica vs 🇸🇮 Slovenia

*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

I have lived in fourteen places, and I can tell you with total confidence that "community" means wildly different things depending on which hemisphere your rubbish bin is in. In Costa Rica, community means your neighbour walking into your kitchen unannounced holding a mango and an opinion about your love life. In Slovenia, community means a beautifully maintained shared stairwell where everyone silently agrees never to make eye contact before 9am.

Both work. Neither is "friendlier," whatever the brochures claim. One is loud affection dressed as nosiness, the other is quiet respect dressed as coldness. I have lived both, and I still haven't decided which one broke me more thoroughly.

## Do's & Don'ts: Costa Rica

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Greet every single neighbour, every single time, even the fifth time that day | Walk past someone's porch without a "buenas" — it will be noted |
| Accept the unsolicited fruit, coffee, or tamales | Refuse a gift outright — it reads as rejection, not politeness |
| Expect your business to be known within 48 hours of moving in | Assume "pura vida" means nobody's paying attention — they are |
| Join the WhatsApp barrio group immediately | Skip the community mingas (volunteer clean-up days) — attendance is basically mandatory socially |

## Do's & Don'ts: Slovenia

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Keep the shared hallway (hodnik) spotless — it's sacred | Make noise after 10pm, even mild noise, even a chair scraping |
| Say a brief, formal hello to neighbours — nothing more required | Expect an invitation inside after a friendly hallway chat |
| Respect the cleaning rota pinned by the mailboxes — it is law | Assume Slovenian reserve is unfriendliness — it isn't, it's just not your business yet |
| Learn ten words of Slovene; it buys you enormous goodwill | Bring up the "Balkans" label — Slovenians will correct you, firmly |

## Costa Rica: The Barrio Owns You Now

Move into a Costa Rican barrio and within a week you will know, and be known by, everyone within a three-house radius, whether you consented to this arrangement or not. Someone's abuela will materialise at your gate holding a plate of something you didn't ask for and won't be allowed to refuse. Your dog's name will be known before you've unpacked the boxes. This is not a metaphor — I once had a neighbour in Turrialba correct my Spanish pronunciation of my own street name, unprompted, while handing me a mango.

The architecture encourages it: low walls, open porches, plastic chairs dragged out onto the pavement at 5pm like clockwork so everyone can watch the street and comment on it. Costa Ricans call this pura vida, a phrase tourists have flattened into a beer-koozie slogan but which actually describes a genuine social contract — life is better witnessed together, in public, loudly, with opinions freely exchanged about your parenting, your car, and your love life.

The flip side is a total absence of privacy as you understand it. Nothing is anonymous. Arguments carry through open windows. Deliveries are received and redistributed by whichever neighbour happens to be sitting outside. If you value being an unknown quantity to the people who live near you, Costa Rica will find this concept baffling and possibly a little sad. Community here isn't optional infrastructure — it's the operating system, and you're already logged in whether you wanted an account or not.

What foreigners get wrong is assuming this warmth is performative. It isn't. It's also not infinitely patient — ignore the barrio, skip the minga clean-up days, decline enough invitations, and you become "the strange gringo" fast, a reputation that sticks harder than any I've encountered elsewhere.

## Slovenia: The Hallway Is a Courtroom

Slovenia runs on the opposite premise entirely: community as quiet, mutual, near-obsessive maintenance of shared space, conducted with almost total social distance from the humans sharing it. Every apartment block has a cleaning rota — the hodnik schedule — taped to a corkboard, and you will follow it to the letter or become, silently, the most discussed person in the building without a single word being said to your face.

Slovenians are not cold, despite what every lazy travel blog insists. They are private, precise, and deeply loyal once you're actually inside the circle — which can take years, not weeks. A neighbour who's greeted you politely every morning for eighteen months without once inviting you in is not being rude. She's simply not finished deciding about you yet, and rushing that process marks you as presumptuous, not friendly.

What impressed me most, living outside Ljubljana, was how functional the reserve is. Nobody's business bleeds into anyone else's. Disputes get resolved via a note left under a door, never a doorstep confrontation. The result is a neighbourhood that runs with the quiet precision of a Swiss watch and the emotional temperature of a filing cabinet — and somehow, once you're finally let in, that same precision becomes fierce, dependable loyalty. Slovenian neighbours will not chat you up. They will, however, notice if your lights don't come on for three days and actually do something about it.

## The Verdict

If you want a village that adopts you on day one, whether you asked for it or not, Costa Rica wins outright — nowhere I've lived makes you feel less alone, for better and occasionally for exhausting worse. If you want your private life to remain private while still being quietly, competently looked after, Slovenia is unmatched — it just asks you to earn it first, at a pace measured in seasons, not days.

Neither model is broken. What's broken is expecting one to behave like the other. Import Costa Rican boundary-lessness into a Ljubljana stairwell and you'll be the neighbourhood pariah by October. Import Slovenian reserve into a Turrialba barrio and you'll be gently, relentlessly interrogated until you crack.

## Conclusion

Community, it turns out, is a design choice, not a universal constant, and both Costa Rica and Slovenia have committed to theirs with total conviction. One hands you a family you never applied for; the other hands you a silence you have to actively, patiently dismantle. Both will teach you something uncomfortable about how much personal space you actually need versus how much you've simply gotten used to. My honest advice, after fourteen moves: don't try to change either system to suit you. Bring fruit to the barrio. Respect the hodnik rota. And stop calling either country "exotic" — Turrialba and Ljubljana are just home to people who've been doing this far longer, and far better, than you have.
