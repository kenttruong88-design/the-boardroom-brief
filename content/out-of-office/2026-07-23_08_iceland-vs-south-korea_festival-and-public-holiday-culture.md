# Iceland Celebrates by Setting Things on Fire. Korea Celebrates by Going Home to Mother.

🇮🇸 Iceland 🇰🇷 South Korea
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Icelandic festival culture peaks, quite literally, in massive bonfires lit on New Year's Eve and Þorláksmessa, entire communities gathering around flames the size of a small building, drinking, singing, and treating fire as both celebration and exorcism of the dark winter it's been fighting for months. Korean festival culture peaks around Chuseok, the harvest festival, when something like a third of the entire population gets in a car or on a train, simultaneously, to sit in a specific living room and perform a specific set of rites for specific ancestors, and the traffic reports genuinely become a national news event.

One country celebrates by lighting things on fire in public. The other celebrates by going home, which — logistically — turns out to be the more dramatic of the two.

## Do's & Don'ts: Iceland

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Attend a New Year's Eve bonfire — it's genuinely one of the best things Iceland does | Bring your own fireworks without checking local rules; regulations vary by area |
| Embrace the 24-hour daylight of summer festivals like Þjóðhátíð | Expect quiet on New Year's Eve; Reykjavík's fireworks display is famously enormous and unregulated-feeling |
| Dress for genuinely unpredictable weather, even mid-celebration | Assume all festivals are tourist-facing; many are deeply local, family, or regional events |

## Do's & Don'ts: South Korea

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Book transport for Chuseok or Lunar New Year weeks in advance — it sells out fast | Plan to travel during Chuseok without booking early; roads and trains are gridlocked nationwide |
| Accept an invitation to observe (not necessarily perform) ancestral rites if offered | Take photos during charye (ancestral rites) without explicit permission |
| Expect most small businesses to close for several days straight | Assume Chuseok is just "Korean Thanksgiving" and nothing more — the ritual weight runs deeper |

## Iceland: Fire as National Catharsis

Icelandic festival culture makes complete sense once you understand the darkness it's responding to. Months of minimal daylight, brutal weather, and isolation have produced a culture that treats fire, light, and gathering as genuinely restorative acts, not just festive decoration. The New Year's Eve bonfires — dozens across Reykjavík alone, organised by neighbourhood associations — draw entire communities who bring their own wood contributions throughout the year specifically for this one burning. It's followed by a fireworks display so extensive and so lightly regulated by international standards that Reykjavík's sky on December 31st looks less like a show and more like a small-scale aerial celebration undertaken by the entire population simultaneously, because it largely is — Icelanders buy and set off their own fireworks in huge quantities.

Þjóðhátíð, the summer festival held in the Westman Islands, is the other pole: weeks of near-endless daylight used for an outdoor gathering involving music, bonfires again, and a specific local folk song that reduces grown adults to tears on cue, every single year, without fail. It's less about spectacle and more about a community actively using its brief window of good weather and light for maximum collective joy before the dark returns.

What's notable is how participatory Icelandic festival culture is compared to spectator-driven celebrations elsewhere — you're not watching the bonfire, you contributed wood to it; you're not watching fireworks, you're setting some off yourself, slightly recklessly, in your own back garden.

## South Korea: The Festival as Filial Obligation

Chuseok is frequently translated for foreigners as "Korean Thanksgiving," which undersells it considerably. It's a three-day (often effectively week-long, accounting for travel) national event built around ancestral rites, family reunion, and a genuinely enormous logistical migration — thepercentage of the population that travels during Chuseok is high enough that road and rail authorities treat it as a full civil-engineering event, publishing detailed traffic forecasts down to the hour.

The core of the holiday is charye, a formal ancestral memorial rite performed at home, typically led by the eldest son's household, involving a precisely arranged table of food, specific bowing sequences, and real ritual seriousness — this isn't background tradition, it's actively practiced and taken seriously by families across generations, including plenty of younger Koreans who might otherwise describe themselves as not particularly religious.

Songpyeon, the crescent-shaped rice cakes made specifically for the holiday, get made collectively — often multiple generations of women in a family, though this gendered division is a genuine point of ongoing social tension in modern Korea worth being aware of rather than romanticising uncritically.

What foreigners often miss is the pressure underneath the tradition: Chuseok can be a source of real stress, particularly for daughters-in-law expected to cook extensively, and for unmarried or divorced adults facing pointed family questions about their life choices during the extended, close-quarters gathering. It's warm and meaningful, but it isn't uncomplicated.

## The Verdict

Iceland's festival culture is about collective catharsis in response to an extreme environment — fire and light as a direct antidote to darkness, deeply participatory and refreshingly unpretentious. Korea's is about reinforcing lineage, obligation, and family structure with a seriousness that produces both genuine warmth and genuine pressure, in roughly equal measure. If you want to feel the immediate, visceral joy of a culture celebrating together, Iceland's bonfires are hard to beat. If you want to understand how a modern, hyper-connected society still organises itself around ancestral duty, Chuseok will teach you more than any museum could.

## Conclusion

Iceland and Korea have built festival cultures around entirely different needs — one responding to an unforgiving climate with fire and collective joy, the other reinforcing family and ancestral structure with ritual precision and real logistical scale. Neither is performative for outsiders; both are, at core, deeply local acts that happen to also be extraordinary to witness. Go to Iceland for the bonfire. Go to Korea for the reminder that some traditions still run the whole country, traffic jams and all.
