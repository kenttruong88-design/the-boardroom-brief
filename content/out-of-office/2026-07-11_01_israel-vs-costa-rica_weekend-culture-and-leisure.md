# Tel Aviv Shuts Down for God; San José Shuts Down for a Nap. Neither Will Apologise.

🇮🇱 Israel vs 🇨🇷 Costa Rica

*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

There's a particular kind of panic that hits every newcomer to Tel Aviv at 3pm on a Friday, when the supermarkets suddenly resemble a bank run and the buses start looking suspiciously infrequent. It's the same panic that hits every newcomer to San José at 2pm on any day of the week ending in "y," except in Costa Rica nobody's rushing anywhere, they've simply decided the day is over and gone to sit under a tree. Both countries have built entire national identities around telling you, gently but firmly, that your Western productivity brain is not welcome here on the weekend.

I've lived through both shutdowns, and I can report they are philosophically opposite events wearing the same closed-sign costume. Israel's weekend pause is a covenant — thousands of years of tradition compressed into twenty-five hours of no buses, no shops, and family dinners so intense they should require a permit. Costa Rica's pause is more of a shrug, a national agreement that since nobody's going to get rich chasing tourists on a Sunday, everyone may as well go to the beach. One is sacred. The other is just extremely, defiantly relaxed. Guess which one makes better small talk at a dinner party and which one actually improves your blood pressure.

## Do's & Don'ts

**🇮🇱 Israel**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Stock up on groceries by Friday noon | Assume you can find an open pharmacy on Saturday afternoon |
| Accept every Shabbat dinner invitation you get | Show up empty-handed — bring wine or flowers |
| Learn the light-rail's Friday shutdown schedule | Plan a Saturday road trip assuming petrol stations are all open |
| Embrace the beach-and-hummus Saturday ritual | Call it "the weekend" to religious friends — it's Shabbat |

**🇨🇷 Costa Rica**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Build your errands around a 10am–2pm window | Expect anything, anywhere, to be quick |
| Learn to love a Sunday "asado" that starts at noon and ends at dusk | Book a Sunday afternoon business meeting — nobody's coming |
| Get used to church bells resetting the town's rhythm | Confuse pura vida with laziness — it's a boundary, not an excuse |
| Say yes to spontaneous beach plans | Assume public transport runs on a real schedule on Sundays |

## Israel: A Weekend With Rules, Enforced by an Entire Country

Shabbat in Israel doesn't creep up on you, it detonates. From Friday afternoon the whole country visibly braces — supermarket queues stretch into the car park, buses thin out earlier than advertised, and by sundown a switch flips: shops shutter, public transport in most cities stops entirely, and the streets of Tel Aviv, usually a symphony of scooters and shouting, go eerily, almost suspiciously quiet. Jerusalem takes it further; entire neighbourhoods become genuinely impassable to traffic, and turning up in a car where you shouldn't will earn you looks that could curdle milk.

What foreigners get wrong is assuming this is a religious inconvenience to be worked around. It isn't. It's the organising principle of Israeli weekend life, observant or not. Secular Tel Avivians who wouldn't set foot in a synagogue all year will still treat Friday night dinner as sacred — extended family crammed around a table, someone's grandmother judging the salad, an argument about politics that somehow never actually ends the evening. Saturday itself splits into two Israels: the one that goes properly still, and the beach cities — Tel Aviv, Herzliya, Eilat — where Saturday becomes the single most important leisure day of the week, packed wall-to-wall with matkot paddleball, dogs, and enough hummus to reroute a river.

The genius, and the maddening part, is the total commitment. There's no "we'll stay open for tourists" compromise. If you didn't plan your Friday around the closure, that's your problem, and Israelis will tell you so without much sympathy — they've been doing this dance since before your country existed. Public transport's absence forces genuine community: you walk to your neighbour's, you stay the night rather than call a cab that doesn't exist. It's inconvenient by design, and that's precisely the point — a mandated, nationwide refusal to let commerce eat the one day meant for people.

## Costa Rica: The Weekend That Never Really Ends, Because the Week Barely Started

Costa Rica's relationship with leisure operates on a philosophy the country has successfully exported as a lifestyle brand: pura vida, "pure life," which sounds soothing until you're the fifth person in a queue at a government office where the clerk has, unofficially, already left for lunch at 11:45am. The Costa Rican weekend isn't a hard stop like Shabbat — it's a long, warm exhale that frequently starts on Friday afternoon and doesn't fully resolve until Monday's "manana" excuses kick in.

Sundays are the real event. Extended families pile into pickup trucks headed for the coast, and by mid-morning half of San José appears to have evaporated. What's left behind moves at the pace of a country that has decided, collectively and without a vote, that rushing is a foreign disease it would rather not catch. Restaurants that promise to open at noon open at 12:20 and nobody bats an eye. Church remains the quiet backbone of small-town Sunday life — bells, plazas filling after mass, ice cream carts working the crowd — while the beach towns run an entirely different weekend economy built on surfers, sunburn, and imperial beers appearing before most countries would consider it decent.

Where Costa Rica trips up newcomers isn't the slowness itself, which is advertised heavily and honestly, it's the inconsistency. Unlike Israel's near-total, predictable Shabbat shutdown, Costa Rica's pace varies wildly by town, by owner's mood, by whether it's raining. You cannot set your watch by pura vida, because pura vida refuses to be set. Expats who arrive expecting a permissive tropical free-for-all instead find a culture with firm, if unstated, boundaries: work stays at work, family Sundays are non-negotiable, and anyone treating leisure time as an inconvenience to be minimised is quietly regarded as a bit sad.

## The Verdict

Israel wins on architecture — a weekend so structurally enforced it forces you into community whether you like it or not, and there's something almost moving about an entire nation collectively refusing commerce for twenty-five hours. But Costa Rica wins on livability, because pura vida isn't a rule you break at your peril, it's a permission slip you're allowed to use badly and often. Shabbat will teach you discipline. Pura vida will teach you that the discipline was probably the problem all along. If you want a weekend that changes you, go to Tel Aviv. If you want a weekend that simply, finally, lets you exist, go to Playa Sámara and stop checking your phone. I know which one I'd choose on a bad week, and it isn't the one with the light-rail schedule.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *Reddit r/israel — a self-described "secular but starving" newcomer paraphrased their first Shabbat as forgetting to buy food on Thursday and surviving on stale pita until Saturday sundown, having assumed at least one supermarket "somewhere" would stay open.*

> *Reddit r/costarica — an expat vented that they'd waited three weeks for a document at a government office, only to be told the clerk handling their case had "gone to the beach for pura vida" and would be back Monday, maybe.*

> *Internations Tel Aviv — a forum thread warned newcomers that scheduling a Saturday flight out of Israel without checking transport in advance is a classic rookie error that ends in an expensive, argument-laden taxi hunt.*

## Conclusion

Neither country is being difficult on purpose, though both will let you believe otherwise if you're rude about it. Israel has built a weekend around obligation to tradition and each other; Costa Rica has built one around obligation to rest and the coastline. Both will humble a visitor who assumes their home country's clock applies here. My advice, offered with the weary authority of someone who has missed a Friday grocery run in Tel Aviv and a Sunday bus in Guanacaste: stop fighting the shutdown. Buy the extra hummus. Catch the extra wave. The weekend was never going to bend to you, and honestly, good — that's rather the point.
