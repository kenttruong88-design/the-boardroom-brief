# One Country Has Rules You Can Read, the Other Has Rules You Only Learn by Almost Breaking Them

🇸🇦 Saudi Arabia vs 🇲🇳 Mongolia

*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Public behaviour norms fall into two broad categories: the codified and the customary. Saudi Arabia, especially post-reform, has a public conduct code that's increasingly written down, explained, and enforced with reasonable clarity — dress modestly, respect prayer times, understand what's expected in mixed and public spaces. Mongolia has almost none of this written down anywhere, because it doesn't need to be — its public etiquette lives in centuries of nomadic custom, passed down orally, and you will absolutely, at some point, step over a threshold the wrong way or point your feet at the wrong object inside a ger before anyone gently, wordlessly corrects you.

I have read Saudi Arabia's public conduct guidelines cover to cover before landing and still gotten small things wrong. I have read nothing at all about Mongolian ger etiquette and gotten far more things wrong, far more memorably, entirely by accident.

## Do's & Don'ts: Saudi Arabia

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Dress modestly in public regardless of gender — loose, covering clothing is expected | Show public affection between couples, even mild — it's still broadly frowned upon |
| Pause respectfully during prayer calls even if you're not participating | Photograph people, especially women, without clearly asking first |
| Greet with your right hand only; the left is considered unclean in traditional contexts | Assume all reform-era relaxations apply equally everywhere — norms vary significantly by city and setting |
| Accept hospitality graciously — refusing coffee or food outright can read as impolite | Raise your voice or show public anger; composure is highly valued in public interactions |

## Do's & Don'ts: Mongolia

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Enter a ger clockwise and take a seat only where directed | Step on the threshold when entering a ger — it's considered genuinely bad luck |
| Accept food or drink offered with your right hand, or both hands together | Point your feet toward the hearth, an altar, or another person while seated |
| Admire livestock or a host's herd if invited to — it's a genuine point of pride | Whistle indoors — traditionally believed to invite bad fortune |
| Receive a snuff bottle offering with both hands and a slight bow if offered one | Turn down hospitality outright in a nomadic household — it's a significant social misstep |

## Saudi Arabia: The Rules Are Written, But the Nuance Isn't

Saudi Arabia has undergone genuinely rapid social change over the past several years — cinemas, mixed public events, and a substantially loosened dress code for foreign visitors have all arrived faster than most outsiders' mental image of the country has caught up with. Official guidance is now reasonably accessible: dress modestly, expect a brief pause in commerce during prayer times, understand that public displays of affection remain broadly discouraged regardless of how relaxed a particular mall or restaurant might otherwise feel.

What the official guidance doesn't fully prepare you for is how much the actual on-the-ground norm varies by city, by neighbourhood, by which specific venue you've wandered into. Riyadh and Jeddah can feel meaningfully different from each other; an upscale international hotel operates by different unspoken rules than a traditional souq two streets away. The written code gives you a floor, not a precise map, and the real skill is watching, calibrating, and erring conservative until you've actually got a feel for a specific place.

What's remained constant throughout the reforms is the premium placed on composure and hospitality in public interaction. Raised voices, visible anger, or aggressive confrontation in public are all read as a genuine loss of face, for you and for anyone you're with. Hospitality, meanwhile, runs deep and sincere — refusing an offered coffee or date outright, rather than accepting even a token amount, can read as a real, if unintentional, rejection of someone's generosity.

## Mongolia: The Rules Live in the Body, Not the Book

Mongolian public and household etiquette draws almost entirely from centuries of nomadic herding custom, and none of it is formally codified for visitors in the way Saudi Arabia's now is — you learn it by watching, by asking quietly, or, most commonly, by getting something small wrong and being gently, wordlessly corrected in real time.

The ger — the traditional round felt tent still central to rural and even much urban Mongolian life — comes with an entire unwritten code. Enter clockwise. Never step directly on the threshold; it's considered to genuinely invite misfortune into the household. Once seated, keep your feet pointed away from the hearth at the centre, which holds real symbolic and practical significance as the heart of the home. Food and drink offered to you should be received with your right hand, or both hands together, and refusing hospitality outright — rather than at least token-accepting it — represents a real breach of the deep, sincere hospitality nomadic Mongolian culture holds as one of its central values.

Beyond the ger, subtler customs persist across public life generally: whistling indoors is traditionally believed to invite bad luck; livestock, where a host has it, is a genuine source of pride worth admiring and asking about rather than overlooking. None of this is written on a government website. All of it is transmitted, patiently, by Mongolians who've clearly decided that correcting a well-meaning foreigner gently, rather than judging them harshly, is simply the more hospitable response.

## The Verdict

Saudi Arabia wins for anyone who wants a genuine, if imperfect, reference point before arriving — you can read the code, calibrate before you land, and get most of the big things right immediately. Mongolia wins for anyone who values how deeply, sincerely human the correction process feels once you inevitably get something wrong — nobody's ever made me feel foolish for stepping on a threshold; they've simply, warmly, shown me the right way.

I'd rather prepare for Saudi Arabia and be corrected in Mongolia. Both, done properly, leave you with a far deeper respect for how much thought sits behind behaviour most of us never think to examine at home.

## Conclusion

Saudi Arabia and Mongolia represent two entirely different ways a culture transmits its expectations to outsiders — one increasingly written and codified, the other still living almost entirely in observed custom and gentle correction. Neither approach is more "advanced" than the other, whatever a certain type of traveller likes to assume about codification equalling modernity. Read what Saudi Arabia has published, and read it as a starting point, not a finish line. Watch, ask, and accept correction gracefully in Mongolia, because that's simply how the knowledge has always moved there. In both places, humility gets you further than confidence ever will.
