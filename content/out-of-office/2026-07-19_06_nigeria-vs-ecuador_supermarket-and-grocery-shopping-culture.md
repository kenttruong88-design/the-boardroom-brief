# Two Countries Where the Real Grocery Shopping Happens Nowhere Near a Supermarket

🇳🇬 Nigeria 🇪🇨 Ecuador
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

I spent my first week in Lagos dutifully doing my grocery shopping at a gleaming, air-conditioned supermarket, paying fixed prices for produce that had clearly travelled further and tasted worse than what I later found, three days on, at a bustling local market ten minutes away, for roughly a third of the cost. In Quito, I made the identical mistake, wandering into a modern supermercado for vegetables that a fifteen-minute walk to the mercado would have delivered fresher, cheaper, and with considerably more theatre. Both Nigeria and Ecuador have fully functional, modern supermarket chains. Both populations treat them as a backup plan. The real shopping, the serious shopping, happens at the market, where prices are negotiable and reputations are everything.

If you've arrived expecting a supermarket-first culture because that's what "modern" apparently means where you're from, both of these countries would like a word, ideally over a haggled bag of plantains or bananas.

## Do's & Don'ts

**Nigeria**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Learn to haggle at open markets — quoted prices are opening offers, not final ones | Assume supermarket prices reflect fair local value — they're priced for convenience, not economy |
| Build a relationship with a specific market vendor for better prices over time | Show up to a busy market expecting fixed, posted prices like a grocery store |
| Bring cash in smaller denominations — market transactions rarely run on card | Be in a rush; market shopping is social and unfolds at its own pace |

**Ecuador**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Visit the mercado early for the best selection and freshest produce | Expect supermarket produce to match the quality of mercado fruit and vegetables |
| Learn a few basic bargaining phrases — gentle negotiation is normal and expected | Insult a vendor by aggressively lowballing; negotiation here is polite, not combative |
| Bring your own bag — many mercados don't automatically provide one | Assume every mercado vendor takes card; cash is still king in most stalls |

## Nigeria: The Market Is the Real Economy

Nigerian grocery culture runs on a dual-track system that newcomers rarely understand until they've overpaid at least once. Supermarkets — Shoprite-style chains and their local equivalents — exist, are modern, air-conditioned, and entirely legitimate, but they function primarily as a convenience option for imported goods, packaged staples, or late-night top-ups, not as the primary venue for everyday fresh food shopping. The real economic and social engine of grocery shopping is the open market, where prices are a starting position rather than a final answer, and where a shopper who doesn't negotiate is, functionally, volunteering to overpay.

What distinguishes Nigerian market culture is the relational aspect layered onto the transactional one. Regular customers who return to the same vendor consistently, greet them by name, and engage genuinely rather than purely transactionally, are rewarded over time with better prices, first pick of fresh stock, and occasionally goods set aside before they even hit the general display. This isn't a hack expats discover; it's simply how the system is designed to function, an economy built on relationship as much as price.

Haggling itself carries its own etiquette — it's expected, even enjoyed, as a mild social performance rather than an adversarial standoff, and vendors generally respect a buyer who negotiates with good humour far more than one who either refuses to engage or negotiates aggressively. Cash remains dominant in market settings, and the pace of a market visit is unhurried by design; treating it like a quick supermarket dash rather than a social outing is the fastest way to be marked as an outsider who doesn't yet understand the rules.

## Ecuador: The Mercado Sets the Standard, the Supermercado Just Follows

Ecuadorian grocery culture follows a broadly similar hierarchy, with the mercado occupying the cultural and practical centre of daily food shopping while supermercados serve a supporting, convenience-oriented role. Markets like Quito's Mercado Central or Guayaquil's larger municipal markets offer produce, meat, and prepared foods at a freshness and price point that modern supermarket chains, however well-stocked, simply cannot compete with — locals know this, and shop accordingly, reserving supermarket trips for packaged goods, imported items, or late-hour convenience.

Bargaining in Ecuador operates with a gentler register than in some other Latin American or African market cultures — negotiation is expected and normal, but it's conducted with a notable warmth and courtesy, more a polite back-and-forth than a hard-edged transactional battle. Aggressive lowballing is considered poor form; a soft, friendly negotiation that acknowledges the vendor's effort tends to yield better results and better relationships than a blunt discount demand.

Timing matters considerably: arriving early secures the best selection and the freshest produce before both thin out through the morning, and regulars who build rapport with specific stalls over repeated visits are often quietly rewarded with better cuts, riper fruit, or a small extra thrown in at no charge — a system of loyalty that functions on memory and goodwill rather than any formal rewards programme. Cash remains the default currency of the mercado, and showing up without your own shopping bag is a small, telling giveaway that you haven't quite learned the local rhythm yet.

## The Verdict

Nigeria wins by a narrow margin, purely on the intensity and centrality of its market culture — the haggling there is more of a genuine skill, the relational payoff for loyal customers more pronounced, and the market's role as the actual economic backbone of daily life more total than in Ecuador, where the supermercado has crept slightly further into everyday relevance. Both countries agree on the fundamental point, though: the supermarket is where you go when the market is closed, not the other way around. Anyone still doing their primary grocery shop under fluorescent supermarket lighting in either country has, quite simply, not been paying attention to where the good stuff actually is.

## Conclusion

Nigeria and Ecuador both quietly agree that the supermarket is a convenience, not a culture — the real shopping, the real prices, and the real social fabric of daily food life live in the market, negotiated, relational, and considerably more alive than anything under fluorescent lighting. Learn to haggle, learn a vendor's name, and show up without being in a rush. Both countries will reward the effort with better produce, better prices, and the quiet satisfaction of no longer being the person who overpaid for tomatoes.
