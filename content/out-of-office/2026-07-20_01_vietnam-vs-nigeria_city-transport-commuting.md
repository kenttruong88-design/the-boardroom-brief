# Nobody Yields, Everybody Arrives, and That's the Whole System

🇻🇳 Vietnam vs 🇳🇬 Nigeria: City Transport & Commuting Culture
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

I have crossed roads on four continents with the calm detachment of someone who has made peace with mortality, and nothing — nothing — prepared me for my first junction in Hanoi or my first go-slow in Lagos. Both cities move roughly nine million people a day using systems that, on paper, do not exist, and in practice, work better than most Western capitals with their four-year infrastructure consultations and their strongly worded traffic apps.

Tourists call this "organised chaos," which is the laziest phrase in travel writing and I refuse to use it sincerely. There is nothing chaotic about either system. There is a logic, it's just not written down anywhere, and it will not be explained to you, because explaining it would take longer than simply surviving it. You learn by nearly dying a few times. That's the curriculum.

## Do's & Don'ts

### 🇻🇳 Vietnam

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Cross the road at a slow, steady pace and let the motorbikes flow around you | Don't stop suddenly mid-crossing — that's the one move that actually causes accidents |
| Book a Grab bike for anything beyond a ten-minute walk, it's absurdly cheap | Don't hail a random xe ôm without agreeing a price first, tourist tax is real |
| Wear a helmet even for a two-minute ride, police checkpoints do exist | Don't expect the bus schedule apps to match reality during rush hour |
| Learn to read a horn beep as "I'm here," not "get out of my way" | Don't rent a motorbike on day one — give it a week of watching first |

### 🇳🇬 Nigeria

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Use Bolt or Uber for anything at night, danfo buses stop being predictable after dark | Don't get into a danfo without small change, conductors will not forgive you |
| Budget serious time for "go-slow" — Lagos traffic eats hours, not minutes | Don't assume a green light means the intersection is yours, it rarely does |
| Try the BRT (Bus Rapid Transit) on the Mile 12 to CMS route, it's genuinely efficient | Don't cross the Third Mainland Bridge on foot, obviously, but people still ask |
| Keep a keke napep (tricycle) driver's number if you find a good one, loyalty pays | Don't argue fare after the ride's started, settle it before the door shuts |

## Vietnam: The Motorbike Is the Message

Hanoi has around eight million motorbikes for roughly eight million residents, give or take a rounding error, and the entire city functions as one continuous, self-organising swarm that somehow never quite collides. Watching it from a café balcony is one of the great free spectator sports of Southeast Asia — thousands of bikes weaving through intersections with no visible signals, no obvious right of way, and an accident rate that, per capita, is nowhere near what the visual chaos implies.

The trick, which nobody explains until you've already had a minor panic attack at a roundabout, is that Vietnamese traffic operates on constant negotiation rather than fixed rules. Everyone is watching everyone else in real time, adjusting speed by increments, and the entire system relies on predictability of behaviour rather than predictability of rules. Foreigners who try to apply Western logic — stopping abruptly, hesitating, waiting for a gap that will never come — are the ones who get hit, ironically, by being too cautious.

Grab, the regional ride-hailing app, has quietly revolutionised expat life here. A motorbike taxi across central Hanoi costs less than a coffee, arrives in minutes, and comes with a helmet you're legally required to wear and that drivers will absolutely make you wear. Buses exist, technically, and cover an impressive route network, but the timetables are more of a philosophical position than a commitment, and non-Vietnamese speakers will find the announcement system offers zero mercy.

What genuinely surprises newcomers is how quiet the whole thing feels once you're inside it rather than watching from outside. The horns aren't aggression, they're communication — a constant, low-level chatter of "I'm behind you," "I'm turning," "I exist, please account for me." Once you stop hearing it as noise and start hearing it as language, Hanoi traffic goes from terrifying to almost meditative. Almost.

## Nigeria: Go-Slow Is a Way of Life

Lagos traffic has its own vocabulary, and the first word every newcomer learns is "go-slow," the local term for a jam so total it becomes a temporary economy — hawkers selling phone chargers, plantain chips, and bottled water window to window while nobody moves for forty minutes. Some Lagosians build entire social lives around go-slow, catching up with friends stuck in adjacent cars via rolled-down windows. There's a strange communal warmth to it that no European traffic jam has ever managed.

The danfo bus, that battered yellow minibus with a conductor hanging half out the door shouting the route, is the backbone of Lagos transit and an experience every expat should have exactly once, ideally not during rush hour, ideally with small denomination naira already in hand. It's chaotic in the way Vietnamese traffic is chaotic — which is to say, not actually chaotic, just running on rules nobody wrote down. The conductor's shorthand shouts, the driver's willingness to invent a lane where none exists, the passengers' collective tolerance for discomfort — it's a system, and it works, roughly eighty percent of the time.

The BRT, Lagos's dedicated bus rapid transit corridor, is the unsung success story locals rarely credit and international coverage rarely mentions. It moves hundreds of thousands of people daily on largely segregated lanes, cutting a two-hour danfo slog down to a genuinely reasonable thirty minutes on the right route. It is, without irony, one of the better-run pieces of transit infrastructure I've used anywhere, and it gets a fraction of the international attention Lagos's traffic chaos generates, because misery travels further than competence.

Then there's the Third Mainland Bridge, the artery connecting the mainland to Lagos Island, which turns into a linear parking lot at peak hours and has spawned an entire micro-economy of boat taxis for people who've simply given up on the road entirely. When your city's commuters start commuting by water to avoid the road network, that tells you something the transport ministry's annual report will not.

## The Verdict

Vietnam wins on sheer elegance — the motorbike swarm is genuinely one of the most efficient informal transit systems on the planet, moving enormous volumes of people at low cost with minimal infrastructure. Nigeria wins on resilience — Lagos commuters have built an entire social and economic ecosystem around a transport network that would have broken lesser cities a decade ago. Neither city has what most Western planners would call "good" infrastructure, and both move more people, more reliably, than several capitals with four times the transit budget.

If I had to choose which commute to do daily for a year, it's Hanoi, purely because the motorbike hums along at a pace that's stressful but rhythmic. Lagos go-slow, for all its social charm, is stressful in a way that simply steals hours from your life you will never get back.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *r/vietnam — paraphrased: "Spent my first week refusing to cross any road without a local. By month two I was the one guiding tourists across. It's a skill, not a leap of faith."*

> *Internations Lagos — paraphrased: "Bought a car thinking it would save me time. It saved me nothing. My driver knows twelve alternate routes I will never understand and I've stopped trying."*

> *Quora — "How do people survive crossing the road in Hanoi?" — paraphrased: "You don't survive it by being careful. You survive it by being predictable. Slow, steady, no sudden moves. The bikes do the rest."*

## Conclusion

Both cities will teach you the same lesson from opposite directions: the system nobody designed usually outperforms the system somebody over-designed. Hanoi's motorbike swarm and Lagos's go-slow economy are proof that commuting chaos, given a decade or two to evolve without top-down interference, organises itself into something that works. Uncomfortably, imperfectly, but it works. Visitors who arrive expecting Western order leave frustrated within a week. Visitors who arrive willing to read the unwritten rules leave, several months later, mildly evangelical about a commute that should, by every objective measure, be a nightmare. It isn't. It's just fluent in a language you haven't learned yet.
