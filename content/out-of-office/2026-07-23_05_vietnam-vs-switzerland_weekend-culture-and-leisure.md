# Vietnam's Weekend Never Stops Moving. Switzerland's Legally Requires You to Be Quiet.

🇻🇳 Vietnam 🇨🇭 Switzerland
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

There is a Swiss law — an actual, enforceable municipal ordinance in many cantons — against mowing your lawn on a Sunday. Not a norm. A law. Meanwhile in Vietnam, a Saturday morning at 6am sounds like the entire country simultaneously decided to fry something, ride a motorbike somewhere, and shout a greeting to a neighbour, all at once, and this is considered completely normal weekend behaviour, not an emergency.

These are two of the most extreme weekend philosophies I've lived inside. One treats Sunday as sacred, silent, and slightly enforced. The other treats the entire weekend as an extension of Tuesday, just with better coffee and more motorbikes. I know which one let me sleep in. I know which one I actually enjoyed more.

## Do's & Don'ts: Vietnam

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Get up early — the best weekend street food and markets happen before 8am | Expect anywhere to be closed; weekends are often busier than weekdays for small business |
| Join a weekend motorbike trip out of the city — it's the default leisure activity | Assume Sunday is a rest day in the Western sense — it rarely is |
| Embrace café culture as the actual weekend social hub, especially in Hanoi | Be surprised by construction noise or market activity starting at dawn |

## Do's & Don'ts: Switzerland

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Plan your grocery shopping for Saturday — almost everything shuts on Sunday | Do laundry, mow the lawn, or make noise on Sunday — some of this is literally regulated |
| Join a hiking or "Wanderweg" club — it's the default national weekend activity | Expect spontaneity; Swiss weekends are often planned days or weeks ahead |
| Respect quiet hours strictly, even mid-afternoon in some buildings | Assume restaurants stay open late; Sunday evening options shrink fast outside cities |

## Vietnam: The Weekend That Refuses to Stop

Vietnam does not really believe in the weekend as a distinct category of time, and this isn't a complaint — it's arguably the secret to how alive the country feels at every hour of every day. Saturday morning markets open before dawn and are in full, shouting swing by 6am, selling produce, fried dough, and impossibly good bowls of phở to people who are clearly not treating this as a lie-in day. Small businesses, street vendors, and family-run restaurants often work harder on weekends than weekdays, because that's when the customers actually show up.

The default leisure activity, especially among younger urban Vietnamese, is motion: weekend motorbike trips out of Hanoi or Ho Chi Minh City to nearby mountains, lakes, or coastline, often organised with the casual efficiency of people who've done this every month since they were teenagers. Café culture fills the gaps — Hanoi's weekend coffee shops are packed by 9am with people working, chatting, or doing nothing in particular for hours over a single cà phê sữa đá, in a way that reads as leisurely despite the surrounding city moving at full speed.

What Vietnam doesn't offer is quiet. Construction doesn't pause for the weekend. Karaoke doesn't observe rest days. The soundscape of a Vietnamese Saturday is, structurally, the same as a Tuesday, just with more people in the streets and slightly better food available. If you need enforced silence to recover from your week, Vietnam will not provide it, and will be somewhat baffled that you'd want it.

## Switzerland: The Weekend as Regulated Silence

Switzerland has, with typical precision, essentially legislated tranquility into its weekends. Sunday closing laws are near-total — supermarkets, most shops, and a lot of services simply shut, forcing an entire nation to do its errands on Saturday with an efficiency that borders on military. In several cantons, noise ordinances specifically restrict lawn mowing, drilling, and other disruptive activities on Sundays, and neighbours will absolutely report you, politely, if you test this.

What fills the resulting silence is, overwhelmingly, the outdoors. Switzerland's hiking culture is not a niche hobby but close to a national identity — the Wanderweg trail network is meticulously signposted, timed to the minute, and used by everyone from toddlers to octogenarians every single weekend, rain or shine. Swiss weekends are frequently planned days in advance: a specific trail, a specific train connection, a specific hut booked for lunch. Spontaneity is rare; competence is assumed.

This produces genuine, deep restoration — a Swiss Sunday really does function as a pause, not a continuation of the week at lower volume. The cost is a certain rigidity: if you didn't plan ahead, or if you need something at 6pm on a Sunday that isn't a hiking trail or a quiet walk, you may simply be out of luck until Monday.

## The Verdict

If your idea of a weekend is total sensory immersion — food, noise, movement, more food — Vietnam delivers an experience no amount of Swiss precision could replicate. If your idea of a weekend is actual rest, enforced by both law and social consensus, Switzerland is unmatched, assuming you remembered to buy milk on Saturday. I've needed Vietnam's chaos to feel alive and Switzerland's silence to recover from feeling that alive. Neither weekend is wrong. One of them, however, will let you buy bread on a Sunday, and that one is not Switzerland.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *r/vietnam — moved here expecting weekends to feel different from weekdays. They don't, really. The market outside my window starts at 5:30am on Saturdays same as any other day. Adjusted eventually.*

> *Internations Zurich — forgot to shop on Saturday my first week. Spent Sunday eating crackers because literally nothing was open. Now it's the first thing I plan every week.*

> *Quora — asked why everything in Switzerland shuts on Sunday. Someone answered, completely deadpan, "because Sunday is for resting, and shops resting means you should be too." Genuinely changed how I think about time off.*

## Conclusion

Vietnam and Switzerland have built two opposite, equally sincere answers to what a weekend is for. Vietnam says: keep living, just with better food and more motorbikes. Switzerland says: stop entirely, and let the law enforce it if your willpower doesn't. Both work. Both, in their own way, produce happy, functional people. I just know which country let me buy emergency wine on a Sunday evening, and it wasn't the one with the mountains.
