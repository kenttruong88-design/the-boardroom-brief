# One Country's Green Space Is a Waterfront Promenade, the Other's Is a National Park the Size of a Small Country

🇺🇾 Uruguay 🇧🇼 Botswana
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

In Montevideo, I spent an entire golden-hour evening watching what felt like the whole city migrate to the rambla, the waterfront promenade, each person clutching a mate gourd and thermos like it was a mandatory civic accessory, settling onto grass, benches, and sea walls for what amounted to a citywide communal exhale. In Botswana, "green space" meant something else entirely — the Okavango Delta, the Central Kalahari, wilderness on a scale so vast it rendered the very concept of a "city park" almost quaint by comparison. Uruguay's green space culture is intimate, daily, and urban. Botswana's is epic, protected, and largely uninhabited by design. Both countries treat their outdoor space as sacred. They simply disagree wildly on scale.

If your mental picture of "parks and green space" is a modest patch of grass with a bench, both of these countries are about to recalibrate that picture rather dramatically, in opposite directions.

## Do's & Don'ts

**Uruguay**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Bring your own mate gourd and thermos to the rambla — it's the local ritual, not a tourist prop | Rush your visit to a park or the rambla; lingering for hours is the entire point |
| Join the evening crowds along Montevideo's waterfront for a genuine slice of local life | Assume city parks are quiet — weekends bring families, asado smoke, and football games |
| Respect shared mate etiquette if offered a sip — it's a genuine gesture of inclusion | Treat the rambla as just a jogging path; it's primarily a social space first |

**Botswana**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Book registered safari operators or park permits well ahead for major reserves | Attempt self-driving into remote parks like the Central Kalahari without serious preparation |
| Respect strict wildlife distance rules — this is genuinely dangerous, protected wilderness | Expect urban-style green spaces in cities; Botswana's green space is wilderness, not parks |
| Support community-based tourism initiatives around reserves — conservation funding depends on it | Underestimate distances between reserves — Botswana's parks are vast and driving times long |

## Uruguay: The Rambla as a National Living Room

Uruguayan green space culture centres overwhelmingly on the rambla, the roughly 22-kilometre waterfront promenade that wraps around Montevideo, functioning less as infrastructure and more as the country's genuine communal living room. Every evening, and especially on weekends, the rambla fills with people walking, cycling, doing impromptu exercise, and — almost universally — carrying a mate gourd and thermos, sipping the bitter herbal tea in a ritual so central to Uruguayan identity that seeing someone without one feels faintly wrong.

What distinguishes Uruguay's approach to green space is how thoroughly social rather than purely recreational it is. Parks and the rambla alike double as venues for asado gatherings, impromptu football matches, and long, unhurried conversations that can stretch for hours — Uruguayans don't really "use" a park the way a jogger might use one elsewhere, ticking off a lap and leaving; they inhabit it, often for an entire afternoon, treating outdoor public space as an extension of the home rather than a separate destination.

Montevideo's other parks — Parque Rodó, Parque Batlle — follow the same unhurried, deeply social logic, hosting family weekend gatherings, casual sports, and a genuinely unpretentious mixing of social classes that some other Latin American capitals' more segregated green spaces don't quite replicate. The scale is modest compared to Botswana's wilderness, but the cultural weight placed on these spaces, as sites of daily communal ritual rather than occasional escape, is arguably just as significant relative to the country's compact size.

## Botswana: Green Space as Wilderness, Full Stop

Botswana's relationship with green space operates on an entirely different scale and philosophy, built around some of the most significant conservation land on the African continent. The Okavango Delta, a UNESCO World Heritage site, and the Central Kalahari Game Reserve, one of the largest protected areas in Africa, mean that when Botswana talks about green or open space, it isn't referring to a manicured city park — it's referring to genuine, largely untouched wilderness, home to elephants, lions, and some of the last great unfenced migration routes left on Earth.

This conservation-first approach is deliberate national policy, not an accident of low urbanisation — Botswana has built its tourism economy around a "low volume, high value" model, prioritising exclusivity and ecological preservation over mass tourist access, which means visiting these spaces requires genuine planning: registered operators, permits, and often significant advance booking for the more sought-after camps and reserves, rather than a spontaneous weekend drive.

What surprises visitors from more urban-park-centric cultures is how little of this translates into everyday accessible green space within Botswana's cities themselves — Gaborone doesn't offer a rambla-style urban promenade culture; the green space that defines the country exists at a remove, often hours of driving away, requiring genuine logistical commitment rather than a stroll after work. The relationship locals have with this wilderness is one of national pride and cautious respect rather than casual daily familiarity — Batswana are proud of their wildlife heritage, but the day-to-day green space experience for most urban residents looks considerably more modest than the world-famous reserves suggest.

## The Verdict

Uruguay wins for livability and daily integration — a green space culture that's woven into ordinary life, every evening, mate in hand, costs nothing and requires no permit. Botswana's green space is genuinely magnificent, arguably among the most important wilderness left on the planet, but it exists at a remove from daily urban life that Uruguay's rambla simply doesn't. One country gave its people a park to live in every single day. The other gave the planet a wilderness worth protecting, at the cost of it being, for most locals, an occasional rather than daily pleasure. Both are right to be proud. Only one of them will let you sip mate while doing it.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *Reddit r/uruguay — paraphrased: a newcomer asked why everyone on the rambla was carrying a thermos and gourd, and the top reply simply said "that's mate, and yes, you need one too, non-negotiable."*

> *Reddit r/Botswana — paraphrased: a visitor described assuming they could self-drive into the Central Kalahari without a permit or experienced guide, and being firmly redirected by park authorities toward a registered operator instead.*

> *Internations Montevideo — paraphrased: an expat noted that weekend asado smoke over Parque Rodó is so constant it's practically part of the park's actual atmosphere.*

## Conclusion

Uruguay and Botswana both treat green space as something worth protecting fiercely, just at opposite ends of the scale — one as an intimate daily ritual along a waterfront promenade, the other as a vast, globally significant wilderness kept deliberately at arm's length from everyday urban life. Bring a mate thermos to Montevideo's rambla, and a registered guide to Botswana's reserves. Both will reward you, though only one of them fits into an ordinary Tuesday evening after work.
