# In One Country Your Hands Do the Talking, in the Other They're Deciding Between Two Entirely Different Languages Mid-Sentence

🇰🇿 Kazakhstan vs 🇮🇹 Italy — Language Barrier Experiences
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Language barriers are usually simple: you don't speak the local tongue, you fumble through, everyone's mildly amused, life continues. Kazakhstan and Italy both refuse to make it that simple, for entirely opposite reasons. Kazakhstan hands you not one but two dominant languages actively competing for airtime in the same conversation, sometimes the same sentence. Italy hands you one language, plus an entire secondary communication system performed with the hands that apparently carries as much semantic weight as the words themselves.

I have stood in Almaty markets genuinely unsure whether the vendor switched to Russian out of politeness or habit, and I have stood in a Roman deli watching a full argument conducted almost entirely through hand shapes, with words functioning as subtitles. Both experiences left me equally lost and equally delighted.

## Do's & Don'ts: Kazakhstan

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Learn a few Kazakh greetings even if Russian is more widely spoken in the cities — it's noticed and appreciated | Assume Russian will work everywhere; rural and southern regions favor Kazakh strongly |
| Expect signage, government forms, and media to shift increasingly toward Kazakh under current language policy | Get confused if a single conversation flips between Kazakh and Russian mid-sentence — that's normal code-switching |
| Use English as a fallback with younger, urban Kazakhstanis — it's rising fast among the under-30 crowd | Assume older generations speak English; Russian is the safer bet with anyone over 50 |

## Do's & Don'ts: Italy

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Watch hands as closely as you listen to words — gestures carry real, specific meaning | Assume all Italians speak English fluently outside major tourist cities; regional variation is huge |
| Learn basic Italian pleasantries — effort is rewarded warmly, even badly pronounced effort | Mix up regional dialects with "real" Italian in front of locals who take pride in both |
| Accept that volume and hand movement increase with sincerity, not anger | Interpret loud, gesture-heavy conversation as conflict — it's usually just enthusiasm |
## Kazakhstan: A Country Mid-Sentence, Mid-Switch

Kazakhstan's language situation is less a barrier and more a moving target, which is honestly more interesting than a simple monolingual wall to bang your head against. Russian remains the dominant language of daily urban life, business, and older-generation conversation, a holdover from the Soviet era that hasn't gone anywhere despite decades of independence. Kazakh, meanwhile, is the constitutionally designated state language and is being actively, deliberately pushed forward — into schools, signage, government paperwork, media — as part of a long national project to reassert it after generations of Russification. The result, for an outsider, is a country conducting an entire identity negotiation out loud, in real time, in every single conversation you have.

What throws visitors most isn't picking a language — it's the code-switching. A single exchange in an Almaty bazaar might start in Kazakh, shift into Russian halfway through a sentence, and finish with a scattering of English loanwords, all without anyone treating this as remarkable. It isn't. It's just how bilingual societies with this kind of layered history actually function day to day, fluidly, without the neat boundaries language learners wish existed. Trying to learn "the" local language here means accepting there isn't one single answer — there are two dominant ones plus a rising third, and which one gets deployed depends on your interlocutor's age, region, and sometimes just mood.

The generational split is stark and worth knowing before you land. Anyone over roughly fifty will lean hard into Russian and likely won't have much English at all. Under thirty, especially in Almaty and Astana, English is climbing fast, driven by international business, universities, and sheer cultural exposure. Rural and southern regions skew far more Kazakh-dominant than the cosmopolitan northern cities. None of this is written down anywhere useful for a tourist — you learn it by getting it wrong a few times, defaulting to Russian with a grandmother who only wanted Kazakh, and recalibrating on the fly. It's a genuinely fascinating linguistic tightrope walk, and infinitely more interesting than the tidy monolingual assumptions most travelers arrive with.

## Italy: Where the Hands Have Their Own Grammar

Italy's language barrier operates on a completely different axis. Yes, English proficiency drops sharply outside Rome, Milan, and Florence's tourist cores — venture into smaller towns in the south or rural Puglia and you'll find yourself entirely reliant on Italian, translation apps, and improvisation. But the far more disorienting layer, the one nobody properly prepares you for, is that gesture in Italy isn't decoration on top of speech. It's a parallel communication channel with its own vocabulary, precise enough that entire meanings shift based on hand shape alone, independent of whatever words are being said.

The fingers-pinched-together "ma che vuoi" gesture means something completely different from an open palm pushed forward, which means something completely different again from fingers tapped against the chin. Italians deploy these constantly, often stacking two or three gestures into a single sentence, and — this is the part that genuinely rattled me — they'll often continue an entire argument or negotiation using gestures alone once the verbal point has technically been made, because apparently some things need reinforcing physically to really land. Watching two Italians disagree about parking is a genuine performance art piece, complete with crescendo, and mistaking the volume and physicality for actual anger is a classic, embarrassing tourist error. It's not anger. It's sincerity turned up to appropriate Italian volume.

Regional dialect adds another wrinkle entirely. Standard Italian, the stuff you learn from an app, is really the Florentine-derived national language — but Neapolitan, Sicilian, Venetian, and Sardinian dialects are close enough to being separate languages that Italians from different regions sometimes genuinely struggle to understand each other's grandparents. Complimenting a Sicilian on their "Italian accent" when they're actually speaking dialect-inflected Italian is a small but real faux pas. The generosity, though, is real and constant: fumble your way through even badly pronounced Italian, and you'll be met with warmth, correction offered kindly, and often a genuine delight that you bothered at all.

## The Verdict

Kazakhstan asks you to navigate a fluid, shifting linguistic landscape where the "correct" language changes based on who you're talking to. Italy asks you to learn an entire second, physical vocabulary running parallel to the spoken one. Both are more textured and rewarding than the flat "everyone speaks English anyway" fantasy most first-time travelers pack alongside their passport. If I have to crown a winner for sheer charm, Italy's gestures win — there's real joy in eventually being able to read a conversation you can't fully hear. But Kazakhstan wins for raw intellectual interest: watching a nation actively renegotiate its own linguistic identity, sentence by sentence, is something you simply cannot get anywhere in Western Europe anymore.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *r/kazakhstan — A visitor greeted an elderly vendor in careful, rehearsed Kazakh and was answered entirely in rapid Russian, leaving them more confused than if they'd said nothing at all.*

> *Internations Milan — An expat described spending six months believing two colleagues were constantly arguing, only to realize the hand gestures and raised voices were simply how they discussed lunch plans.*

> *expat.com Kazakhstan forum — A newcomer noted that switching to English with a young barista in Almaty got a fluent, enthusiastic response, while the same request with an older shopkeeper two doors down got a blank, faintly offended stare.*

## Conclusion

Neither Kazakhstan nor Italy will let you get away with the lazy assumption that "the language" is a single, stable thing you can master and move on from. Kazakhstan is a live negotiation between two major tongues and a rising third. Italy is a spoken language shadowed by an entire physical one, regional dialects layered underneath like geological strata. Show up humble, watch more than you assume, and let both countries teach you that communication was never really just about words in the first place — it just took two very different nations to prove it to me properly.
