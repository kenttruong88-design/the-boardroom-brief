# One Country Dates Like It's Auditioning for a Family, the Other Dates Like the Coast Is About to Close for Winter

🇭🇷 Croatia 🇻🇳 Vietnam
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

A Croatian friend once told me, with total sincerity, that meeting someone's parents happens embarrassingly early because "what's the point otherwise" — a directness that horrified my more conflict-avoidant sensibilities. In Vietnam, I watched a colleague's courtship unfold over eight months of family dinners, careful gift-giving, and what I can only describe as a formal admissions process, before anything resembling an official relationship was declared. Croatia treats dating as a fairly blunt, fast-moving negotiation conducted largely in bars along the Adriatic. Vietnam treats it as a slow-building institution that involves, quite literally, an entire family's due diligence. Neither is wrong. Both will confuse anyone raised on Western swipe-culture dating.

If you've been raised on the assumption that dating means "two people, some drinks, minimal parental involvement," both of these countries are about to reorganise your expectations rather firmly.

## Do's & Don'ts

**Croatia**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Expect direct, sometimes blunt communication about interest — subtlety isn't the local love language | Overanalyse texting gaps — Croatians don't obsess over response times the way some cultures do |
| Be prepared to meet friend groups fairly early — social circles matter enormously | Assume summer coastal flings translate automatically into serious relationships |
| Embrace café culture as the default first-date setting, not dinner | Be surprised by strong opinions on politics or football surfacing on a first date |

**Vietnam**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---| 
| Understand that family approval is often a real, active part of serious dating | Rush physical affection in public — it's still relatively conservative outside big cities |
| Expect group dates or double dates as a common, low-pressure early stage | Assume dating apps work the same way here as they do in the West — norms differ |
| Take gift-giving and small gestures seriously — they carry real relational weight | Bring up marriage-track questions too early — the slow build is deliberate, not evasive |

## Croatia: Blunt, Fast, and Slightly Football-Obsessed

Croatian dating culture runs on a directness that can feel almost jarring to anyone coming from a culture built on ambiguity and plausible deniability. If a Croatian is interested, you will generally know, because they will more or less tell you, and if they are not interested, you will also generally know, for the same reason. The performative game-playing that defines dating in some cultures — deliberately delayed texts, calculated aloofness — is far less central here; people say what they mean with a bluntness that borders on refreshing once you adjust to it.

Social life in Croatia is deeply café-centric, and first dates gravitate naturally toward a leisurely coffee or drink along a promenade rather than a formal dinner — low stakes, low commitment, easy to extend or easy to end. What surprises newcomers is how quickly friend groups get involved; Croatians tend to date within or adjacent to established social circles, meaning meeting someone's friends — and by extension their unfiltered opinions of you — often happens within the first few weeks, not months.

The Adriatic coast adds its own seasonal wrinkle: summer tourist season produces a genuine, well-documented culture of short-lived romances along the coast, flings that both parties understand from the outset are bounded by the tourist calendar. Locals distinguish clearly between this seasonal, low-stakes dating and the more serious, family-oriented courtship that governs long-term relationships — confusing the two is a classic expat mistake, mistaking a August fling for the opening chapter of something permanent.

## Vietnam: The Family Is Basically on the Date

Vietnamese dating culture, particularly outside the more Westernised pockets of Ho Chi Minh City's expat scene, operates on a fundamentally different timeline — one where family involvement isn't a late-stage formality but an active, ongoing consideration from surprisingly early on. Serious dating intentions are often understood, tacitly or explicitly, to be building toward marriage, and family approval carries real weight; a partner's parents forming an unfavourable opinion can genuinely derail a relationship regardless of how the couple themselves feel.

Group dating and double dates function as a common, culturally comfortable early stage, lowering the pressure of one-on-one intensity and allowing courtship to develop gradually within a social buffer. Physical affection in public remains relatively conservative outside the largest, most cosmopolitan areas, and gestures that read as small in Western dating — a thoughtfully chosen gift, a favour done for a partner's family — carry outsized relational significance, functioning almost as a formal expression of intent rather than a casual nicety.

Dating apps have taken hold in urban centres, but expats report the underlying norms diverging from Western app culture considerably — matches often move toward meeting family and defining the relationship's direction far sooner than the casual, exploratory pace common on Western apps, because the entire cultural scaffolding underneath dating here assumes it's heading somewhere specific, not simply somewhere fun.

## The Verdict

Croatia wins for anyone who values speed and honesty over ceremony — you'll know where you stand within weeks, filtered through a friend group and a few blunt conversations over coffee. Vietnam wins for anyone actually looking for something durable, because a system that vets you through an entire family before declaring anything official produces relationships built on considerably more scrutiny than a summer fling on the Adriatic ever will. If you want fun and clarity fast, Split. If you want a courtship that functions like a small, loving tribunal, Hanoi. Choose based on how much you enjoy meeting parents.

## Conclusion

Croatia and Vietnam sit at opposite ends of the dating-speed spectrum, and both will disorient anyone used to the noncommittal ambiguity of modern Western dating apps. Croatia hands you clarity fast, wrapped in bluntness and a friend group's honest opinion. Vietnam hands you a slower, family-vetted institution that assumes you're building something, not just passing time. Neither culture is interested in your ambivalence. Pick a side of the Adriatic or the South China Sea, and commit to the pace, because both of these countries have already decided how seriously they're taking this — the only question is whether you're keeping up.
