# Two Countries Built by Weather That Refuses to Behave, and the People Who Simply Decided Not to Care

🇮🇸 Iceland 🇳🇿 New Zealand
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

I have stood on a beach in Reykjavik in June wearing a wool jumper, a waterproof shell, and a face of quiet betrayal, while a local jogged past in shorts as if the sideways sleet were a light drizzle he'd simply agreed to ignore. Three weeks later I stood on a beach in Wellington and experienced actual sunburn, hail, and a rainbow within the same ninety minutes, and nobody around me so much as glanced up from their flat white. Both nations have made peace with meteorological chaos by the simple trick of no longer discussing it as chaos. This is the story of two populations who looked at their own skies, shrugged, and got on with their lives — one through sheer Nordic stoicism, the other through a sort of cheerful, sunscreen-slathered fatalism.

People who describe either country's weather as "unpredictable" have not been paying attention. It is entirely predictable. It will be four seasons before lunch, and everyone already knew that.

## Do's & Don'ts

**Iceland**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Own a proper windproof shell, not a "water-resistant" fashion jacket | Trust a sunny forecast past the hour it was issued |
| Layer like you're preparing for an expedition, even for a grocery run | Schedule anything outdoor without a backup plan |
| Accept that "summer" means 12°C and count it as a win | Complain about the wind to an Icelander — they've stopped hearing it |

**New Zealand**

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Wear sunscreen even when it's cloudy — the UV index doesn't care | Assume this morning's sunshine says anything about this afternoon |
| Carry a jacket in your bag in December, the height of summer | Leave washing on the line and walk away confidently |
| Learn the phrase "four seasons in one day" — you'll need it hourly | Trust anyone's five-day forecast, including MetService's own |

## Iceland: The Nation That Weaponised Indifference

Iceland's weather is not weather so much as a personality test administered daily by the North Atlantic. The wind alone has its own folklore — there are documented gusts strong enough to rip car doors clean off, and Icelanders discuss this the way other nations discuss mild traffic. The Icelandic Meteorological Office issues colour-coded warnings — yellow, orange, red — and I have watched locals glance at an orange alert, mutter "hmm," and go buy bread anyway. This is not recklessness. It is four hundred years of practice.

What foreigners get wrong immediately is packing for "Europe" rather than packing for Iceland, which is its own weather system entirely, sitting smugly at the meeting point of warm Gulf Stream air and cold Arctic fronts, producing a place where you can experience sun, hail, and wind strong enough to reconsider your life choices within the same walk to the bus stop. The joke that Iceland has no bad weather, only bad clothing, is not a joke. It is policy. Every Icelandic child owns a full waterproof shell suit before they own real opinions.

Then there's the darkness, which is the part nobody warns you about properly. Winter delivers perhaps four or five hours of grey daylight, and the population's response has been to turn indoor cosiness into a national art form — they even have a word for it, and no, it is not the Danish one everyone stole for candle marketing. Icelanders swim outdoors, in geothermal pools, through actual blizzards, because the water is roughly 38°C and the air temperature is simply not their problem anymore. I once saw a woman doing gentle backstroke while snow settled on her hair like she was being personally garnished. Nobody blinked.

The failure mode for expats is trying to impose a "normal" relationship with weather onto a place that discarded that relationship generations ago. You will lose. Buy the coat. Learn to love the pool.

## New Zealand: Four Seasons, One Barista Queue

New Zealand's weather operates on a different but equally merciless principle: total unpredictability delivered with total sunny confidence. The country sits directly in the path of the Roaring Forties, gets weather systems thrown at it from Antarctica and the tropics in the same week, and somehow every single local acts as though this is charmingly quirky rather than a genuine logistical hazard. "Four seasons in one day" is printed on tea towels, and it is not an exaggeration — it is a weather advisory disguised as merchandise.

The sun in New Zealand is a separate, more dangerous problem than most visitors clock, because the ozone layer here is thinner and the UV index regularly reaches "extreme" while the temperature sits at a deceptively mild 22°C. I have watched tourists get lobster-red in under twenty minutes because the air felt cool and the sun felt gentle, and both of those sensations were lies. Locals slather on SPF 50 as a reflex, the way other nationalities check their phones — constant, automatic, barely conscious.

What genuinely separates Kiwis from most weather-battered nations is their refusal to let any of it cancel plans. Rain during a barbecue is not a reason to go inside; it is a reason to move the barbecue slightly, or not. Tramping groups set off into forecasts that would trigger evacuation orders elsewhere, armed with a merino layer and a sense of proportion. The national attitude seems to be that the weather is a slightly eccentric flatmate — occasionally infuriating, permanently uncontrollable, but not worth actually moving out over.

Where New Zealand loses points is infrastructure's quiet refusal to acknowledge any of this. Older houses are famously under-insulated for a country that gets proper cold snaps, meaning locals layer up indoors nearly as much as outdoors, an oddity that baffles nearly every European arrival who assumed "temperate" meant "warm inside."

## The Verdict

Iceland wins on sheer commitment to the bit. When your national coping mechanism for four hours of winter daylight and horizontal sleet is to build geothermal pools and swim through it anyway, that's not resilience, that's an entire culture's raised middle finger to the concept of "acceptable conditions." New Zealand copes by pretending everything is fine while quietly getting sunburned and slightly damp, which is admirable but a touch more passive. Iceland looked at the worst weather in Europe and turned it into hot-tub culture. That's not adaptation. That's alchemy. New Zealand, you get a solid silver for enthusiasm; Iceland takes gold for actually solving the problem while the rest of us were still complaining about it.

## Conclusion

Neither country's weather will ever behave, and neither population is under any illusion that it will. Iceland's answer was to become emotionally and physically waterproof — swimming through blizzards, ignoring wind warnings, treating darkness as decor. New Zealand's answer was to develop a national attitude of cheerful denial, sunscreen in hand, plans unchanged, jacket optional until it very much isn't. If you're moving to either, stop checking the forecast for reassurance. It was never going to give you any. Buy the coat, buy the SPF, and do what the locals do: proceed anyway.
