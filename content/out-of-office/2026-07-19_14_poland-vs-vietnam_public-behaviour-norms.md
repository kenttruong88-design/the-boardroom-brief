# One Country Queues Like It's a Moral Test, the Other Doesn't Believe in Queues at All

🇵🇱 Poland vs 🇻🇳 Vietnam: Public Behaviour Norms
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Poles queue with the grim discipline of people who remember what happens when systems break down, and they will silently, comprehensively judge you for cutting in front of an elderly woman at the pharmacy. Vietnam, by contrast, has never met a queue it didn't immediately dissolve into a loosely organized cluster, and somehow — impossibly — everyone still gets served, gets on the bus, gets their bowl of phở, in an order that makes sense to absolutely everyone except the newly arrived foreigner standing there, dumbfounded, still waiting for a line that was never going to form.

I have stood in both. In Warsaw, I once watched a man get openly tutted at by six strangers for using a self-checkout basket in the "10 items or fewer" queue with eleven items. In Hanoi, I watched forty people converge on a single motorbike parking spot with zero apparent order and total mutual understanding, not one collision, not one raised voice. Both systems work. Neither makes sense to the other's citizens, and I have never seen two countries more committed to being right about it.

## Do's & Don'ts

### 🇵🇱 Poland

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Queue properly and visibly — Poles notice and remember queue violations | Don't be loud in public spaces, especially on public transport, it draws real disapproval |
| Greet shopkeepers with "dzień dobry" on entry, silence reads as rude | Don't smile at strangers without reason — it's read as insincere, not friendly |
| Give up your seat for elderly passengers immediately, it's expected without asking | Don't jaywalk, even on an empty street — Poles will wait for the green light regardless |
| Keep noise down in residential courtyards, especially on Sundays | Don't skip the personal space buffer — standing too close reads as aggressive |

### 🇻🇳 Vietnam

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Cross the road with steady, predictable movement — stopping suddenly causes accidents | Don't expect formal queues at markets, bus stops, or counters — clusters are the norm |
| Use both hands when giving or receiving something from an elder, it's a real courtesy marker | Don't raise your voice in anger publicly — loss of face is taken seriously on both sides |
| Bargain cheerfully at markets, it's expected and even enjoyed as an interaction | Don't point with a single finger — use an open hand, it's considered more polite |
| Remove shoes when entering someone's home, no exceptions | Don't be surprised by public napping — daytime rest in public spaces is completely normal |

## Poland: Order as a Quiet National Value

Polish public behaviour runs on an unspoken but rigorously enforced code, and the queue is its purest expression. Cut a line in Warsaw and you won't be shouted at — Poles don't really do public confrontation — but you will feel the temperature of the room drop by several degrees, and someone will mutter something under their breath that you'll be grateful not to fully understand. This isn't petty rule-following; it's a cultural inheritance from decades of scarcity, when queuing fairly for limited goods was a genuinely serious matter, and the etiquette never fully relaxed even once the shelves filled back up.

Public transport in Poland has its own strict, mostly unwritten rulebook. Offering a seat to an elderly person, a pregnant woman, or someone visibly struggling isn't a nice gesture, it's close to mandatory, and failing to do it gets noticed fast. Conversely, loud phone conversations or boisterous groups on a tram will earn you the specific, withering Polish stare that says everything a raised voice never could. Poles are not cold, despite the reputation — they're reserved in public and warm in private, and mixing up which mode you're in is a classic foreigner mistake.

Jaywalking is where this discipline gets almost comedic. Stand at an empty Warsaw intersection at 2am, not a car in sight for a kilometre in either direction, and the Pole next to you will still wait for the green light, occasionally shooting a disapproving glance if you don't. It's not really about the traffic law. It's about being the kind of person who follows the rule regardless of whether anyone's watching, which says more about Polish civic identity than almost anything else in daily life.

Small courtesies matter enormously here too — greeting a shopkeeper on entering, saying goodbye on leaving, not because anyone will call you out for skipping it, but because skipping it marks you, instantly and permanently, as someone who doesn't quite belong yet.

## Vietnam: Fluid Order Hiding in Plain Sight

Vietnamese public life looks, to the untrained eye, like glorious anarchy — motorbikes weaving in every direction at once, market stalls with customers converging from all sides with no visible queue, buses boarded via what appears to be a friendly stampede. And yet the system works, consistently, because it isn't actually chaos, it's a completely different operating logic built on constant mutual awareness rather than fixed rules.

Crossing a Hanoi street as a newcomer is the single best crash course in this philosophy. The instinct to wait for a gap in traffic is precisely wrong, because there is no gap, ever — the correct move is to walk at a slow, steady, predictable pace and let the motorbikes flow around you like water around a rock. Stop suddenly, hesitate, change your pace, and you become genuinely dangerous, to yourself and everyone else. It's a system that punishes indecision far more than it punishes boldness.

Beneath the apparent lack of structure, Vietnamese public behaviour actually runs on a strong underlying code around respect and face. Raising your voice in a public dispute is deeply frowned upon, since losing composure means losing face for everyone involved, not just the person shouting. Elders are given clear, visible deference — offering and receiving items with two hands, letting them eat first, addressing them with the correct kinship term rather than a generic "you." Bargaining at markets, meanwhile, isn't rudeness or exploitation, it's a genuinely enjoyed social ritual, performed with a smile, and vendors respect a confident haggler far more than a passive one who pays the inflated first price without a word.

Public napping, common in shops, on parked motorbikes, in hammocks strung across storefronts during the hottest hours, baffles visitors who read it as unprofessional. It isn't. It's a practical, culturally sanctioned response to heat and long working days, and nobody blinks at it.

## The Verdict

Poland wins on predictability — you always know exactly what's expected, even if the expectations are exhausting to maintain. Vietnam wins on resilience — a system with no visible rules that somehow produces less actual friction than Poland's rigorously policed one, because everyone's watching everyone else in real time rather than watching a rulebook. If you need structure to feel safe, Poland will comfort you. If you can tolerate ambiguity and trust a crowd's collective intelligence, Vietnam will reward you. I'll admit a soft spot for Hanoi's chaos — there's something honest about a system that doesn't pretend to have rules it can't actually enforce.

## Conclusion

Public behaviour is the clearest window into what a country actually values, and these two couldn't be more different in their answer. Poland values order, restraint, and rules followed even when nobody's checking. Vietnam values fluid mutual awareness over rigid structure, and trusts its people to read a room — or a road — better than any traffic light could. Visitors who arrive expecting the other country's norms to apply will spend their first month baffled and their second month, if they're paying attention, quietly recalibrating. That recalibration is the whole point of leaving home in the first place.
