# Mauritius Built an Entire Industry Around Selling You a Villa. Seychelles Would Rather You Just Visited.

🇲🇺 Mauritius 🇸🇨 Seychelles
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Mauritius spent the last two decades methodically building legal pathways — IRS, RES, PDS, an alphabet soup of foreign-ownership schemes — specifically so people like you could buy a villa, get residency attached to it, and settle into an island economy that has professionalised the business of hosting wealthy foreigners long-term. Seychelles took the opposite approach: land ownership for foreigners remains tightly restricted, long-term accommodation options are genuinely scarce outside a handful of luxury developments, and the entire apparatus of the country seems quietly, politely uninterested in you staying much longer than a fortnight.

I've toured a Mauritian villa development with a sales agent who had residency paperwork ready before I'd finished the property tour. I've also tried, and largely failed, to find a mid-term rental in Seychelles that wasn't either a backpacker guesthouse or a thousand-dollar-a-night resort villa with nothing sensible in between. One island wants your money and your address. The other just wants your money, briefly, and a nice tip.

## Do's & Don'ts: Mauritius

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Research IRS, RES, and PDS schemes properly before committing — they carry different price and residency thresholds | Assume any villa purchase automatically grants residency; scheme details matter enormously |
| Use a licensed local property agent for any long-term rental or purchase | Rely on informal listings or unverified developer promises |
| Consider the north and west coasts for a livelier long-term expat community | Expect every coastal area to offer the same infrastructure and social scene |

## Do's & Don'ts: Seychelles

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Book resort accommodation well in advance — supply is genuinely limited across the islands | Expect flexible last-minute long-term rental options; they largely don't exist |
| Budget realistically — Seychelles is one of the most expensive accommodation markets per square metre anywhere | Assume prices will resemble Mauritius or other Indian Ocean destinations |
| Treat any long-term stay ambition as a serious legal project requiring specialist advice | Assume you can simply buy land or a home the way you might in Mauritius |

## Mauritius: An Economy Built to Host You Indefinitely

Mauritius has done something genuinely unusual among small island nations: it built accommodation and residency into a single, coherent commercial pipeline. The IRS (Integrated Resort Scheme), RES (Real Estate Scheme), and PDS (Property Development Scheme) each offer foreigners a legal route to buy property — villas, apartments, resort-adjacent developments — with residency rights attached above certain investment thresholds. This isn't a workaround or a grey-market hack; it's deliberate national policy, actively marketed, and the entire long-term accommodation sector here has organised itself around servicing exactly this kind of buyer.

The practical result is a genuinely mature market for long-term expat living: gated villa communities with real infrastructure, property management services accustomed to absentee or semi-resident owners, and a rental market with enough depth that you can find something between "backpacker hostel" and "ultra-luxury resort," which is more than can be said for most Indian Ocean destinations. The north coast around Grand Baie has become something close to an expat enclave, complete with international schools and a genuine long-term foreign community rather than a transient tourist one.

What Mauritius sacrifices for this accessibility is a certain exclusivity — this is not an undiscovered paradise, and its accommodation market reflects a country that has fully commercialised its appeal to long-term foreign residents, for better and worse.

## Seychelles: Paradise That Prefers You as a Guest

Seychelles has pursued almost the opposite strategy, and it's a deliberate one: foreign land ownership is heavily restricted, with most property remaining in Seychellois hands, and the government has consistently prioritised high-value, low-volume tourism over long-term foreign settlement. The practical consequence for anyone hoping to relocate rather than visit is a genuinely thin accommodation market outside the resort sector — long-term rentals exist, but they're scarce, often informally arranged, and rarely advertised through channels a newcomer would easily find.

What Seychelles does offer, spectacularly, is short-to-medium-stay luxury: private island resorts, boutique villa properties, and accommodation that justifies its considerable expense through genuine exclusivity and natural beauty that Mauritius, for all its infrastructure, doesn't quite match. This is a market built for visitors who come, spend significantly, and leave — not for people trying to build a permanent life on the islands.

For anyone determined to stay long-term, the path exists but requires real specialist legal navigation, employment-linked permits, or niche investment routes — nothing resembling Mauritius's straightforward, marketed villa-to-residency pipeline. Seychelles rewards visiting lavishly far more than it rewards settling.

## The Verdict

Mauritius wins decisively for anyone actually planning to relocate — the legal pathways, rental depth, and expat infrastructure simply don't have a Seychellois equivalent. Seychelles wins decisively for anyone wanting the more spectacular, more exclusive island experience, provided they accept it as a guest rather than a resident. I'd buy in Mauritius. I'd only ever rent, briefly and extravagantly, in Seychelles. Both are paradise. Only one is interested in a lease.

## Conclusion

Mauritius and Seychelles both sell paradise, but only one is selling you a piece of it to keep. Mauritius has built an entire commercial and legal apparatus around welcoming foreign residents, villas and all. Seychelles has built its appeal around scarcity and exclusivity, and it guards that scarcity fiercely, including from you. Go to Mauritius to stay. Go to Seychelles to be spoiled, briefly, and go home richer in memories and considerably poorer in cash.
