# Fiji Shrugs at Its Own Cyclones. Papua New Guinea Has Four Climates Before Lunch.

🇫🇯 Fiji 🇵🇬 Papua New Guinea
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Fiji gets hit by genuine cyclones most years, sometimes severely, and yet the prevailing local response to almost any weather disruption is a version of "Fiji time" applied to meteorology itself — calm, unhurried, well-practised preparation followed by an equally unhurried recovery, backed by tourism infrastructure that's had decades to get resilience right. Papua New Guinea doesn't get the luxury of a single unified weather story at all: coastal areas run tropical and humid, the highlands run genuinely cold enough for frost, and the climate can shift dramatically within a single day's drive, which means "coping with weather" here isn't one skill but several, learned regionally and often without much infrastructure to soften the learning curve.

I've sat out a cyclone warning in Fiji with locals who treated the whole thing with a calm that bordered on indifference, stocked up, boarded up, and carried on. I've also driven from PNG's humid coast into the genuinely cold Highlands in a single afternoon and needed a jacket I hadn't packed because nobody warned me a "tropical" country could feel like this. One country has one big weather problem it's mastered. The other has several, and mastery looks different in each.

## Do's & Don'ts: Fiji

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Take official cyclone warnings seriously and prepare early, even if locals seem relaxed | Mistake calm local demeanour for genuine unpreparedness — the calm is practised, not careless |
| Stock emergency supplies during cyclone season (November to April) as standard practice | Assume tourist infrastructure guarantees safety during a severe cyclone warning |
| Follow official Fiji Meteorological Service updates over informal social media rumours | Travel between islands casually during an active cyclone warning |

## Do's & Don'ts: Papua New Guinea

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Pack for dramatically different climates if travelling between coast and highlands | Assume "tropical country" means uniformly hot weather everywhere, including the highlands |
| Respect how deeply weather ties to subsistence agriculture and local livelihoods | Treat weather-related scheduling delays as inefficiency rather than a genuine local priority |
| Build flexibility into travel plans between regions — weather-driven road and flight delays are common | Underestimate highland cold, especially overnight; frost is a real possibility at altitude |

## Fiji: Practised Calm in the Face of Real Cyclones

Fiji's relationship with severe weather is genuinely instructive, because it's built on repetition rather than denial: cyclone season runs roughly November through April, and severe cyclones — Winston in 2016 among the most devastating in the country's history — are a real, recurring feature of life here, not a hypothetical. What's distinctive is the local response, which channels the same unhurried "Fiji time" ethos that governs everyday life into disaster preparedness itself: methodical stocking of supplies, community-level coordination, and a calm that comes specifically from having done this before, repeatedly, and survived it.

Tourism infrastructure has adapted accordingly — resorts and hotels have genuinely robust cyclone protocols, built environment standards have improved considerably since Winston, and the Fiji Meteorological Service provides reliable, well-communicated warnings that both locals and visitors are expected to actually heed. The local calm shouldn't be mistaken for casualness about risk; it's closer to earned confidence, the specific kind that comes from a system that's been stress-tested repeatedly and refined each time.

For a new resident or long-term visitor, the lesson is straightforward: match the preparation, not the outward calm. Locals are relaxed because they've already done the serious parts. Skipping those parts and just adopting the relaxed demeanour is how newcomers get caught out.

## Papua New Guinea: One Country, Several Climates, No Universal Coping Strategy

Papua New Guinea defies any single weather narrative almost entirely, and that's the central fact anyone relocating here needs to internalise immediately. The coastal and lowland regions run hot, humid, and tropical in the way outsiders typically expect. The Highlands region, by sharp contrast, sits at genuine altitude, with temperatures cold enough for frost in some areas overnight, a reality that surprises almost everyone arriving with generic "Papua New Guinea is tropical" assumptions. Between these extremes sit numerous microclimates shaped by PNG's extraordinarily varied and mountainous terrain.

This climatic diversity isn't just a curiosity; it fundamentally shapes daily coping strategies across the country, which vary enormously by region rather than following one national pattern. Coastal and lowland communities build around humidity and tropical storm patterns. Highland communities build around cold nights, altitude-related agricultural cycles, and a genuinely different rhythm of daily life. Subsistence agriculture, still central to most of the population's livelihood, ties weather patterns directly and consequentially to food security in a way that makes local weather priorities feel considerably higher-stakes than a simple travel inconvenience.

Infrastructure to soften any of this remains genuinely limited outside major centres — weather-related road and flight disruptions are common and not always predictable, and travellers moving between regions need real flexibility built into their plans rather than tight, weather-dependent scheduling.

## The Verdict

Fiji wins on preparedness and infrastructure — its cyclone response is genuinely mature, tested, and reassuring once you understand the calm is earned rather than careless. Papua New Guinea wins on nothing resembling ease, but offers something more genuinely humbling: a direct, unfiltered lesson in just how much climate can vary within a single small country, with infrastructure that hasn't fully caught up to that variety yet. I'd trust Fiji's system in a real cyclone. I'd pack a jacket for Papua New Guinea and trust absolutely nothing about my own assumptions.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *r/fiji — locals were completely calm during a cyclone warning that had me genuinely anxious. Turns out "calm" here means "already fully prepared," not "not taking it seriously."*

> *Internations Port Moresby — flew from the coast to the highlands for a work trip, didn't pack anything warm, spent the first night genuinely cold in a country I'd been told was tropical. Nobody warns you about the highlands.*

> *Quora — asked how different PNG's highland and coastal climates really are. One answer simply said: "pack like you're going to two different countries, because you are."*

## Conclusion

Fiji and Papua New Guinea both sit in the same general Pacific neighbourhood and cope with weather in almost entirely unrelated ways. Fiji has turned repeated cyclone experience into practised, infrastructure-backed calm. Papua New Guinea has never had one weather story to master, only several, region by region, largely without the infrastructure Fiji has built. Prepare seriously for cyclone season in Fiji, then relax like a local once you have. Pack for four climates in Papua New Guinea, and don't relax at all until you've actually seen the forecast for wherever you're headed next.
