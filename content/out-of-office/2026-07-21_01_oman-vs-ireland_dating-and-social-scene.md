# Everyone Wants to Marry You in Muscat, Nobody Wants Your Number in Dublin

🇴🇲 Oman vs 🇮🇪 Ireland — Dating and Social Scene
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Let me set the scene. In one country, the entire architecture of courtship is designed around families, formal introductions, and the sort of chaperoned patience that would make a Regency novelist weep with joy. In the other, courtship happens in a room with sticky floors, at a decibel level that requires you to read lips, and concludes — if you're lucky — with someone shouting "SAME AGAIN?" into your ear as a substitute for "I like you."

I have dated, or attempted to date, in both. Oman will politely ask about your intentions before you've finished your coffee. Ireland will ask you nothing at all and simply buy the next round, and you're expected to divine everything else from vibes and eye contact across a beer garden. Neither system is wrong. Both are, in their own ways, completely mad. Here's the breakdown, delivered with the fondness of someone who has been thoroughly confused in both places.

## Do's & Don'ts: Oman

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Understand that public dating as Westerners know it largely doesn't exist — relationships move through family and community channels | Hold hands, hug, or show affection in public — it's illegal for unmarried couples and enforced |
| Expect any serious relationship with a local to eventually involve meeting the family, often early | Assume the absence of nightclubs means the absence of romance — it just moves indoors and online |
| Use apps quietly and discreetly if you're an expat dating other expats | Bring up dating culture as a joke with Omani colleagues — it's not a punchline, it's family structure |

## Do's & Don'ts: Ireland

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Accept that the pub is not a backup venue — it is THE venue, for the first date and several after | Expect anyone to define the relationship out loud; ambiguity is a national pastime |
| Learn the difference between "we're seeing each other" and "we're together," because Irish people will not explain it to you | Suggest a coffee date to a Dubliner over 25; you will be gently pitied |
| Bring wit. It is the actual currency of Irish flirtation, more than looks, money, or a car | Take slagging personally — if they're teasing you, it's going well |

## Oman: Courtship by Committee, and I Mean That as a Compliment

Here is the thing nobody tells you before you land in Muscat: the absence of a "dating scene" in the Western sense is not a gap. It's a different, older operating system that happens to still work extremely well, thank you very much. Omani society organizes romantic partnership through family networks, and yes, that includes actual matchmaking conversations between mothers, aunts, and increasingly, meticulously vetted introductions arranged with the seriousness of a corporate merger. It is, if you strip away your own cultural baggage for one second, a remarkably efficient system for finding a life partner whose values and family expectations already align with yours before date one even happens.

For expats, this doesn't mean romance is unavailable — it means it moves to different rooms. Within the expat community, relationships form through work, compounds, and social clubs, largely under the radar, because Oman's laws are unambiguous: public displays of affection between unmarried couples are illegal and can be enforced, cohabitation outside marriage is not legally recognized, and the state takes its role as chaperone of public morality seriously. I've had friends here quietly panic over holding hands in a mall. This isn't paranoia. It's just knowing the rules of the country you've chosen to live in, which — radical idea — is generally advisable everywhere.

What I find genuinely appealing, and I say this as someone allergic to sincerity, is how little performative dating theatre there is. No one's optimizing a Hinge profile with a fish they caught once. Intentions are stated plainly, early, and to the correct authority — which is occasionally your prospective partner's father, and I have made my peace with that. It's blunter than the West's endless "talking stage," and honestly, after a decade of ghosting culture, blunt is refreshing. Omanis are also disarmingly warm once you're inside the circle — invitations to family majlis gatherings, generous hospitality, genuine curiosity about your life. You just have to understand you're being evaluated the entire time, and that's fine. Everyone is always being evaluated. Ireland just hides it behind six pints.

## Ireland: Ambiguity as a Load-Bearing Structure

Now, Ireland. Where the entire dating culture appears to have been designed by someone who found direct communication mortally embarrassing and built a nation around avoiding it. The pub is not where dating happens — the pub is dating. There is no alternative venue. Suggesting a quiet coffee to discuss feelings will get you a look normally reserved for people who put ketchup on a full Irish breakfast.

The genuinely maddening part is the vocabulary. "We're seeing each other" and "we're together" are apparently distinct relationship states with different obligations, and no Irish person will ever explain the difference to you directly — you're meant to absorb it through cultural osmosis, like a fungus. Expats report, and I concur entirely, that Irish dating runs on a kind of comic understatement where the depth of someone's feelings is inversely proportional to how much they'll admit to them out loud. Someone besotted with you will describe you as "alright, I s'pose" to their mates. It's a love language built entirely on plausible deniability.

That said — and I resent how much I mean this — it works, because it strips away the try-hard sincerity of dating apps and replaces it with something closer to genuine chemistry tested through banter. If you can't take a joke, Dublin will eat you alive on a first date, and honestly, that's a better filter than most algorithms. The craic is the actual test of compatibility. Fail it, and no amount of good looks saves you. Irish social life is also blessedly communal — you rarely date one-on-one in isolation; you're absorbed into a friend group almost immediately, vetted by proxy, which is its own quieter version of the family scrutiny happening in Muscat, just with more Guinness and fewer chaperones.

## The Verdict

Both systems are, underneath the surface, doing the exact same job: filtering out people who aren't serious, using whatever social technology the culture has built over centuries. Oman does it through family and formal intention. Ireland does it through wit, understatement, and the ability to survive relentless slagging. Neither is remotely compatible with the swipe-left instant-gratification nonsense most of us have been conditioned into.

If I'm picking a winner for sheer entertainment value, Ireland wins by a mile — there is no funnier place on earth to watch someone try to figure out if they're "seeing" or "with" their situationship. But if I'm picking the system I'd trust to actually produce a marriage that lasts, Oman's committee approach, unfashionable as it sounds to Western ears, has a far better track record. Efficiency over entertainment. Sorry, Dublin.

## Conclusion

Two islands — one desert, one perpetually drizzling — have built entirely different machines for the same universal problem: finding someone to put up with you long-term. Oman does it with the gravity of a state occasion. Ireland does it with the gravity of a pub quiz. Both would be baffled by the other's approach, and both would probably be right to be. My advice, as someone who has fumbled both systems: if you're in Muscat, respect the process and let the family vet you — it's not intrusive, it's thorough. If you're in Dublin, learn to laugh at yourself immediately, because they've already started laughing, and pretending otherwise just means you missed the joke.
