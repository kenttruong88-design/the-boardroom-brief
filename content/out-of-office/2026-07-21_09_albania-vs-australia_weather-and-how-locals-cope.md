# One Country Empties Entire Cities to the Coast Every August, the Other Has Turned Sunscreen Into a Moral Obligation

🇦🇱 Albania vs 🇦🇺 Australia — Weather and How Locals Cope
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Heat does strange, revealing things to a culture. Albania responds to its blistering Mediterranean summers by simply relocating — entire cities empty out toward the Riviera in August as if evacuating from something, except the something is just Tuesday. Australia responds to its brutal sun by building an entire moral framework around sunscreen, shade, and UV index numbers treated with the gravity most countries reserve for actual emergencies.

I've watched Tirana go quiet for weeks as everyone decamps to Sarandë and Vlorë. I've also been sincerely lectured by an Australian stranger for standing in direct sun without a hat, as though I'd committed a minor public health violation. Both responses are, in their own ways, completely rational.

## Do's & Don'ts: Albania

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Expect major cities to genuinely empty out in August as residents head to the coast for weeks | Plan important business or bureaucratic errands in August; half the country is at the beach |
| Embrace long, slow coastal afternoons as the correct response to summer heat | Expect air conditioning everywhere; many older buildings rely on shutters, fans, and thick stone walls instead |
| Adjust your schedule around the midday heat the way locals do, with a long, unapologetic pause | Push through the hottest hours out of habit; even locals structure the day around avoiding them |

## Do's & Don'ts: Australia

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Take "Slip, Slop, Slap" seriously — sunscreen, a shirt, and a hat are near-mandatory in summer | Underestimate UV index warnings; Australian sun causes real, fast skin damage even on mild-looking days |
| Check the daily UV forecast the way other cultures check rain forecasts | Assume a cloudy day means low UV risk — it often doesn't in Australia |
| Respect total fire bans and heatwave warnings during extreme heat events | Light a barbecue or outdoor fire during a declared total fire ban — it's a real legal offense, not a suggestion |

## Albania: The Coast Becomes the Country

Albania's approach to summer heat is refreshingly simple: when the temperature becomes unreasonable, leave. Not for a weekend — for weeks. Come August, Tirana and the other inland cities empty out with a thoroughness that catches visitors off guard, as entire families relocate to the Adriatic and Ionian coasts, to Sarandë, Vlorë, Durrës, and the smaller beach towns that swell from sleepy fishing villages into packed, buzzing seasonal cities practically overnight. Businesses shorten hours or close outright. Government offices operate on skeleton staff. If you need to get anything official done in August, the honest advice is simply: don't try, wait until September, and go to the beach instead like everyone else has correctly decided to do.

What's striking is how uncomplicated this collective decision is. There's no elaborate justification needed, no debate about productivity lost — the heat is genuinely oppressive by midsummer, older buildings often lack air conditioning and rely instead on thick stone walls, shutters, and cross-ventilation that were perfectly adequate a century ago and remain stubbornly in place today, and the coast offers actual relief along with, frankly, a better lifestyle. The whole country essentially agrees that fighting the heat through indoor climate control is a losing and expensive battle, while simply moving to where the sea breeze does the work for free is the obviously smarter move.

Daily rhythm adjusts accordingly, coast or inland. The hours around midday, when the sun is at its most punishing, become a natural pause — shops shutter, streets empty, and life resumes in the late afternoon and stretches long into the cooler evening, when restaurants fill and the real social life of the day actually begins. It's not laziness; it's an entirely sensible recalibration around a climate that simply doesn't reward the standard nine-to-five structure during its hottest months. Visitors who try to power through midday errands out of habit find themselves fighting a heat that locals have long since decided isn't worth fighting.

## Australia: Sunscreen as a National Ethic

Australia's relationship with its own sun is less about escape and more about disciplined, near-ritualistic management, born from hard experience: Australia has some of the highest skin cancer rates in the world, a direct consequence of a population largely descended from pale-skinned European settlers living under some of the most intense UV exposure on the planet. The result is "Slip, Slop, Slap" — slip on a shirt, slop on sunscreen, slap on a hat — a public health campaign so successful and so deeply internalized that it's stopped being a slogan and become closer to an actual moral expectation, one Australians will genuinely, sincerely enforce on strangers, children, and each other without much hesitation.

Checking the UV index is a daily habit here in a way that genuinely surprises visitors from milder climates, treated with the same routine seriousness other cultures reserve for checking rain forecasts. A cloudy day is not, contrary to visitor assumptions, a low-risk day — Australian UV can punch through cloud cover at levels that catch newcomers badly sunburned within an afternoon, a lesson most expats learn exactly once, painfully, before adjusting permanently. Sunscreen isn't an occasional beach-day accessory; it's kept by the front door, in cars, in bags, applied as automatically as brushing teeth, and a startling number of adults maintain this habit with a discipline that borders on the devotional.

Extreme heat gets treated with equal, codified seriousness. Total fire bans, declared during periods of extreme heat and dryness, are actual enforceable law — lighting so much as a backyard barbecue during a declared ban is a genuine offense, not a mild social faux pas, given the very real, catastrophic bushfire risk Australia lives with every summer. Heatwave warnings trigger real behavioral shifts: outdoor work is rescheduled, vulnerable people are checked on, and the entire culture treats extreme heat with a gravity that a lot of visitors initially mistake for overcaution, right up until they experience an actual Australian heatwave and immediately understand.

## The Verdict

Albania's response to heat is collective, seasonal, and almost joyful — an entire country agreeing to relocate to the coast rather than fight the sun. Australia's response is individual, daily, and deeply disciplined — a nation that has turned sun safety into genuine civic culture out of hard necessity. Both are entirely sensible reactions to their respective climates. If I'm ranking sheer lifestyle appeal, Albania wins hands down — there's real joy in a country deciding, en masse, that August simply belongs to the sea. But if I'm ranking which approach will actually save your skin long-term, Australia's relentless sunscreen discipline wins without question.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *r/Albania — A visitor tried to get paperwork processed at a government office in mid-August and was told, without much apology, to come back in September, because "everyone" was at the coast, including, apparently, most of the relevant staff.*

> *Internations Sydney — An expat described being stopped by a stranger on a beach and handed sunscreen unprompted after being visibly unaware their shoulders were burning, calling it "the most Australian thing that has ever happened to me."*

> *expat.com Albania forum — A newcomer noted that power outages became noticeably more frequent during the hottest weeks of August, as older grids strained under a nationwide surge in fan and air conditioner use.*

## Conclusion

Albania and Australia have built two completely different survival strategies for brutal summer heat, one collective and seasonal, the other individual and daily. Albania simply leaves — decisively, joyfully, as a nation. Australia stays and manages, with a discipline forged by genuinely dangerous UV exposure and a public health campaign that actually worked. Pack patience for August bureaucracy in Tirana. Pack sunscreen, always, for Sydney. Ignore either country's relationship with its own sun at your own considerable, sunburned peril.
