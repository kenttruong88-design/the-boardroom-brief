# Ecuador Splits Into Two Different Countries Depending on Altitude. Paraguay Just Wants You to Share the Gourd.

🇪🇨 Ecuador 🇵🇾 Paraguay
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Ecuador is really two public-behaviour cultures wearing one passport: the Andean highlands, formal, reserved, quietly deferential to hierarchy and age, and the Pacific coast, loud, warm, and considerably more relaxed about personal space and spontaneity — cross from Quito to Guayaquil and the unwritten rules change almost as fast as the climate. Paraguay, meanwhile, barely registers on most people's South America itinerary at all, which is precisely why its public culture remains so unfiltered by tourism: a slower, more insular, deeply communal society built around terere, the cold yerba mate ritual passed silently around a shared gourd, where refusing a round is a bigger social error than any verbal misstep.

I've been gently corrected in Quito for using first names too early with someone twice my age, then landed in Guayaquil the same week and been hugged by a stranger before I'd finished introducing myself. I've also sat in an Asunción terere circle for two hours, saying almost nothing, and left feeling more genuinely welcomed than a month of small talk elsewhere had managed. Both countries value warmth. They just have wildly different thresholds for how loudly it should be expressed.

## Do's & Don'ts: Ecuador

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Use formal titles and last names with older or professional contacts, especially in the highlands | Assume coastal informality applies uniformly across the whole country |
| Greet with a handshake in Quito, expect a warmer cheek-kiss or hug on the coast | Rush greetings anywhere — a proper hello matters more than punctuality in most settings |
| Match your tone to the region — reserved in Quito, expressive in Guayaquil | Import one region's norms wholesale into the other; locals notice immediately |

## Do's & Don'ts: Paraguay

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Accept the terere gourd when it's passed to you — refusing is a genuine social misstep | Drink directly from anywhere but the shared bombilla straw during a group session |
| Embrace comfortable silence — conversation doesn't need to fill every moment | Treat quiet as awkwardness that needs fixing; it's simply the local rhythm |
| Show patience with a slower, more understated pace of public interaction | Expect the loud spontaneity found in bigger, more tourism-heavy South American countries |

## Ecuador: Two Climates, Two Codes of Conduct

Ecuador's public behaviour norms split along genuinely stark regional lines, and understanding this is more useful than any single blanket rule about "Ecuadorian culture." In Quito and the Andean highlands, formality runs deep: greetings favour handshakes over hugs among new acquaintances, titles and surnames matter in professional and even casual settings, and there's a reserved, unhurried quality to public interaction that owes something to both indigenous Andean custom and the general highland pace of life. Interrupting, rushing greetings, or being overly familiar too quickly reads as mildly disrespectful here, not charmingly casual.

Travel down to Guayaquil and the coast, and the temperature of public interaction rises to match the climate. Greetings become warmer and more physical, conversation grows louder and more expressive, and the reserved highland formality gives way to something closer to Caribbean warmth — quicker hugs, quicker first names, a much lower bar for spontaneous friendliness with strangers. Neither register is "the real Ecuador"; both are, simultaneously, and the skill for any long-term resident is recalibrating depending on where you've landed, sometimes within the same week.

What unites both regions is a genuine value placed on personal connection before business or logistics — rushing straight to the point, in either Quito or Guayaquil, reads as coldness rather than efficiency.

## Paraguay: The Quietest Culture in South America, By Design

Paraguay's public culture operates on an entirely different register from its more tourism-saturated neighbours, and that's not incidental — this is one of South America's least-visited countries, largely bypassed by the backpacker circuits that shaped expectations elsewhere, and its social norms have developed with minimal external pressure to perform for outsiders. The result is something genuinely distinctive: quieter, more communal, less performative than Argentina's theatricality or Brazil's exuberance.

The defining public ritual is terere — yerba mate served ice-cold rather than hot, passed silently in a shared gourd through a circle of friends, family, or colleagues, each person drinking through the same metal straw before passing it on. This isn't a quirky beverage habit; it's the primary vehicle through which Paraguayan social life happens, and understanding its etiquette (accept when offered, don't linger too long before passing it back, don't treat the silence between rounds as something to fill) matters more for genuine integration than almost any other single custom in the country.

Beyond terere circles, Paraguayan public behaviour favours understatement generally: modest volume in conversation, genuine patience with silence, and a communal orientation that assumes you're part of a group rather than an individual performing for one. Guaraní, still widely spoken alongside Spanish, reinforces this — it's a language deeply tied to communal and land-based identity, and its persistence says something real about how little Paraguay has bent its own culture to outside expectation.

## The Verdict

Ecuador wins on range and adaptability — you'll get both reserved formality and coastal warmth within one passport stamp, a genuinely useful cultural education. Paraguay wins on authenticity in the most literal sense: almost nothing here has been shaped by tourist expectation, and that unfiltered quality is increasingly rare anywhere in South America. I'd want Ecuador for variety. I'd want Paraguay for the quiet, slightly humbling experience of a culture that genuinely isn't performing for me.

## Conclusion

Ecuador and Paraguay both reward patience with public norms, just in different directions. Ecuador asks you to read the room region by region, recalibrating formality as the altitude drops. Paraguay asks you to slow down entirely, accept the gourd when it's offered, and stop treating silence as a problem to solve. Get either wrong and you'll simply look like a tourist who hasn't done the reading. Get them right, and you'll be handed something considerably better than tolerance — genuine, unhurried welcome.
