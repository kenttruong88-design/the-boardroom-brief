# Check-In Takes Ninety Seconds in One Country and a Guarantor, a Notary, and Your Firstborn in the Other

🇰🇭 Cambodia vs 🇨🇱 Chile

*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Accommodation culture splits into two camps almost everywhere I've lived: places where finding a bed is a five-minute conversation, and places where it's a bureaucratic ordeal requiring documentation you didn't know existed. Cambodia is emphatically the former — guesthouses that will negotiate a monthly rate on the spot, no contract, no fuss, no notary in sight. Chile is emphatically the latter, a country where renting a perfectly ordinary apartment can require a local guarantor (aval), a notarised contract, and proof of income that would satisfy a mortgage lender.

I've settled into a Siem Reap guesthouse in the time it takes to drink one coffee. I've also spent six weeks in Santiago failing to secure a lease because I, a foreigner with excellent references and no Chilean co-signer, apparently did not exist as a person the rental market was designed to accommodate.

## Do's & Don'ts: Cambodia

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Negotiate the monthly rate directly with the owner — it's expected and normal | Assume the nightly rate applies automatically to long stays; always ask for a monthly discount |
| Inspect the room in person before committing to anything longer than a night | Expect a written contract for shorter guesthouse stays — a verbal agreement is standard |
| Ask about generator backup — power cuts happen and not every property covers you | Pay far in advance for an unfamiliar property; a few nights first is normal practice |
| Build rapport with the owner — long-term guests get real, meaningful flexibility | Overlook mosquito netting and drainage quality when choosing a room |

## Do's & Don'ts: Chile

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Start hunting for a local aval (guarantor) or a rental insurance policy immediately | Assume your foreign income statement will satisfy a landlord without extra reassurance |
| Budget for a notarised contract and the associated fees | Expect a quick, casual handshake deal — Chilean leases are formal, documented processes |
| Use a corredora (real estate agent) — it genuinely speeds up an otherwise slow process | Skip reading the contract's exit clauses carefully; penalties for early termination can be steep |
| Be patient; securing a lease as a foreigner commonly takes several weeks | Take a landlord's initial hesitation about foreigners personally — it's systemic, not targeted |

## Cambodia: Accommodation as a Handshake

Cambodian guesthouse culture, particularly outside the very top-tier hotel chains, runs on trust, flexibility, and direct negotiation that would make a Chilean landlord break out in hives. Turn up at a guesthouse in Siem Reap or Phnom Penh, ask about a monthly rate instead of the nightly one posted at reception, and you will very often walk away with a genuinely substantial discount, agreed verbally, on the spot, no paperwork beyond a passport copy for the register.

This isn't informality born of carelessness — it reflects a hospitality sector built for flexibility, where owner-operators are making real-time judgment calls about a guest rather than running everything through institutional process. Stay a few nights first, prove you're a reasonable human being, and the owner will often extend genuine warmth: a lower rate, a room upgrade, laundry thrown in, dinner recommendations delivered like you're already a regular.

The trade-off is a total absence of the protections a Western renter takes for granted. No formal lease means no formal recourse if something goes wrong — a rate hike, a sudden request to vacate, a maintenance issue nobody addresses. It works because it runs on relationship rather than contract, and for the most part, in my experience, that relationship holds. But it requires a level of trust, and tolerance for ambiguity, that takes real adjustment if you've only ever rented through agencies and lawyers.

## Chile: Accommodation as a Bureaucratic Gauntlet

Chile, at the absolute opposite extreme, has built one of the most formally documented rental markets I've encountered anywhere. Securing even a modest apartment typically requires a notarised lease, proof of stable income — often specifically Chilean income, which is a genuine catch-22 for new arrivals — and, most crucially, an aval: a local guarantor, usually a Chilean citizen with property or strong credit, who agrees to cover your rent if you default.

For a foreigner with no local social network, finding an aval is close to impossible, and this single requirement has quietly become the defining obstacle of Chilean accommodation-hunting for new expats. The workaround — increasingly common but still a genuine cost — is a rental insurance policy (póliza de arriendo) that substitutes for a human guarantor, usually at a meaningful monthly premium on top of rent itself.

Once you're through the gauntlet, the system rewards you with real stability: contracts are detailed, tenant protections are genuinely enforced, and disputes have actual legal recourse in a way Cambodia's handshake system simply doesn't offer. A corredora — a rental agent — earns their commission here by genuinely smoothing this process for foreigners who'd otherwise spend two months getting nowhere. It's slow, formal, and occasionally maddening, but it's also honest about what it is: a system built on paper trails rather than trust, for better and for worse.

## The Verdict

Cambodia wins on speed, flexibility, and sheer human warmth — nowhere else have I settled into a home this fast, this painlessly. Chile wins on protection and stability once you're through the door — a formal contract genuinely means something here, in a way a Siem Reap handshake never quite promises.

If I need somewhere tomorrow, Cambodia. If I need somewhere for three years with real legal footing, Chile, aval and all.

## Conclusion

Cambodia and Chile represent two entirely legitimate philosophies of housing a stranger: trust the person in front of you, or trust the paperwork behind them. Cambodia's guesthouse economy moves at the speed of a genuine handshake; Chile's rental market moves at the speed of a notary's stamp. Arrive in Cambodia ready to charm an owner over a few nights. Arrive in Chile ready to hunt for a guarantor or budget for insurance, and settle in for a process measured in weeks, not days. Neither system is wrong. Just don't expect either to bend to accommodate your assumptions about how renting a room is supposed to work.
