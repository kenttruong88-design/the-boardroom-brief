# One Country Will Not Speak to You Until You've Properly Greeted Them, the Other Will Report You to the Landlord for Vacuuming on a Sunday

🇳🇦 Namibia vs 🇩🇪 Germany — Public Behaviour Norms
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Public behaviour norms exist to answer one question: what happens if you get it wrong in front of everyone? In Namibia, getting it wrong means skipping the greeting ritual and being quietly, correctly regarded as rude for the rest of the interaction. In Germany, getting it wrong means running your washing machine after 10pm on a weeknight, or god forbid, mowing your lawn on a Sunday, and discovering that an entire nation has weaponized peace and quiet into enforceable municipal law.

I've fumbled the greeting sequence in Windhoek and been met with the patient, faintly disappointed silence of someone waiting for me to catch up. I've also been lectured by a German neighbor, in genuinely impressive detail, about the acoustic properties of my recycling bin. Let's compare notes.

## Do's & Don'ts: Namibia

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Greet properly before any transaction or conversation — ask how someone is, and mean it | Launch straight into a request or question without the greeting; it reads as genuinely rude |
| Move at the unhurried pace locals set, especially outside the capital | Rush a conversation or interrupt to get to the point faster |
| Show particular respect toward elders in both speech and physical deference | Address elders casually or by first name unprompted |

## Do's & Don'ts: Germany

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Respect Ruhezeit — legally protected quiet hours, typically 10pm–6am and all day Sunday | Run a vacuum, drill, or washing machine during quiet hours; neighbors will complain, and they're legally entitled to |
| Wait for the pedestrian light even with an empty street in sight | Jaywalk in front of children — you will be publicly and sincerely scolded by a stranger |
| Separate your recycling into the correct bins with genuine precision | Mix glass colors or paper into the wrong container; it will be silently re-sorted and judged |

## Namibia: The Greeting Is the Gatekeeper

If you take one piece of advice from a lifetime of getting local customs wrong, take this one: in Namibia, you do not skip the greeting, ever, under any circumstances, no matter how small the interaction. Walking into a shop and immediately asking for what you need, without first asking how the shopkeeper is and genuinely pausing for the answer, isn't a minor social shortcut — it's read as a real breach, the conversational equivalent of shoving past someone in a doorway. This holds whether you're in a formal meeting in Windhoek or buying tomatoes from a roadside stall in the north. The greeting comes first. Always. It is the actual gate through which every other interaction has to pass.

What makes this fascinating rather than merely a rule to memorize is what it reveals about the underlying values: Namibian public life, across its many ethnic communities — Owambo, Herero, Damara, Nama, and others — treats acknowledging another person's presence and wellbeing as a precondition for doing anything else together, business included. It's the inverse of the transactional efficiency Western cultures often prize. You don't get to the point faster by skipping pleasantries; you get there slower, and correctly, by respecting the person before the task.

The pacing extends outward from there. Life outside Windhoek moves at a rhythm dictated by heat, distance, and community rather than schedules, and visitors who bring a rushed, task-first energy tend to find themselves gently but firmly out of step. Elders receive a level of deference — in address, in physical deference, in whose opinion carries weight in a group conversation — that can surprise visitors from flatter, more egalitarian-presenting societies, even ones that don't actually practice what they preach on this front. Get the greeting right, respect the pace, defer appropriately to elders, and Namibia opens up with a warmth that's very hard to manufacture through any other cultural entry point.

## Germany: Quiet Hours Are Not a Suggestion

Germany's public behaviour code operates on an entirely different value system: not deference to people, but deference to collective peace, codified with an almost comic degree of legal specificity. Ruhezeit — quiet hours, typically running 10pm to 6am on weeknights and effectively the entire day on Sundays — isn't a polite cultural norm you can shrug off if you're feeling rebellious. In many municipalities it's actual, enforceable regulation, baked into tenancy law, and neighbors absolutely will invoke it, calmly, with the confidence of people citing a statute because they are, in fact, citing a statute.

The Sunday rules deserve their own paragraph of respect. Shops close. Mowing the lawn is banned. Vacuuming is frowned upon at minimum and actionable at worst. Drilling into a wall on a Sunday afternoon is the kind of transgression that gets discussed at length in the building's stairwell for weeks afterward. It's not that Germans are humourless about this — most will happily explain the reasoning with total sincerity: everyone deserves genuinely undisturbed rest, and the only way to guarantee it is to make disturbing it illegal rather than optional. There's a communitarian logic underneath the apparent rigidity that's easy to miss if you only experience it as an inconvenience.

Then there's jaywalking, the single most reliable way to get publicly corrected by a total stranger in Germany. Cross against a red pedestrian light on an empty street at 2am, and somewhere, a German will materialize specifically to inform you that children learn by watching adults, and you have just failed the children. This isn't hostility — it's a genuine, deeply held belief in rule-following as a form of civic responsibility that starts with the smallest, most inconsequential-seeming rules and scales up from there. The recycling system operates on the same logic: glass sorted by color, paper separate from cardboard, and yes, someone will notice and silently correct your bin if you get it wrong.

## The Verdict

Namibia's public code is built around people — greet them, respect them, move at their pace. Germany's is built around systems — respect the quiet hours, respect the queue, respect the recycling schedule, and the people part sorts itself out. Both produce remarkably functional societies by completely different mechanisms. If I'm choosing which country's norms I'd rather violate by accident, Namibia wins — the correction is a patient pause and a raised eyebrow. Get it wrong in Germany and you may genuinely receive a lecture with statistical citations.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *r/Namibia — A visitor described asking a shopkeeper directly for directions without greeting first and being met with total silence until they circled back and asked "how are you" properly.*

> *Internations Berlin — An expat recounted being formally warned by their landlord after running a washing machine at 10:15pm, with the exact minute cited as the violation.*

> *expat.com Namibia forum — A newcomer noted that a business meeting that was scheduled for 30 minutes ran nearly two hours because skipping the extended greeting and small talk simply wasn't an option.*

## Conclusion

Namibia and Germany have built two of the most internally consistent public behaviour codes I've encountered, and both will make you feel, at some point, like a barbarian who's never left home. Namibia asks you to slow down and see the person in front of you before anything else. Germany asks you to respect the invisible architecture of rules that keeps collective life peaceful. Get either one wrong, and you'll know immediately — one with a patient silence, the other with a citation you didn't know existed until it was read to you, in full, on your own doorstep.
