# One Country Runs Trains to the Second, the Other Runs Them Underground as Bomb Shelters

🇺🇦 Ukraine vs 🇯🇵 Japan — City Transport and Commuting Culture
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Commuting is usually the most boring part of anyone's day, a grey non-event between home and work. Then you land somewhere like Kyiv or Tokyo and realize commuting can be a five-star anthropological event, just for wildly different reasons. One city has turned its metro into a masterpiece of Soviet-era engineering that now doubles as literal air-raid infrastructure. The other has turned the daily commute into a silent, choreographed ritual so precise it makes ballet look improvisational.

I've stood on platforms in both. In Kyiv, you learn to read the room — and the sirens — with the same instinct locals have honed out of grim necessity. In Tokyo, you learn that speaking above a whisper on the train marks you as an uncultured barbarian, full stop. Let's get into it.

## Do's & Don'ts: Ukraine

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Know your nearest metro station doubles as an air-raid shelter — locate it on day one | Panic if you hear an air raid siren on a commute; follow locals' lead calmly to shelter |
| Expect delays and route changes without much notice, especially on rail lines near front-adjacent regions | Complain loudly about disruptions — Ukrainians have earned the right to eye-roll at that |
| Appreciate that the Kyiv Metro is some of the deepest, most architecturally stunning transit in the world | Assume normal service; check Ukrzaliznytsia and local Telegram channels before travelling |

## Do's & Don'ts: Japan

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Stand to the correct side of the escalator (left in Tokyo, right in Osaka) without being told twice | Talk on your phone on the train — silence it, and yourself |
| Queue in the marked lines on the platform, even at 8am rush hour with a thousand other people | Eat on a local commuter train — it's tolerated on long-distance shinkansen only |
| Marvel, unironically, at a train apologizing for a 20-second delay | Assume the trains ever run late enough to matter — they simply don't |

## Ukraine: Commuting Under Siege, and Still Somehow On Time

Let's start with the obvious, because pretending otherwise would be an insult to the people actually living it: commuting in Ukraine right now happens under conditions no transport authority in the West has ever had to plan for. Kyiv's metro — genuinely one of the deepest and most beautiful subway systems on the planet, all soaring Soviet mosaics and chandelier-lit stations — has a second job as a bomb shelter, and its stations are now stocked, marked, and understood by every resident as dual-purpose infrastructure. Ordinary commuters have learned to keep half an ear on air raid apps while doing something as mundane as heading to the office, and they do it with a matter-of-fact composure that would floor most Westerners into paralysis.

What gets me, every time I've been through Kyiv, is the sheer stubborn normalcy underneath all this. Trams still run. The metro still opens on schedule most days. People still complain about a delayed marshrutka the exact same way people in London complain about a delayed bus, because complaining about transport is apparently a universal human right that no amount of wartime gravity can suppress. Long-distance rail via Ukrzaliznytsia has become almost mythologized — the night trains to Kyiv have carried world leaders, journalists, and returning soldiers alike, running with a reliability that frankly shames several EU rail operators I could name. There's something almost defiant about it: the trains keep running because stopping them would be its own kind of surrender.

Expats living there will tell you the adjustment isn't really about learning new routes, it's about recalibrating your relationship to uncertainty. You check Telegram channels the way people elsewhere check weather apps. You build in shelter time the way others build in traffic time. And you learn, eventually, the same dark humor locals use to get through it — because the alternative is not getting through it at all.

## Japan: The Silence Is the System

And then there's Japan, where the entire commuting culture operates on the opposite emotional register entirely: nothing is wrong, nothing has ever been wrong, and the trains will not be nearly so rude as to arrive even ninety seconds off schedule. JR East and the wider network run some of the most punctual rail services on Earth, to the point where a delay of a few minutes triggers a formal, printed apology slip for salarymen to hand their bosses — an artifact I still find both hilarious and deeply moving as a piece of institutional politeness.

The real culture shock isn't the punctuality, though. It's the silence. Tokyo's rush hour trains pack humans in at a density that should, by rights, produce chaos, and instead produces something closer to a library with wheels. No phone calls. Notifications off or on silent. Conversations dropped to a murmur. Eye contact avoided with the practiced skill of people who've spent decades in close quarters and decided collective calm was worth more than individual expression. Expats routinely describe the adjustment period as unlearning the instinct to chat, to answer a call, to exist loudly — because in Japan, commuting is a shared act of restraint, and breaking that restraint is read as a small act of aggression against everyone around you.

Escalator etiquette gets its own quiet mythology too — stand left in Tokyo, stand right in Osaka, and god help the tourist who gets it backwards during rush hour and blocks the entire flow of humanity trying to power-walk to their platform. It's a system built on decades of unspoken consensus, maintained without signage, enforced purely through collective side-eye. It's astonishing, and slightly unsettling, how well it works without anyone ever actually saying the rules out loud.

## The Verdict

There is no fair contest here, and I refuse to pretend otherwise for the sake of a tidy structure. Japan's transit system is the best in the world by almost every engineering and punctuality metric humans have invented. Ukraine's transit system is, right now, an act of civic resilience under conditions that would have broken lesser infrastructure entirely. One is a triumph of design. The other is a triumph of will. If you're grading efficiency, Japan wins in a landslide. If you're grading sheer human tenacity, Ukraine wins something far more important than a ranking.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *r/Kyiv — A commuter described ducking into the metro mid-journey for an air raid alert and continuing their commute twenty minutes later like nothing had happened, because for them, nothing unusual had.*

> *Reddit r/JapanLife — An expat admitted to answering a phone call on the Yamanote line once, out of habit, and said the collective silent judgment from the carriage was "worse than actual public shaming."*

> *Internations Tokyo — A member noted it took them three months to stop apologizing out loud every time they brushed against someone on a packed train, because apparently a small bow is the correct, silent substitute.*

## Conclusion

Two commuting cultures, two entirely different relationships with control. Japan has engineered uncertainty almost completely out of the system, then built an elaborate social code around the silence that leaves behind. Ukraine has had uncertainty forced upon it and responded not with collapse but with an unglamorous, essential kind of persistence. Neither city needs my approval. But if you ever find yourself irritated by a two-minute delay on your local line, spare a thought for the people boarding a Kyiv metro car who've built shelter routes into their morning commute and still, somehow, made it to work on time.
