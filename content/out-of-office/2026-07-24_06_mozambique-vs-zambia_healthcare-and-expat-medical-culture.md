# Mozambique Will Get You Stable. Zambia Will Get You Comfortable. Neither Will Get You Through Anything Serious Without a Flight to Johannesburg.

🇲🇿 Mozambique 🇿🇲 Zambia
*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Mozambique's healthcare system is still, honestly, recovering — decades after a civil war that gutted infrastructure, the public system remains under-resourced outside Maputo, private clinics are improving but limited, and the honest expat consensus is that anything beyond routine care means a flight south. Zambia has made more visible progress: private hospitals in Lusaka are genuinely decent for a growing range of conditions, English is the working language of the entire medical system, and the overall experience feels considerably less precarious. But scratch the surface of "serious procedure" in either country and you'll hear the same three words: South Africa, eventually.

I've had a minor procedure at a private clinic in Lusaka that was efficient, English-speaking, and entirely unremarkable in the best possible sense. I've also watched a fellow expat in Maputo get stabilised competently after an accident, then immediately medevaced to Johannesburg because the follow-up surgery simply wasn't available locally. Both countries can handle Tuesday. Neither wants to be your answer for anything that requires a specialist you've actually heard of.

## Do's & Don'ts: Mozambique

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Buy comprehensive medical evacuation insurance before you arrive, without exception | Assume Maputo's private clinics can handle anything beyond routine or moderately urgent care |
| Identify your nearest reputable private clinic immediately upon relocating | Rely on public hospitals outside genuine emergencies — resourcing varies dramatically |
| Learn basic medical Portuguese or secure a bilingual doctor contact in advance | Assume English will get you through a serious medical conversation outside major private clinics |

## Do's & Don'ts: Zambia

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Use Lusaka's private hospitals confidently for routine and moderately serious care | Assume the same standard extends reliably to rural or provincial healthcare facilities |
| Still carry medevac insurance — Lusaka is good, not comprehensive | Treat Lusaka's improving private sector as a substitute for South African specialist care |
| Register with a private clinic early so you have an established medical history on file | Wait until an emergency to find your first doctor; relationships matter here |

## Mozambique: Rebuilding, Unevenly, Still

Mozambique's public health system reflects the country's broader post-conflict trajectory: real, measurable progress over the past two decades, still operating from a genuinely difficult starting point. Outside Maputo, public facilities are frequently under-resourced, understaffed, and reserved by most expats for genuine emergencies only, if that. Even within the capital, public hospital quality varies enough that most long-term foreign residents default to private care as the norm rather than the backup plan.

The private clinic sector in Maputo has improved meaningfully, and for routine care, general practice, and moderate urgent care, it's genuinely serviceable — competent doctors, reasonable facilities, workable if you've established a relationship in advance. Where the system runs out of road is specialist and surgical care: anything beyond a certain complexity threshold and the honest, consistent advice from every expat community here is the same — Johannesburg. Comprehensive medical evacuation insurance isn't a nice-to-have in Mozambique; it's close to a prerequisite for responsible long-term residence, and the expat community treats it with a seriousness that borders on ritual.

Language adds a further layer of complexity: Portuguese is the language of the medical system, and while English-speaking doctors exist, particularly in the more internationally-oriented private clinics, you cannot assume English will carry you through a genuinely complex medical conversation the way it might elsewhere.

## Zambia: A Genuinely Decent Middle Tier

Zambia's healthcare landscape sits a clear step ahead of Mozambique's in most practical respects, without quite closing the gap to South Africa's tier. Lusaka's private hospital sector — facilities with genuinely modern equipment, English as the working medical language throughout, and doctors trained to standards that inspire real confidence — handles the vast majority of what an expat community actually needs day to day: general practice, diagnostics, moderately complex procedures, maternity care, and reasonable emergency response.

The English-language advantage shouldn't be underestimated; navigating a medical system in your own language, particularly during something stressful, is a genuinely underrated quality-of-life factor that Zambia offers more reliably than most of its regional neighbours. Registering with a private clinic early, building an actual medical history and relationship with a doctor before you need one urgently, pays off considerably here.

The honest limitation remains scale and specialisation: for genuinely serious surgical intervention, advanced oncology, or highly specialised care, Lusaka's private sector, however competent within its range, isn't a substitute for South African facilities. The expat consensus here mirrors Mozambique's, just with a higher floor before that consensus kicks in — medevac insurance remains standard practice, treated as prudent rather than paranoid.

## The Verdict

Zambia wins clearly on day-to-day medical confidence — English-language care, decent private facilities, and a system that handles the ordinary competently. Mozambique wins nothing outright here except honesty about its own limits, which counts for something; nobody pretends Maputo's system is more than it is. I'd live in Lusaka with real confidence in routine care. I'd live in Maputo with my medevac insurance details memorised. Both, ultimately, are one serious diagnosis away from a flight to Johannesburg — Zambia just delays that flight a little longer.

## What Nobody Warned You About

> *r/mozambique — got stabilised well after a minor accident in Maputo, then found out the actual surgery needed to happen in Johannesburg. Insurance covered it, but nobody tells you "stabilised" and "treated" are very different words here.*

> *Internations Lusaka — genuinely impressed with my private hospital experience here, better than I expected. Still keep my medevac policy active because everyone local told me to, repeatedly, unprompted.*

> *Quora — asked if Zambia's healthcare was "good enough" to skip evacuation insurance. Every single reply, without exception, said absolutely not.*

## Conclusion

Mozambique and Zambia both ask the same quiet question of every expat: how much are you willing to trust a system that's still, in different ways, building itself up? Zambia earns more day-to-day trust, with English-language care and a genuinely competent private sector. Mozambique earns less, and is honest enough about it that nobody local pretends otherwise. Buy the insurance in both. Build the doctor relationship early in both. And accept, in both, that Johannesburg remains everyone's unspoken plan B.
