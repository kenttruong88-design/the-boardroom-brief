# One Weekend Starts With a Braai, the Other Starts at the Mall at 4pm Because It's 43 Degrees Outside

🇿🇼 Zimbabwe vs 🇶🇦 Qatar

*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Weekends are where a country's real priorities show up, unfiltered by whatever it tells tourists about itself. Zimbabwe's weekend is an outdoor, communal, unhurried affair built around fire, family, and whatever patch of shade is available. Qatar's weekend — which, crucially, falls on Friday and Saturday, not Saturday and Sunday, a scheduling detail that will wreck your first month of calendar planning — is an indoor, air-conditioned, mall-centric affair built around escaping a climate that is actively hostile to human leisure for roughly eight months of the year.

Both are entirely rational responses to their environments. I just wish someone had told me about the Friday-Saturday thing before I showed up to an empty Doha office on what I assumed was a Monday.

## Do's & Don'ts: Zimbabwe

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Accept a braai invitation without hesitation — this is where real social life happens | Show up empty-handed; bring meat, drinks, or both |
| Get outdoors early — mornings and late afternoons are the pleasant windows | Plan anything too rigid; weekend timing runs loose and social |
| Visit a local market or national park if you're near one — weekends are when locals actually go | Assume weekend leisure means expensive leisure; most of it is free and outdoors |
| Bring your own chair if invited somewhere casual — seating is often first-come | Rush the goodbyes; farewells at a Zimbabwean gathering take genuine time |

## Do's & Don'ts: Qatar

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Reorient your entire week to a Friday–Saturday weekend, mentally and socially | Schedule anything assuming a Saturday–Sunday rhythm — you'll be alone all Friday |
| Embrace the mall as a legitimate, air-conditioned leisure destination, not a last resort | Plan extensive outdoor activity between May and September — the heat is genuinely dangerous |
| Try Friday brunch culture — a genuinely major expat weekend institution | Expect much to be open early Friday morning; the day often starts late, post-prayer |
| Visit the Corniche in the cooler evening hours | Underestimate how much weekend social life migrates entirely indoors for half the year |

## Zimbabwe: The Braai Is the Weekend

Zimbabwean weekends run on an unhurried, outdoor, deeply communal logic that starts, more often than not, around a fire. The braai — Southern Africa's answer to the barbecue, though considerably more ritual than the word suggests — is less an event than an entire social institution, one that can start at midday and drift, without anyone quite deciding to let it, well into the evening.

What struck me hardest, moving from more scheduled cultures, was the sheer elasticity of time here. Nobody's checking a watch. Invitations are loose, arrival times are suggestions, and the gathering simply continues until it doesn't, shaped by conversation rather than any calendar. Weekend leisure leans heavily outdoors and often free — a walk, a market visit, time at a dam or a patch of bush if you're near one, kids and adults mixing freely in a way that a lot of more segregated Western weekend cultures would find genuinely disorienting.

There's an economic reality underneath the culture, too, worth naming honestly rather than romanticising away: leisure in Zimbabwe has had to be resourceful and largely free for a long stretch of difficult economic years, and what's emerged from that constraint is something genuinely admirable — a weekend culture built on relationship and presence rather than spending. You don't need much money for a good Zimbabwean Saturday. You need people, a fire, and no particular plan to end it on time.

## Qatar: The Weekend Is a Climate Survival Strategy

Qatar's weekend culture makes no sense at all until you've experienced a Doha summer, at which point every single design choice suddenly reads as obvious. The Friday–Saturday weekend follows the Islamic week, with Friday reserved substantially for prayer and rest before the more social Saturday. And for roughly half the calendar year, from May through September, outdoor temperatures make daytime leisure of any real duration genuinely dangerous, not just uncomfortable — so weekend life has migrated, almost entirely, indoors.

This is where the mall stops being a shopping errand and becomes, unironically, the primary leisure infrastructure of the country. Villaggio and Doha Festival City are air-conditioned to near-arctic temperatures, packed with families on a Friday evening, and function as genuine social space — ice rinks, cinemas, elaborate food courts, kids running loose while parents wander for hours with zero intention of buying anything. Sneering at "mall culture" here, as some newly arrived expats do, badly misreads what's actually a sensible, climate-driven adaptation rather than a lack of imagination.

The other major weekend institution, particularly among the expat community, is Friday brunch — a lavish, hours-long, often boozy affair at hotel restaurants that has become genuinely central to Doha's Western expat social calendar, running from late morning well into the afternoon. The cooler evening hours, meanwhile, bring the Corniche to life — families walking, cycling, picnicking along the bay once the sun's brutal peak has passed, a brief nightly window when outdoor Doha finally, properly breathes.

## The Verdict

Zimbabwe wins on unstructured, low-cost, deeply human weekend warmth — nowhere I've lived makes hanging around a fire with near-strangers feel more instantly like family. Qatar wins on sheer adaptive ingenuity — nowhere else have I seen an entire leisure culture rebuilt so thoroughly and effectively around a hostile climate, mall culture and all.

I'd take the Zimbabwean braai for genuine human connection nine times out of ten. But I'll never again mock a Friday brunch after surviving a Doha August without one.

## Conclusion

Zimbabwe and Qatar have arrived at their weekend cultures from opposite directions — one shaped by economic resourcefulness and outdoor abundance, the other by climate hostility and engineered indoor comfort. Neither is inferior; both are precisely calibrated to the world outside the front door. Show up in Zimbabwe ready to sit by a fire for longer than planned. Show up in Qatar ready to treat the mall, and Friday brunch, with genuine seriousness. And whatever you do, check which two days of the week are actually yours before you plan anything at all.
