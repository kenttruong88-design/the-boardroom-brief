# Bring a Folder, Bring Patience, Bring a Photo Nobody Told You About

🇪🇸 Spain vs 🇵🇱 Poland: Banking and Bureaucracy

*By Suki Nakamura, Out of Office*

Spanish bureaucracy operates on a principle I've come to call strategic unavailability — the appointment system exists, technically, in the way a mirage exists on a hot road. Polish bureaucracy, by contrast, is bureaucracy in its purest, most unapologetic form: rigid, document-hungry, entirely consistent in its demands, and refreshingly honest about exactly how difficult it intends to be from the outset. Neither country will process your paperwork quickly. Only one of them will pretend otherwise.

I have refreshed a Spanish NIE appointment booking site at 6am for eleven consecutive days like a lunatic trying to buy concert tickets, and I have stood in a Warsaw urząd (government office) clutching a folder of seven documents, only to be told I needed an eighth, stamped, translated, and notarised copy of something I'd never heard of. Both experiences left me weeping, quietly, in a stairwell. Only one of them, weirdly, respected me for it.

## Do's & Don'ts

### 🇪🇸 Spain

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Book your NIE (foreigner ID number) appointment the moment you decide to move | Don't expect appointment slots to appear reliably online, refresh at odd hours |
| Use a gestor (paperwork specialist) if your budget allows, it saves real time | Don't assume one bank's account-opening rules apply to another, they vary widely |
| Bring multiple photocopies of every document to any appointment, always | Don't lose your patience with staff, a calm approach genuinely gets better outcomes |
| Consider opening a basic "cuenta no residente" first, requirements are simpler | Don't leave paperwork until the visa deadline, buffer months, not weeks |

### 🇵🇱 Poland

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Get your PESEL (national ID number) sorted before attempting to open a bank account | Don't expect English-language service at smaller branches or local offices |
| Bring a sworn translator for any document not already in Polish or English | Don't assume digital government services fully replace in-person visits yet |
| Arrive at government offices (urząd) right at opening time, queues form fast | Don't expect same-day resolution for anything involving multiple departments |
| Keep every stamped document afterward, you'll need copies again eventually | Don't assume politeness substitutes for correct paperwork, bring everything listed |

## Spain: The Appointment That Doesn't Exist Until It Does

The NIE, Spain's foreigner identification number, is the single document every expat needs before they can do essentially anything else — open a bank account, sign a lease, get a phone contract, start a job. Getting the appointment to apply for it has become its own minor national industry of frustration, with online booking slots for the cita previa (prior appointment) system releasing unpredictably, often in the middle of the night, and disappearing within minutes of appearing. Expat forums are full of people who set alarms for 3am purely to compete for a booking slot that, in a functioning system, should be a five-minute administrative task.

Once you're actually in front of a caseworker, the Spanish system reveals its second personality: reasonably efficient, courteous, and willing to help, provided your paperwork is exactly right. The gap between "getting the appointment" and "the appointment itself" is where most of the suffering lives, and a small industry of gestores, paid paperwork specialists, has sprung up specifically to help foreigners navigate this gap, often for a fee that expats universally describe as worth every euro.

Banking follows a similar two-tier logic. Opening a basic account as a non-resident is genuinely straightforward at several major banks, requiring only a passport and proof of address in some cases. But upgrading to a full resident account, or doing anything more complex like a mortgage application, drags in the NIE requirement and the accompanying bureaucratic gauntlet, creating a chicken-and-egg problem newcomers routinely get stuck in: you need a bank account to prove financial stability for your NIE application, and you sometimes need an NIE to open a full bank account.

What redeems the Spanish system, once you've cleared the appointment hurdle, is genuine warmth from staff — Spanish bureaucrats are, on balance, more willing to explain, clarify, and occasionally bend a minor rule for a visibly confused foreigner than their reputation abroad suggests. The system is broken. The people working within it are frequently not.

## Poland: Consistent, Document-Hungry, and Honest About It

Polish bureaucracy doesn't pretend to be fast, and there's an odd comfort in that honesty. The PESEL, Poland's national identification number, functions similarly to Spain's NIE as the foundational document unlocking bank accounts, healthcare registration, and formal employment, and obtaining it requires an in-person visit to a local urząd, government office, with a specific, non-negotiable list of supporting documents that will be checked meticulously against a checklist that never seems to be available in advance online.

The Polish system's defining feature is its appetite for documentation. Where Spain might accept a reasonable equivalent of a requested document, Polish offices tend to want the exact document specified, in the exact format, often requiring official Polish translation by a certified sworn translator (tłumacz przysięgły) even for documents already in English. This adds real time and cost to processes that already move slowly, and expats who arrive with what they believe is a complete file are routinely sent away to fetch one more stamped, translated, or notarised item.

Queuing culture compounds this. Government offices operate on a first-come, first-served or numbered ticket system, and arriving even fifteen minutes after opening can mean a multi-hour wait or, in busier offices, being told to return the next day entirely. Poles themselves navigate this by arriving absurdly early, a habit expats adopt quickly out of necessity rather than choice.

Banking, once PESEL is secured, is comparatively painless and increasingly digital — Polish banks have invested heavily in mobile banking infrastructure that genuinely rivals or exceeds much of Western Europe, and everyday banking tasks post-setup are fast, modern, and largely hassle-free. It's the front door, not the house itself, that's the genuine obstacle.

## The Verdict

Spain wins on eventual pleasantness — once you're through the appointment gauntlet, staff interactions are warm and the process, while slow, feels human. Poland wins on predictability — the requirements are rigid but consistently stated, and there's no lottery-style appointment system standing between you and a resolution, just a long list of documents you either have or don't. If forced to choose which bureaucracy to fight through again, I'd take Poland's document-hungry consistency over Spain's appointment roulette, if only because I know exactly what I'm walking into rather than gambling on a 3am refresh session.

## Conclusion

Spain's bureaucracy hides its difficulty behind sunshine and eventual friendliness, which somehow makes the appointment scarcity more infuriating, not less. Poland's bureaucracy makes no such attempt at charm, delivering its demands upfront, in full, with a document checklist that at least has the decency to be consistent. Neither system respects your time. Both will eventually yield, provided you arrive with more patience, more photocopies, and more buffer weeks than you think you'll possibly need. You will need every single one of them.
