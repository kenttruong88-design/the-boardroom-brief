**[HEADLINE]** How to Make a Friend: A Field Guide for the Brazilian Barbecue and the Swedish Coffee Break

**🇧🇷 Brazil · 🇸🇪 Sweden**

***By Priya Mehta, The Global Office***

In São Paulo, a colleague you met on Tuesday may invite you to their mother's birthday churrasco by Friday; in Stockholm, a colleague you have shared an office with for three years may still address you, cordially, as a stranger who happens to know where the printer is. Neither approach is rude. Both are simply operating on completely different definitions of what "knowing someone" means, and the foreign worker who fails to learn the local definition fast tends to spend their first year abroad either smothered or profoundly lonely, sometimes both in the same week.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇧🇷 Brazil

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Accept the first invitation to a churrasco (barbecue) — declining reads as social rejection, not scheduling conflict | Show up exactly on time to a social gathering — arriving within the stated hour is considered slightly awkward |
| Greet colleagues with a cheek kiss or warm handshake and genuine small talk before any business | Rush straight into the agenda in a first meeting — it reads as cold and transactional |
| Ask about someone's family, weekend, and children — personal questions build trust quickly | Assume a friendly lunch conversation stays private — Brazilian social and professional circles overlap constantly |
| Reciprocate hospitality — if invited to a home, bring something and return the invitation eventually | Be surprised when plans shift or run late; treat flexible timing as normal, not disrespect |
| Use physical warmth (a touch on the arm, standing close) as the social norm it is | Interpret a fast, effusive friendship offer as insincere — it usually isn't, even if it takes time to become a "real" tie |

#### 🇸🇪 Sweden

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Show up religiously for fika — declining the daily coffee break repeatedly signals disinterest in the team | Take Swedish reserve personally — it is a cultural default, not a verdict on you specifically |
| Let friendships build slowly through repeated, low-key contact rather than forcing intimacy | Ask deeply personal questions early — it can feel intrusive rather than warm |
| Respect silence and personal space, including the empty-seat rule on public transport | Expect to be folded into an existing Swedish friend group quickly — most Swedes keep tight groups formed in school |
| Organize or join structured activities (sports clubs, hobby groups, "afterwork" drinks) — shared activity is the easiest entry point | Show up late to a planned social event — Swedish punctuality applies socially, not just professionally |
| Treat a Swedish friendship, once formed, as durable — it tends to last | Mistake calmness and understatement for coldness — lagom (balance, moderation) shapes how warmth is expressed |

Brazil scores 38 on individualism against Sweden's 71 on Hofstede Insights' comparison — meaning Brazilian social life defaults to tight, obligation-bound in-groups (family, neighborhood, church, workplace) that absorb new members quickly but expect loyalty in return, while Swedish social life defaults to looser, self-directed networks that are harder to enter but impose fewer ongoing obligations once you're in. That single number explains most of what follows.

In Brazil, the workplace is not walled off from the rest of life; it is often the entry point into it. According to the OECD Better Life Index, 83% of Brazilians say they know someone they could rely on in a time of need — slightly below the OECD average of 91%, a gap researchers attribute less to a lack of warmth than to the intensity with which Brazilians already invest in a smaller, tighter circle rather than spreading trust broadly. Brazilians work longer hours than the OECD average of 36 per week, according to the same index, but take unusually long breaks — 53 to 60 minutes — built around eating and talking together, which functions as an informal relationship-building institution baked into the workday itself. The churrasco, effectively a barbecue with the cultural weight of a team offsite, extended-family reunion, and Friday happy hour combined, is where colleagues become friends and friends become professional references.

Sweden runs on the opposite mechanism: formalized, scheduled informality. Employees there work around 1,441 hours a year, roughly 18% below the OECD average, according to sweden.se, and the law mandates 11 consecutive hours of daily rest — structural protections that, combined with a strong cultural aversion to overtime, keep work from bleeding into personal life the way it does in Brazil. Socializing at work happens largely through fika, the twice-daily coffee ritual that sweden.se and multiple workplace culture surveys describe as near-mandatory: repeatedly skipping it is read as a refusal to bond, even though the ritual itself is built on restraint rather than effusiveness. InterNations' 2023 Expat Insider survey ranked Sweden 44th of 53 destinations for both "Local Friendliness" and "Finding Friends," with 62% of expats calling it hard to make local friends there, against a 36% global average — a reputation Swedish outlets like The Local have covered for close to a decade with headlines that amount to a shrug: yes, it's true, and yes, it's still worth it once you're in.

**The Reckoning:** The paradox is that Brazil, the country famous for warmth, produces friendships that take work to keep at full intensity — the same expressiveness that makes you feel instantly liked can make it hard to tell which invitations are genuine long-term bids and which are simply Tuesday. Sweden, the country famous for iciness, produces friendships that take months to form but, once formed, are widely described as unusually stable and low-maintenance, requiring little upkeep to survive years of silence between meetups. Put bluntly: in Brazil you can have ten new friends by Friday and wonder in six months which three actually are; in Sweden you may have zero friends by month six and then one, quietly, forever.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *r/sweden — a poster who interned in Stockholm recalled that when one exchange student in their building finally cracked a genuine friendship with a Swedish coworker, word spread through the entire dorm within days, with people asking how it was even done — evidence of just how rare, and prized, the achievement was considered.*

> *Quora — a longtime resident described the "cold Swede" stereotype as a category error: Swedes aren't unfriendly, they're calm, and what outsiders read as social rejection is usually just an unwillingness to perform warmth they don't yet feel — real warmth, once earned, tends to be steady rather than showy.*

> *The Local Sweden (reader comments) — several expats agreed that Swedes are wary of inviting people into their lives quickly because friendship there functions as a genuine commitment, not a light social gesture, so an invitation that seems slow in coming is actually a sign that it's being taken seriously.*

> *Internations — an expat working in São Paulo described being invited to a colleague's family barbecue during their first week, only to realize a year later that the same colleague still hadn't been told anything about their actual personal problems — the openness, they concluded, was social texture rather than automatic deep trust.*

> *TeamBlind — a tech worker who transferred between a Brazilian and a Scandinavian office wrote that the biggest adjustment wasn't the friendliness gap itself but recalibrating what counted as progress: in Brazil, being invited somewhere was step one of many; in Sweden, being invited anywhere at all was closer to the finish line.*

### Conclusion

If you are choosing between the two for the relationships you'll build alongside your job, the decision comes down to what kind of loneliness you can tolerate better. Brazil will not let you be alone in year one — you'll be fed, teased, and included constantly — but expect the deeper trust that makes those relationships load-bearing to take longer than the invitations suggest. Sweden will very possibly let you be alone in year one, and that will sting, particularly if you're used to workplaces doubling as social life; but the InterNations data and the forum consensus agree that what eventually forms tends to be sturdier and less exhausting to maintain. Neither country is wrong about friendship. They've just built two entirely different machines for producing it, and only one of them tells you honestly, up front, how long the wait will be — which, I'd tell a friend over a drink, is oddly the more Swedish thing to appreciate about Sweden.
