**In Tokyo You Get Assigned a Senior Who Outranks You for Life. In Paris You Get a Login and a Shrug.**

**🇯🇵 Japan · 🇫🇷 France**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

A new hire in Tokyo may spend their first weeks pouring tea correctly and learning the company song before anyone discusses actual job duties; a new hire in Paris may finish orientation in an afternoon and be expected to figure out the rest by watching who takes coffee breaks with whom. Both are functioning onboarding systems. Neither resembles the 90-day plan an American HR department would recognize, and the gap between them — one built on lifelong hierarchical mentorship, the other on quiet self-sufficiency — is worth understanding before your first Monday.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇯🇵 Japan

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Accept an assigned *senpai* (senior mentor) and defer to them visibly, even on small matters | Don't skip the group orientation ritual — company history and philosophy sessions are taken seriously |
| Learn the office's implicit rules by watching before asking | Don't expect a written 30-60-90 day plan — much onboarding is observational |
| Address seniors with appropriate honorifics from day one | Don't use casual language with a *senpai* even after months on the job |
| Show gratitude and loyalty to whoever trained you — it's remembered | Don't treat your *senpai* relationship as temporary; it often persists for years |
| Ask questions privately rather than interrupting group harmony | Don't publicly question a senior's guidance, even if you disagree |

#### 🇫🇷 France

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Expect your contract type (CDI vs. CDD) to shape how seriously onboarding is invested in | Don't assume a mentor will be formally assigned — ask if one exists |
| Address colleagues formally ("vous") until they switch to "tu" | Don't skip the ritual of greeting everyone individually each morning |
| Learn the informal social map — who takes which coffee break — fast | Don't bring up salary or personal life in early small talk |
| Respect the strict boundary between the two-hour lunch and work time | Don't schedule meetings that eat into the communal lunch window |
| Direct early questions up the hierarchy to your manager, not sideways | Don't go over your manager's head to another department without asking first |

---

Japan's onboarding runs on the *senpai-kōhai* system, an informal but binding hierarchy where a senior employee is expected to guide, correct, and vouch for a junior one — a relationship that, per Wikipedia's summary of the practice, often persists well beyond the training period and carries real obligations of loyalty in both directions. Industry research from edstellar's 2026 report on Japanese work culture notes this hierarchical structure is reinforced from orientation onward, with heavy emphasis on group identity and company philosophy rather than role-specific task lists. Hofstede Insights scores Japan 88 on long-term orientation against France's 63, which tracks: Japanese firms are onboarding you for a career, not a quarter, and the ritual reflects that horizon. The tradeoff, frequently reported by newcomers, is that explicit instructions are scarce — you're expected to absorb norms by observation, and asking too many direct questions can read as not paying attention.

France runs a leaner, more contractual process. Chronus's research on mentoring notes onboarding there blends legal formality — registering with social security, safety briefings, contract paperwork — with a cultural expectation that new hires will integrate socially without much hand-holding. Hofstede scores France 68 on power distance, meaningfully lower than Japan's structural weight but still high enough that questions flow up a defined chain rather than sideways. What actually determines how smooth your first months feel is less the org chart and more the informal rhythm: coffee breaks, the two-hour lunch, and the switch from formal "vous" to informal "tu," which functions as an unofficial signal that you've been socially accepted — a milestone with no HR equivalent.

**The Reckoning.** The instinct is to assume Japan's system, being more structured, produces faster competence, and France's system, being looser, produces faster belonging. The data suggests roughly the opposite. Japanese onboarding, precisely because it invests years into a *senpai* relationship, can leave new hires technically underprepared for weeks while they learn the social terrain, whereas French onboarding gets legal and contractual matters resolved quickly but leaves social integration almost entirely to the individual, with the "vous" to "tu" shift as the only visible marker of progress. Neither system tells you explicitly how you're doing. You just have to know which unspoken signal to watch for — deference in Tokyo, a change of pronoun in Paris.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — Someone who moved to Paris for a corporate role wrote that the biggest surprise wasn't the language, it was realizing hierarchy dictated even who they were allowed to ask a simple IT question to — going around their manager to another department was read as a serious breach.*

> *Expat.com France forum — A contributor described the two-hour lunch and rigid coffee-break rhythm as the real onboarding curriculum, since missing them meant missing the informal conversations where actual project context got shared.*

> *r/japanlife — One new arrival described spending their first two weeks almost entirely on ritual — correct tea-pouring order, seating hierarchy at meetings — before anyone explained their actual job responsibilities, and said no orientation packet had mentioned any of it.*

> *Quora — A Western hire at a Japanese firm advised newcomers to identify their assigned senpai's preferences and unspoken expectations within the first month, since the relationship, once set, was nearly impossible to renegotiate later.*

> *LinkedIn (Maria Kulakhmetova, "French office culture through the expat lens") — A marketing professional wrote that the moment a French colleague switched from "vous" to "tu" felt like a bigger professional milestone than her actual performance review, since nothing else in the office culture signaled acceptance so clearly.*

### Conclusion

Moving to Japan for work means accepting that your first real onboarding document is a person, not a PDF — invest in that *senpai* relationship early, because it will outlast your job description. Moving to France means accepting that the paperwork will be handled efficiently and the belonging will not be handed to you; you have to notice the rhythm and step into it. Neither culture is going to hold your hand through the transition — Japan will assign you a hand to hold, and France will expect you to find your own way to the coffee machine.
