# Risk Is Relative: Startup vs. Corporate Mindset in Canada vs. Singapore

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

Canada is ranked the fourth-best startup ecosystem in the world, a fact that its startup community finds simultaneously gratifying and infuriating, because the country is quietly losing its best founders to the United States while its domestic investors wait for those founders to succeed before committing capital. Singapore, meanwhile, has no domestic market of note — a city-state of six million people — and has responded to this constraint by building one of the most aggressively supportive entrepreneurial environments on earth, driven partly by government strategy, partly by cultural terror of falling behind, and partly by a concept called *kiasu* that makes even a successful company feel like it is perpetually one product cycle from irrelevance.

## Canada: The Polite Hesitation Economy

Canada's startup reputation is built on genuine strengths: world-class universities producing AI and biotech research, significant immigration of skilled technical talent, a legal and regulatory environment that is coherent by global standards, and a cost base that was, until recently, competitive with American cities. Toronto-Waterloo has produced legitimate global technology companies. The ecosystem exists.

The critique, which is also well-established, is that it exists somewhat despite the culture surrounding it. Canadian investors are, by the assessment of their own industry, disproportionately risk-averse — waiting for proof of traction that their American counterparts would provide funding to generate. Early-stage funding has been declining relative to the US, with Canada's three leading startup cities pulling in less than 5% of the venture capital that flows to their American equivalents. The inevitable result is that founders who want to build at scale leave: TD Economics has documented a persistent "silent brain drain" in which Canada-educated talent exits for San Francisco, New York, and increasingly, Singapore.

The cultural dimension is harder to quantify but widely discussed. Canada scores 48 on Hofstede's Uncertainty Avoidance — moderate, suggesting reasonable tolerance for risk in the abstract. But surveys and observational data consistently suggest that the gravitational pull of stable, well-compensated corporate employment — in banks, in government, in large resource companies — remains very strong, particularly as the cost of living in Toronto and Vancouver has risen to levels that make financial survival without a stable salary a more acute consideration. A generation of Canadian graduates that might, in a different cost environment, have taken startup risk tends to look at downtown Toronto rents and select a Bay Street salary instead.

The startups that do succeed in Canada often note a particular dynamic: Canadian talent is highly skilled, collaborative, and professional, but less willing to work the hours or accept the compensation uncertainty that early-stage startups typically demand. One founder, writing in BetaKit, described recruiting as requiring more structured roles, clearer equity explanations, and longer timelines than she had anticipated — a culture that valued psychological safety and work-life balance in ways that were not always compatible with 18-month runway.

## Singapore: The Small State That Moves Like It Is Running Out of Time

Singapore's relationship with risk is shaped by the existential logic of its geography. A city-state without natural resources, surrounded by larger neighbors, that achieved one of the world's highest living standards through manufacturing, trade, and financial services is not inclined toward complacency. *Kiasu* — the Hokkien term for "fear of losing" — is simultaneously a cultural self-critique and a description of how Singapore's economy actually works. It is the fear of being left behind translated into a national competitive posture.

The government has been an unusually active ecosystem architect. Singapore hosts over 4,500 technology startups, 220+ incubators and accelerators, and more than 500 venture capital firms. Enterprise Singapore, the statutory board responsible for supporting business growth, runs grant programs, co-investment schemes, and international market development programs that function as a government-subsidized risk buffer for early-stage companies. The 2025 Global Startup Ecosystem rankings placed Singapore in the top five globally, with first-place rankings in financial services and among the world's top two in fintech.

The tradeoff is visible in the workforce data. Singapore's average workweek runs to 45 hours, with 23% of workers regularly exceeding 48. Gallup's 2025 State of the Workplace report ranks Singapore second-lowest for employee engagement in Southeast Asia, and a TNGlobal survey found 61% of Singaporean employees report burnout. The meritocratic ideal — that performance is rewarded and results matter more than seniority — produces motivation but also acute performance anxiety. The same *kiasu* mindset that drives founders to build global companies from a standing start in a market of six million people also drives employees to answer emails at midnight and treat a lateral move as personal failure.

The cultural orientation of Singaporean startups is, by necessity, global from day one. The domestic market cannot sustain a company at scale; the default assumption is Southeast Asia as the first growth market, followed by the rest of Asia, followed by wherever the product finds traction. This produces a particular kind of startup employee — globally oriented, market-aware, and accustomed to code-switching between cultural contexts. Singapore scores only 8 on Hofstede's Uncertainty Avoidance — among the lowest in the world — suggesting an actual appetite for ambiguity that matches the startup environment well.

## The Reckoning: Different Versions of the Same Trade

Both countries are grappling with the same fundamental tension: the corporate path is safer, better compensated in the short term, and increasingly incompatible with the ambitions of economies that want to compete in technology and innovation. Canada has more natural talent than it can retain. Singapore has more institutional support than any comparable jurisdiction and a burnout rate that suggests the support is not painless.

One Canadian founder who relocated to Singapore for a year noted on a thread discussing startup culture that the environment was more energizing and more exhausting in roughly equal measure — more capital available, more market opportunity, but also a pace that made Canadian startup life feel, by comparison, like a reasonable approximation of normal.

## Priya's Take

Canada has the talent and loses it. Singapore has the infrastructure and extracts a toll for using it. If you want to build a company that might become significant, Singapore offers a more aggressive support system, a more globally connected market, and a culture that treats entrepreneurship as the sensible choice. If you want to build a company while maintaining something resembling a functioning personal life, Canada, with its imperfections and its polite risk aversion, may be the more humane operating environment. The optimal answer, which several founders in both countries have arrived at independently, is probably to incorporate in Singapore and live in Canada — though this has not been empirically tested at scale.
