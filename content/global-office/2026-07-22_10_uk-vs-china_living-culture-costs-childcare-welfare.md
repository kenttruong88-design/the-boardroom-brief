**Everyday Life Is Cheaper in China. The One Thing That Isn't Is the Thing Your Kid Needs to Go to School.**

**🇬🇧 UK · 🇨🇳 China**

<br>

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

Advance Moves' comparative cost data puts China's overall cost-of-living index at 30.7 against the UK's 68.2 — roughly 55% cheaper including rent, driven by food, transport, and utilities. But that headline number collapses the moment children enter the picture: international school fees in China's expat hubs run £10,000 to £25,000 a year and, per multiple relocation guides, routinely become a family's single largest expense — while OECD data shows UK childcare already consumes about a quarter of an average couple's household income, one of the highest shares in the OECD after the US and Switzerland. Cheap everyday life, expensive children — in that specific order — is the actual bargain being offered in China.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇬🇧 UK

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Budget seriously for childcare — OECD data shows it can approach mortgage-level costs | Don't assume the NHS eliminates all healthcare-related financial planning; private top-ups are common |
| Use the NHS and register with a GP promptly after arrival | Don't delay GP registration — it affects access speed for non-emergency care |
| Expect housing costs to dominate your budget more than food or transport | Don't underestimate deposit and letting-agency fees when budgeting a move |
| Look into workplace nursery schemes and tax-free childcare support | Don't assume childcare costs are a fixed, unavoidable full price — subsidies exist |
| Plan for a welfare and insurance system that's centralized and comprehensive | Don't assume it fully substitutes for private insurance if you want faster specialist access |

#### 🇨🇳 China

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Budget heavily for international school fees if relocating with children | Don't assume low general living costs extend to your child's education |
| Compare central Beijing/Shanghai housing costs carefully — they can rival London | Don't assume "China is cheaper" applies uniformly to housing in top-tier cities |
| Get private international health insurance before relying on local hospital tiers | Don't assume public healthcare access matches Western-style comprehensive coverage |
| Take advantage of city-specific childcare subsidy pilots where available | Don't assume subsidy pilots are nationwide — they vary sharply by city |
| Budget for a 70-year property lease structure if considering buying | Don't treat Chinese property purchase as equivalent to UK freehold ownership |

---

The UK's living-cost structure is expensive but comprehensive: high childcare costs are offset, at least partially, by a National Health Service that removes most day-to-day healthcare cost anxiety, and a welfare system built around universal access rather than tiered private coverage. The tradeoff, OECD data suggests, falls hardest on dual-income families with young children — nearly two-thirds of UK families report childcare costs exceeding their mortgage or rent, a dynamic serious enough that 43% of mothers surveyed said they'd considered leaving their job over it. Housing, not food or transport, is what actually drives the UK's higher cost-of-living index relative to China.

China's structure inverts almost every part of that equation. Daily life — food, transport, utilities — is dramatically cheaper, with typical monthly utility bills around £15 according to Advance Moves' research. But the systems a Western family relies on as a safety net — public healthcare, standardized education — don't map cleanly onto what a foreign professional's family actually needs. International schooling, the default route for most expat families, is excellent but priced closer to elite private UK schooling than to China's low general cost baseline. Property ownership follows a 70-year leasehold structure rather than the UK's freehold norm, and central Beijing or Shanghai housing, per multiple Quora threads from residents and expats, can genuinely rival London or Silicon Valley pricing despite the country's much lower overall cost index. China's government has also begun piloting childcare subsidies and tax incentives in over 20 cities specifically in response to falling birth rates — a sign the system itself recognizes the same affordability gap Western arrivals notice.

**The Reckoning.** The trap in comparing these two countries on "cost of living" alone is that the number that makes headlines — China dramatically cheaper overall — is true and almost irrelevant for anyone moving with a family. The UK's expense is broad and predictable: higher costs across the board, offset by universal systems that reduce catastrophic financial risk. China's expense is narrow and concentrated: genuinely cheap daily life, but with childcare, international education, and top-tier city housing capable of erasing the entire cost advantage for a family that needs all three. Single professionals experience China's affordability nearly as advertised. Families relocating with children experience something closer to a bait-and-switch, unless they've specifically budgeted for the exceptions.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — Someone asked whether Shanghai's cost of living matches Western Europe, and a detailed answer noted that daily expenses were dramatically cheaper, but international school tuition and central-district apartment rent alone could match or exceed what a family spent in London — a gap the general cost-of-living indexes don't capture.*

> *teamblind.com — A tech professional comparing a Shanghai total-compensation package to US and European offers found the comp-to-cost-of-living ratio genuinely favorable for a single person, but flagged that the calculation changed entirely once international schooling and private health coverage were added for a family.*

> *Quora — A respondent addressing property prices in Beijing and Shanghai noted that despite China's overall lower cost index, top-tier city apartments could exceed Silicon Valley pricing per square meter, and cautioned that the standard 70-year lease structure was a real legal difference from Western freehold ownership, not just a technicality.*

> *Quora — Someone comparing Beijing and Shanghai living costs for foreigners advised prospective movers to research district-by-district pricing rather than trusting national averages, since expat-heavy central areas ran close to New York or San Francisco levels while outer districts remained dramatically cheaper.*

### Conclusion

If you're moving to the UK, budget for high fixed costs but lean on the safety net — the NHS and universal welfare system genuinely reduce catastrophic risk, even if childcare will eat a serious share of your income. If you're moving to China, don't let the low headline cost-of-living number lull you into under-budgeting for the specific line items — international schooling, private insurance, and central-city housing — that don't follow the national average at all. The honest version: the UK charges you steadily and protects you broadly; China charges you almost nothing until the moment it charges you everything, all at once, for your kid's school.
