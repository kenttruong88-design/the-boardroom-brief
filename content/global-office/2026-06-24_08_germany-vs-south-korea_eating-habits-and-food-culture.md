# Kimchi and Kartoffeln: Germany and South Korea's Very Different Philosophies of Eating

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

Germany and South Korea share a reputation for taking food seriously and a demographic history of food scarcity that shaped current eating culture in ways that both countries are simultaneously proud of and in the process of moving past. The similarities end there. One country has built its food identity around a fermented cabbage dish that functions simultaneously as condiment, side dish, national symbol, and probiotic, and eats it at every meal including breakfast. The other country has built its food identity around a meal structure that begins, ends, and is periodically interrupted by bread, and considers a good rye loaf to be a genuine object of cultural patrimony. Both are correct. Neither fully understands the other.

## Germany: The Land of Serious Bread and Earnest Lunch Breaks

German food culture is characterised by structure, regional pride, and an honest relationship with meat and starch that no amount of wellness industry pressure has fundamentally altered. The three daily meals maintain their basic architecture across generations: a typically light breakfast (Frühstück) of bread, cold cuts, and cheese; a hot lunch (Mittagessen) that remains the main meal of the day in many traditional and family contexts; and a cold dinner (Abendbrot — literally "evening bread") that is often, again, bread with accompaniments. The hot dinner that defines meal culture in many other European countries is, in Germany, more of a modern concession to urban working schedules than a cultural norm.

German bread culture deserves special mention. Germany has approximately 3,000 registered types of bread — a figure that sounds like nationalist hyperbole until you walk into any German bakery and are confronted with the actual taxonomy of rye, wheat, spelt, and mixed-grain loaves arranged by region, density, and intended social occasion. UNESCO added German bread culture to its Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2015, a recognition that Germans accepted with the careful combination of satisfaction and understatement that is their default register for praise.

Eating habits in Germany have evolved significantly with urbanisation and internationalisation. Doner kebab, introduced by Turkish migrant workers in the 1970s, has become so thoroughly integrated into German street food that it is now considered essentially local. Italian, Vietnamese, Thai, and Japanese cuisine are mainstream in German cities. The Mittagstisch — the daily lunch menu offered by restaurants and canteens at reduced prices — remains a fixture of office culture, with many German workers viewing a proper hot lunch as non-negotiable. Per the AdventureKorea food comparison, "in Germany, people normally go to restaurants on special occasions" for dinner, but the lunch landscape tells a different story.

Drinking culture is inseparable from German food culture. Beer accompanies most informal meals; regional varieties — Weissbier in Bavaria, Kölsch in Cologne, Pils across the north — are points of local identity that Germans defend with a conviction that suggests the arguments have been running for centuries, because they have.

## South Korea: The Communal Table and the Midnight Delivery App

South Korean eating culture is organised around rice, community, and a side-dish ecology of remarkable complexity. The baseline Korean meal structure consists of plain boiled rice (bap), soup (guk or jjigae), kimchi, and three to five additional side dishes (banchan) — a system that treats every meal as a social occasion requiring distribution, sharing, and the implicit understanding that leaving a dish unfinished is acceptable but leaving the rice bowl unfinished is not.

Eating together carries weight in Korean professional culture that extends well beyond the pleasure of the meal. The *hoesik* — the mandatory social dining and drinking outing with coworkers — has been a defining feature of Korean corporate culture for decades. These gatherings, typically involving multiple rounds at different establishments (the first for food, subsequent ones for drinks), functioned as loyalty demonstrations and relationship-building events with attendance pressure that was not entirely voluntary. The hoesik is declining in frequency, particularly among younger workers who have less patience for mandatory conviviality at 11pm on a Tuesday, but its cultural logic — that eating together creates bonds that working together does not — remains active.

Korean eating patterns have shifted substantially in the past two decades, with Statista data showing increasing ultra-processed food consumption, declining breakfast rates (particularly among millennials and Gen Z), and an explosion of delivery app usage that has made midnight tteokbokki delivery a normalised behaviour in major cities. Skipping meals, previously a mark of circumstance rather than choice, has become increasingly common among young urban professionals navigating demanding work schedules and beauty-standard pressures.

The shared-plate dining model — multiple dishes in the centre of the table, everyone eating from common dishes — produces a social dynamic that German restaurant meals, where individual plates are the norm and sharing requires advance agreement, typically do not. First-time German visitors to Korea report the shared-plate system as either deeply welcoming or slightly alarming, depending on their relationship with food boundaries.

## The Reckoning: What Dinner Is For

The contrast between German and Korean food culture maps onto a broader cultural difference in what meals are supposed to accomplish. German meal culture has become increasingly private and individual, with Abendbrot eaten at home and dinner in restaurants a deliberate social occasion rather than a default. Korean meal culture, even as hoesik declines and delivery apps atomise eating, retains a residual communal logic — the table as the primary site of relationship maintenance.

One expat who had worked in both Frankfurt and Seoul described the adjustment in a forum post: "In Germany, my colleagues and I worked together for eight months before we had a proper meal together. In Seoul, we were eating together by the second week. By the fourth week we were eating together at midnight and I had opinions about which fried chicken chain was best."

## Priya's Take

Every culture's food tells you what that culture values when it is not performing for anyone. Germany values craftsmanship, regional specificity, and the restorative logic of a proper lunch. South Korea values communal participation, flavour complexity, and the social utility of a table that nobody leaves until the relationship has been adequately maintained. Both are legitimate food philosophies, and both produce, as a side effect, some of the most rewarding dining experiences available on the planet.
