# Team Spirit, Two Ways: Japan Bonds Over Beer, France Bonds Over Not Bonding With You Yet

🇯🇵 Japan · 🇫🇷 France

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

In Japan, colleagues will happily drink with you until midnight and still call you "san" the next morning. In France, colleagues will decline your lunch invitation for a decade and then, without warning, invite you to their mother's house in the Dordogne. Both countries insist they have rich, cohesive social fabric. Both are technically correct, and both will make a newcomer feel, for a suspiciously long time, like furniture.

#### 🇯🇵 Japan

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Attend the first nomikai you're invited to | Assume declining is neutral — repeated no-shows can quietly cost you visibility |
| Pour drinks for others before your own runs low | Fill your own glass first |
| Join a hobby circle for faster social traction | Expect friendship from a single good conversation |
| Order a soft drink if you'd rather not drink | Make a scene about abstaining — quiet non-participation works better |
| Attend the company trip (shanai ryoko) once | Skip every optional event and expect information to reach you anyway |
| Stay polite and indirect even after drinks | Assume alcohol erases hierarchy — it loosens tongues, not org charts |

#### 🇫🇷 France

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Keep lunch talk light at first — no politics, salary, religion | Try to become close friends with colleagues in week one |
| Say "bonjour" individually to each colleague | Give the room one generic wave — it reads as impersonal |
| Accept an apéro invitation when it comes | Treat apéro as networking — it's social ritual, not a work event |
| Respect the lunch hour as a real break | Eat at your desk while everyone else goes out |
| Be patient; real friendship often needs a club or shared activity | Expect after-work texts to follow naturally from office rapport |
| Disconnect after hours where possible | Assume evening emails will be answered — disconnection is law since 2017 |

### Japan

Japan's approach to bonding rests on a simple premise: harmony is manufactured, not discovered. *Nomikai* — after-work drinking sessions, sometimes called "nominication," a portmanteau of drinking and communication — has functioned for decades as the informal operating system beneath the formal one. A 2024 Tokyo Shoko Research survey of 4,578 companies found nomikai attendance has fallen from 51.8% in 2019 to 29.1%, a genuine post-pandemic shift. But the ritual hasn't disappeared so much as gone optional in name while staying consequential in practice.

The OECD's Measuring Social Connectedness report finds only 44% of people in Japan have in-person conversations with friends or family at least weekly — a figure that trails behind the habit of outsourcing socializing to work, since work is where the hours go. Japan sits below the OECD average on the Community and Work-Life Balance dimensions of the Better Life Index, and separate OECD data show roughly 17.9% of Japanese employees working more than 50 hours a week. The government's 2021 creation of a Minister of Loneliness was less symbolism than acknowledgment that a culture built on group cohesion has, paradoxically, produced significant individual isolation. Since 2022, a revised power-harassment law makes coercing attendance or drinking legally risky, which hasn't eliminated the social cost of opting out so much as moved it from the org chart to the grapevine.

### France

France's cohesion metrics look, on paper, considerably healthier. The OECD Better Life Index finds 94% of people in France say they know someone they could rely on in a crisis, above the OECD average of 91%, and voter turnout of 75% beats the OECD average of 69%. INSEE data show roughly 40% of French residents over 16 — about 21.6 million people — belong to at least one association, from sports clubs to civic groups. On paper, this is a nation of joiners.

The catch is that French bonding runs on a closed-shop model. As accounts collected by The Local France describe it, friendships form largely in school and university and aren't renewed with much enthusiasm afterward; workplace relationships stay professional rather than personal. The apéro — the post-work, pre-dinner ritual of drinks and light snacks — is real and frequent, but it isn't an audition for friendship; it's simply what French colleagues do instead of becoming friends. The country's 2017 "right to disconnect" law was meant to protect the boundary between office and home, though a Randstad study found 71.4% of French employees still work more than the legal 35-hour week — the boundary is better protected in statute than in practice.

### The Reckoning

Here is the irony: Japan, the more collectivist society on Hofstede's cultural dimensions (individualism score of 46, versus France's 71), bonds through an activity — drinking together — that is external and observable, a performance of unity anyone can, in theory, attend. France, the more individualist of the two, bonds through pre-existing closed networks nearly invisible to outsiders and nearly impossible to join by simply showing up. Japan's system is inclusive in form and exclusionary in consequence; France's is the reverse — outwardly aloof, but durable once the gate opens.

The other reversal is temporal. Japanese bonding front-loads the effort: attend enough nomikai in year one and the social debt is largely paid. French bonding back-loads it indefinitely — colleagues pleasant-but-distant in year one may become genuine friends in year five, on a timeline no early enthusiasm will accelerate. Neither country would call its system inefficient. Both are, in their own ways, correct not to.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — one contributor described being technically excellent at their job in Japan but consistently overlooked for informal-coordination projects, having skipped nomikai and spoken only English; learning conversational Japanese and attending a handful of drinking sessions visibly shifted how colleagues treated them.*

> *Quora — another respondent, on the consequences of skipping nomikai, noted that Japan's 2022 power-harassment law now makes coercing attendance illegal, but quietly declining too often still tends to leave you out of important meetings — the pressure just moved underground.*

> *TheLocal.fr — a longtime resident wrote that forming a real friendship with French people can take the better part of a decade, since most adults stay close with school friends and feel little pull to expand the circle, making newcomers' early friendliness look faintly suspicious.*

> *TheLocal.fr — a separate piece on workplace mistakes in France warned against being too familiar too quickly with colleagues, noting French office relationships stay professionally bounded far longer than an American or British newcomer would expect, and overreaching early sets things back rather than speeding them up.*

> *Hacker News — a commenter who had worked inside a Japanese corporation described the office itself as a bonding mechanism: enormous rooms, long rows of desks organized strictly by section, the manager's desk at the row's end — hierarchy, not sociability, as the room's organizing principle, with nomikai as the only sanctioned release valve.*

### Conclusion

If a friend asked which posting would make them feel at home faster, the honest answer is neither, on the timeline they'd want. Japan hands you a schedule of mandatory-but-not-mandatory rituals that, attended dutifully for a year, earn provisional belonging. France hands you nothing, asks nothing of you, and quietly evaluates whether you're worth including for roughly five years before deciding. The real variable isn't which country is friendlier — it's whether you'd rather do the work up front, hungover, or do the waiting, sober and slightly bored. I'd tell a friend: pack liver supplements for Tokyo, and pack patience for Paris. Neither is optional, and neither comes with a refund.
