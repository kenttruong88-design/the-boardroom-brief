**Bébé or Bust: What Having a Baby Actually Costs You in France and Australia**

🇫🇷 France · 🇦🇺 Australia

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

France will hand a new mother sixteen weeks of leave paid at roughly ninety percent of her salary and then, three years later, put her toddler into free full-time public school. Australia will hand a new mother twenty-six weeks of leave paid at the flat national minimum wage and then invite her to spend a meaningful share of it on childcare that can run to $144 a day. Both countries consider themselves reasonably generous to parents. Only one of them is arguing from evidence.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇫🇷 France

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Register for a crèche spot the moment you're pregnant — waitlists in Paris can run a year | Assume "generous parental leave" means an open-ended stay-at-home option; the system nudges toward full-time return |
| Use the CAF quotient familial calculator to find your real childcare cost, not the sticker price | Skip registering with Sécurité Sociale before the birth — both reimbursements and paid leave depend on it |
| Plan your return-to-work date around free, full-time école maternelle starting at age 3 | Expect the extended, flexible stay-at-home norm common in Anglophone countries — it isn't built into the culture |
| Learn the term "congé de naissance" — the 2026 reform adding two paid months per parent, shareable | Overlook that high earners pay steeply more for the same crèche place than low earners |
| Confirm whether your employer tops up the state's roughly 90% maternity pay rate | Be surprised if colleagues seem puzzled that you'd want to stay home long-term — full-time work is the default |
| Keep paperwork current with CAF — family benefits require ongoing documentation | Assume unpaid extended leave (PreParE) works like paid leave — it is a separate, lower-income track |

#### 🇦🇺 Australia

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Apply for Parental Leave Pay through Services Australia well before the birth — it doesn't backdate | Expect Parental Leave Pay to track your usual salary — it is paid at the flat national minimum wage |
| Check whether your employer tops up the government rate — many corporate policies do | Assume affordable, universal childcare is imminent — subsidies help, but out-of-pocket costs stay high |
| Factor the new 12% superannuation contribution on leave (since July 2025) into retirement planning | Wait until the birth to look for childcare — decent centres fill up during pregnancy |
| Compare long day care, family day care, and outside-school-hours-care rates by postcode — they vary wildly | Treat a drop to part-time as a personal failure — the tax-and-subsidy system quietly pushes many mothers there |
| Check your Child Care Subsidy percentage against household income before assuming care is unaffordable | Ignore the compounding gap this creates in your superannuation balance over decades |
| Budget for the "childcare cliff" — a fifth working day can barely cover its own fees after tax and care costs | Assume Australia's laid-back reputation extends to its formal parental-leave generosity — it doesn't |

France's approach to new parents reads like a policy built by people who assumed mothers would keep working, because mostly they do. Maternity leave runs sixteen weeks for a first or second child, paid at roughly 90% of salary through Sécurité Sociale, alongside 28 days of paternity leave — itself more than doubled from 11 days as recently as 2021. From January 2026, a new "congé de naissance" reform adds up to two further paid months per parent, shareable and usable within the child's first year, paid at around 50% of salary up to roughly €1,900 a month. Crucially, the state then meets working parents halfway on childcare: crèche fees are scaled to income through the CAF's quotient familial, from as little as €50 a month for the lowest earners up to a capped rate — commonly cited around €600 to €1,000 monthly — for the highest. By age three, nearly every French child is enrolled in free, full-time public école maternelle, which the OECD has specifically praised. The result is a country with both one of Europe's highest birth rates and one of its highest rates of maternal full-time employment — not a coincidence, according to family-policy researchers, so much as the point.

Australia's system is younger and thinner by comparison. Paid Parental Leave has expanded steadily — from 18 weeks at the scheme's 2011 launch to 24 weeks shareable for children born in the 2025–26 financial year, rising to 130 days (26 weeks) from July 2026 — but it is paid flat, at the national minimum wage, regardless of a parent's actual salary. From July 2025, the Australian Taxation Office began adding a 12% superannuation contribution on top of Parental Leave Pay, a genuine improvement aimed at closing the retirement gap the system has long created. Even so, the OECD calculates the average member country offers 53 weeks of paid maternity and parental leave combined; Australia remains well below that. Meanwhile childcare is priced by the market rather than income-scaled the way French crèches are: the Department of Education's own data puts the national average at roughly $343 a week, or about $144 a day for centre-based long day care, with the Child Care Subsidy easing but not eliminating the bill for most households. The practical consequence, widely documented by Australian researchers, is that a middle-income mother's fourth or fifth working day can net her as little as five to eight dollars an hour after tax and childcare fees are deducted — which is less an incentive to work than a polite request not to.

**The Reckoning.** The irony is that Australia markets itself internationally as the relaxed, family-first alternative to workaholic Europe, while France carries a reputation for bureaucratic rigidity and unmovable striking unions — yet it is France's rigid system that gets mothers back to full-time paid work faster and keeps them there. Hofstede Insights scores France notably lower than Australia on individualism (71 versus 90) and on masculinity, or achievement-orientation (43 versus 61), and the parenting infrastructure tracks the theory neatly: France built collective, state-run solutions — subsidised crèches, universal preschool — while Australia largely left new parents to negotiate a private, market-priced arrangement with their own employer and budget. Australia's 24-to-26-week leave now sounds generous next to France's 16, until you notice it is paid at minimum wage rather than salary, and that the childcare bill waiting on the other side of it is not softened by any equivalent of the quotient familial.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *The Local France — Contributors to the expat parenting blog Mama Loves Paris told the outlet that France "is not an easy place to be a stay-at-home parent": French mothers typically return to paid work within a few months of birth, the childcare system is built entirely around working parents, and expat mothers who choose to stay home long-term often report friends and neighbours treating the choice as slightly odd rather than admirable.*

> *Quora — A respondent describing Australia's childcare system pointed to the math working mothers actually face: once tax, the Child Care Subsidy taper, and daily fees are netted out, many mothers earn only a handful of dollars for an extra day of work, which is why so many quietly settle into three- or four-day weeks rather than five.*

> *Américaine in France (expat blog) — An American who gave birth in a French public hospital described being startled that her only itemised bill was 70 euros a night for a private room upgrade her top-up insurance fully covered — no bill for the delivery itself, plus extensive, state-covered postpartum pelvic-floor physiotherapy she had never heard offered back home.*

> *Career Catch Up (expat career-coaching blog) — Its comparison of French and Australian work-life attitudes put it plainly: in France, a child is "one slice of the pie" and mothers are expected to want an identity beyond parenting, while in Australia the child tends to become "the core center around which the rest gravitates," with mothers more often pausing careers for extended stretches rather than returning quickly.*

> *Quora — A separate thread on relocating with children noted that families moving to France for the schooling and childcare infrastructure alone often recoup the adjustment cost within a year or two, once they compare their old day-care invoices to a French crèche bill calculated on the quotient familial.*

### Conclusion

The decision-relevant difference is not which country loves children more in the abstract — both will insist they do — but which one has actually engineered its system so a parent's income survives contact with parenthood. France front-loads structure: shorter leave, but income-linked pay, income-scaled childcare, and free full-time schooling from three, all pushing toward a fast, full-time return. Australia front-loads flexibility and a longer leave window, but hands parents a flat minimum-wage payment and a childcare market that quietly taxes the second income until part-time work looks like the rational choice rather than the reluctant one. If your priority is protecting your career trajectory on a fixed timeline, France does more of that work for you than its bureaucratic reputation suggests. If your priority is lifestyle and you can absorb the childcare bill, Australia will not stop you — it will just send you the invoice.

If a friend asked me over drinks, I'd put it this way: France will make you fill out more forms, and Australia will make you do more math — pick your bureaucracy accordingly.
