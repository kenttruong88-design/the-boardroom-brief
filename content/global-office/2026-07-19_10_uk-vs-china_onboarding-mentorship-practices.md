# In Britain, You Get a Buddy. In China, You Get a Master. Neither Will Show You Where the Printer Is.

🇬🇧 UK · 🇨🇳 China

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

In the United Kingdom, a new hire is typically assigned a "buddy" — a peer, roughly your level, whose job is to be vaguely available for the first fortnight before returning to their actual workload. In China, a new hire may be informally attached to a shifu, or master, in a lineage of instruction that traces back through guilds, kung fu schools, and centuries of apprenticeship before it ever touched a spreadsheet. One tradition is a scheduling accommodation. The other is a relationship with obligations attached in both directions. According to the CIPD's 2024 Resourcing and Talent Planning Report, 41 percent of new UK employees quit within twelve weeks of starting, and 27 percent of employers have been "ghosted" by a recruit who simply never turned up on day one. Whatever Britain is doing to welcome people, it is not, by its own professional body's admission, working especially well.

## Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇬🇧 UK

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Ask for the induction schedule in writing on day one — CIPD guidance treats a structured induction plan as standard, so if none appears, request one | Assume your assigned "buddy" has been given protected time for the role; CIPD's own community forum shows employers debating whether to compensate buddies at all, and often deciding not to |
| Use the probation review (typically at three and six months) as a genuine checkpoint, not a formality to nod through | Take "let's grab a coffee sometime" as a scheduled commitment — in UK offices it is frequently sincere and almost never diarised |
| Attend the after-work pub trip in your first fortnight, even briefly — relationship capital in UK offices is built informally, not in training modules | Interpret a manager's soft phrasing ("might be worth considering") as optional; British indirectness often carries more instruction than its wording suggests |
| Read the flexible-working and hybrid policy yourself rather than waiting to be told — UK firms increasingly assume self-service | Wait to be chased for missing paperwork; with over a quarter of new starters not showing up at all, HR teams are stretched thin and follow-up is inconsistent |
| Ask your buddy direct, practical questions (expenses, IT access, meeting norms) early — informal systems reward people who use them proactively | Assume silence from your manager in week one means disapproval; it more often means nobody has allocated time to check in |

#### 🇨🇳 China

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Identify who is functioning as your shifu, even if no one uses that word or assigns the role on paper — mentorship in Chinese firms is frequently informal but still real and hierarchical | Pitch an idea directly to a senior leader in an early meeting without routing it through your direct supervisor first — one foreign hire described a room going silent after doing exactly this |
| Learn and use proper titles — "Manager Li," "Director Zhang" — rather than first names, even after you've been there months | Openly disagree with your boss in front of the group; raise concerns privately instead, or expect the meeting itself to produce only polite agreement |
| Invest time in relationship-building outside the office — meals, WeChat groups, informal gatherings — since guanxi shapes daily cooperation as much as job title does | Expect a formal HR onboarding packet with a checklist; a great deal of onboarding knowledge is transmitted by observation, not documentation |
| Treat a soft "we'll think about it" as the polite version of no, not as active consideration | Assume performance alone secures goodwill; in accounts from foreign employees, a boss's personal favor has been described as mattering more than output |
| Expect long hours to double as the primary mechanism for building trust with colleagues and superiors | Interrupt or "correct" a senior colleague's English-language slip or accent in a mixed meeting — face (mianzi) is preserved carefully in group settings |

## UK: The Buddy Nobody Budgeted For

The UK's onboarding infrastructure looks, on paper, sensible and light-touch: an induction pack, a line manager check-in schedule, and a buddy system pairing new joiners with an established colleague. The CIPD's own factsheets recommend exactly this, framing buddy and mentor arrangements as a standard part of induction. What the guidance does not resolve is who pays for the time. A thread on the CIPD's own community forum captures the tension neatly: a member asked whether staff acting as buddies should receive extra compensation, since the role is time-consuming and currently unpaid at her organisation. The most upvoted response noted, with some surprise, that this was "the first time" the commenter had heard of a company outsourcing onboarding responsibility to staff without adjusting their workload or pay. In other words, even the professionals who write the guidance are unsure whether the informal buddy model is generous flexibility or quietly unfunded labour.

The consequences of that ambiguity show up in retention numbers rather than philosophy. The CIPD's 2024 data — 41 percent of new hires leaving within twelve weeks, more than a quarter ghosting employers before they even start — suggests that "informal but well-intentioned" onboarding is not obviously outperforming more rigid systems elsewhere. The OECD's 2025 Employment Outlook notes that UK training participation drops off sharply with age, with only about a third of workers aged 60–65 receiving training in 2023, compared with over half of those aged 25–44 — a pattern consistent with a system that treats onboarding and skills development as front-loaded and then largely abandoned. British informality has genuine charms: flat hierarchies, self-deprecating humour used deliberately to build rapport, and managers who mean it when they say you can ask anything. It is simply an approach that assumes competent adults will self-organise their way to belonging, which — per the CIPD's own churn figures — a meaningful minority do not.

## China: The Master Who Was Never Formally Announced

China's mentorship tradition predates the modern office by roughly a millennium. The shifu (師傅/師父) relationship — a term meaning, depending on the character used, "expertise with teaching ability" or "teaching as a father would" — governed the transmission of skill in guilds, martial arts schools, and craft trades long before it was adapted to the workplace. Academic accounts of the Chinese apprenticeship model describe it as more than skills transfer: a bond built on loyalty, respect, and an implicit hierarchy that persists even after the apprentice becomes proficient. That hierarchical instinct survives intact in the modern Chinese office, where power distance — measured by Hofstede Insights at 80 for China versus 35 for the UK — translates into onboarding that runs through observation, deference, and relationship rather than a printed checklist.

Foreign hires consistently describe learning this the hard way. One contributor on Quora described guanxi — personal connections — as mattering more for day-to-day cooperation than raw output, noting that strong performance "means nothing if your boss does not like you." A separate account, shared on the professional forum Blind, described meetings conducted fluently in Mandarin that would abruptly switch to English the moment a non-Chinese colleague entered — a courtesy that nonetheless left the newcomer permanently a beat behind the actual discussion. China's National Bureau of Statistics data shows average weekly working hours climbing from 47.5 in 2022 to 49 in 2024, alongside falling voluntary turnover — evidence that companies are extracting more from existing staff rather than investing heavily in bringing new ones up to speed. An SCMP opinion piece put the sharper edge on it: that the country's work culture, as currently configured, is giving young employees less reason for optimism, not more.

## The Reckoning: Buddy Systems Don't Scale, Master-Apprentice Doesn't Document

The irony is that both systems solve for the wrong failure mode. The UK optimises for approachability — flat structures, first names, an assigned buddy — while leaving unresolved who is actually accountable for a new hire's competence, which is presumably why over a quarter of new starters vanish before finishing their first day. China optimises for depth of relationship and long-term obligation, embedding the new hire in a hierarchy that assumes years of tenure, while leaving almost nothing written down for someone who needs an answer on day three. One system is generous with information and stingy with structure; the other is generous with structure (however unspoken) and stingy with information for anyone outside the hierarchy's inner circle.

The most counterintuitive divergence is who ends up more supported, on paper versus in practice. British onboarding looks more egalitarian — no shifu, no formal deference required — but the CIPD's own attrition figures suggest the informality frequently curdles into abandonment. Chinese onboarding looks more rigid and exclusionary from the outside, but the shifu relationship, once actually established, tends to produce a level of individual investment few UK buddy assignments ever approach. Neither country has solved the problem so much as chosen which kind of loneliness a new employee experiences first.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *CIPD Community forum — a UK HR practitioner asked colleagues whether staff should be paid extra for acting as onboarding "buddies," since the unpaid role was eating into their actual workload; the top reply noted it was the first time they'd heard of a company shifting onboarding responsibility onto staff without adjusting pay or hours.*

> *Hacker News (Ask HN) — a Western professional who relocated to China for work described the adjustment as genuinely rewarding once past the initial disorientation, citing the chance to absorb a new culture and language as compensation the job offer letter never mentioned.*

> *Quora — a contributor working in China described guanxi as functionally outweighing merit in daily office life, warning that strong performance "means nothing if your boss does not like you," and that cultivating goodwill with colleagues and superiors was itself a core, unstated job duty.*

> *Blind (teamblind.com) — an employee at a Chinese-headquartered firm described meetings conducted entirely in Mandarin switching abruptly to English whenever they entered the room, a gesture of politeness that nonetheless left them structurally a step behind every decision being made.*

> *Medium (a foreign employee's account of their first Chinese office) — after pitching an idea directly to a senior manager in an early meeting, the room went quiet; a colleague later explained, privately, that the idea should have gone through their direct supervisor first — a lesson in hierarchy nobody had thought to mention in advance.*

### Conclusion

The practical difference that matters most isn't warmth versus coldness — both countries produce plenty of both — it's where the burden of figuring things out actually lands. In the UK, it lands on the new hire, cushioned by an approachable but often under-resourced buddy who was never quite told this was part of their job. In China, it lands on the new hire too, but the path out involves correctly identifying, without being told, whose good opinion actually matters and building it deliberately over time. Neither brochure mentions this, because neither company particularly wants to admit that "onboarding" is, in both places, largely a euphemism for "figure out the unwritten rules quickly, or don't."

If a friend asked me which one to take a job in: ask, in the interview itself, who exactly is accountable for your first ninety days — not whether there's a "buddy" or a "mentor," because both countries have learned that the label impresses recruiters more than it protects new hires.
