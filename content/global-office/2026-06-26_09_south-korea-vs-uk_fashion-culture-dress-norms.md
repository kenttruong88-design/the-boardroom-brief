# Coordinated or Eclectic: Fashion Culture in South Korea vs. the UK

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

South Korea is a country where dermatology apps offer discount packages on eyelid surgery and Botox to recent high school graduates, cosmetics exports exceeded $10 billion in 2024, and going to a convenience store without makeup is, in parts of Seoul, a social act requiring conscious decision. Britain, meanwhile, has produced both the most precise tailoring tradition in the world and a celebrated history of subcultural fashion so deliberately ugly that it required theory to explain. Both countries care deeply about appearance. They are simply caring about it in entirely different directions.

## South Korea: The Coordinated Republic

Korean fashion culture operates within a logic of high collective investment and high social visibility. Appearance in South Korea is not merely personal preference; it is social currency. A 2017 survey found that nearly 40% of respondents reported experiencing discrimination based on appearance when applying for jobs — a statistic that is either alarming or clarifying, depending on how many people around you are wearing coordinated outfits.

The term *lookism* (*외모지상주의*) has been formalized in Korean public discourse as a recognized phenomenon: the systematic advantage conferred by conforming to appearance standards that are themselves codified, commercially serviced, and heavily influenced by the K-entertainment industry. The K-beauty complex — multi-step skincare routines, cosmetic dermatology, a domestic cosmetics industry that exported $10.2 billion worth of product in 2024 alone — is both cause and effect of a culture in which appearance maintenance is understood as a reasonable personal expenditure and a reasonable social expectation.

Daily fashion in South Korea reflects this investment. Seoul's streetwear scene is internationally influential — brands like Musinsa-stocked designers and labels worn by K-pop artists have made Korean street style a globally referenced aesthetic. The dominant codes involve layering, oversized silhouettes, immaculate sneakers, and a kind of studied casualness that requires considerable effort to achieve. Gender boundaries in fashion have loosened considerably among younger Koreans, with gender-fluid styling normalized in urban contexts in ways that would have been unusual a generation earlier. Korean luxury fashion brands have moved from domestic novelty to global recognition, with Seoul now occupying a peer position alongside Paris and Milan in international fashion commentary.

The social pressure attached to all this is real and documented. Bare arms are discouraged — Korean women commonly wear cardigans over summer outfits regardless of temperature. Skincare routines average nearly six products per person. Plastic surgery rates are among the world's highest. A thread on r/korea featuring a foreign woman's description of being told her appearance would limit her professional prospects in Korea generated significant response volume, primarily from other expats nodding in recognition. The expectation is coordinated, polished, and current. Deviation is noted.

## UK: The Sovereign Right to Look Odd

British fashion culture has produced, across roughly the same period that Korea was developing its beauty standards, punk, Britpop, the New Romantics, mod, rave, grime aesthetics, and the sustained global dominance of Savile Row bespoke tailoring. These things are not accidents; they reflect a culture that treats fashion simultaneously as class performance, subcultural identity, institutional heritage, and occasionally performance art. Britain's approach to dress is less coordinated and, if anything, more ideologically committed.

The contemporary UK fashion market is substantial — revenue hit an estimated $85.85 billion in 2024, with British per-capita clothing spend around £1,260 annually. High street brands (Primark, Marks & Spencer, Zara in its British context) coexist with heritage tailors, a robust charity shop culture, and a secondhand fashion market that has grown considerably as environmental awareness and cost-of-living pressures have converged. Online fashion accounts for 31% of the market, the highest in Europe.

What distinguishes British fashion culture from Korean is the range of what is socially acceptable. The UK has no equivalent to Korean lookism as a formalized social phenomenon — discrimination on the basis of appearance is at least nominally prohibited under the Equality Act, though anyone who has observed British workplace culture will have noticed that "smart" and "put together" retain their meaning. Class, however, operates powerfully through fashion in ways that are subtler and more difficult to regulate: the right brand, the right cut, the right deliberate shabbiness all signal belonging to particular social groups with precision that requires decoding.

British subcultural fashion — the deliberate transgression of appearance norms as cultural statement — has no direct Korean equivalent. The punk who safety-pins their jacket and the Savile Row client who pays £4,000 for a suit that fits with invisible precision are both, in their way, making a statement that is essentially British: that dress is a choice, an act of self-expression, and that its social function is acknowledged but not necessarily deferred to. The Hofstede Individualism score of 89 supports this reading: British identity is constructed personally and expressed individually, even when that individual expression is group-coded.

## The Reckoning: Collective Standards vs. Individual Expression

Korea's fashion culture produces a high average standard of appearance maintenance and significant social cost for those who cannot or will not meet it. Beauty in South Korea is an industry, a norm, and a professional expectation simultaneously. The pressure to conform is real, the infrastructure for doing so is extraordinary, and the results are visible.

British fashion culture produces more variation, more eccentricity, and considerably more tolerance for failure. A thread on r/AskEurope comparing British and Korean fashion noted that the UK felt like "anything goes as long as it's not bothering anyone," while Korea felt like "everything is noticed and assessed." Whether the first of these is freedom or indifference, and the second is community standards or social surveillance, depends largely on how one feels about being evaluated by strangers.

## Priya's Take

South Korea has decided that appearance is a collective standard, that the standard is worth maintaining, and that the infrastructure to do so is worth building and funding. Britain has decided that appearance is an individual expression, that individual expressions are worth tolerating, and that the infrastructure for this is mostly secondhand shops and strong opinions. Both are coherent positions. Only one of them has produced a globally dominant skincare industry and the other has produced an internationally beloved tradition of looking deliberately terrible on purpose, which is perhaps sufficient evidence that both countries have found their natural register.
