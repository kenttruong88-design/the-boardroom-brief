# South Korea vs. the UK: Two Countries, Two Ways to Leave a Meeting Without Saying What You Mean

🇰🇷 **South Korea** · 🇬🇧 **United Kingdom**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

In a Seoul conference room, the most senior person present can end a debate by saying nothing at all — a pause held two seconds longer than comfortable functions as a verdict, and everyone in the room reads it correctly. In a London conference room, the most senior person present can end a debate by saying "that's a really interesting point, let's take it offline," which also functions as a verdict, just one delivered with a compliment stapled to it so nobody quite notices they've lost. South Korea logged 1,872 hours of work per employee in 2023, according to the OECD — 17 percent above the OECD average, which is a great deal of time in which to hold meetings whose outcomes were largely settled before anyone sat down. The UK's full-time employees, per the Office for National Statistics, worked a comparatively modest 36.7 hours a week as of Q4 2025 — plenty of time, evidently, to soften every disagreement into a suggestion. Both countries hold meetings constantly. In neither one would most employees claim the meeting is where the real decision actually happens.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇰🇷 South Korea

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Wait for the most senior person in the room to speak first, even if you already have the answer | Volunteer your opinion before someone senior to you has spoken |
| Address colleagues by title and rank ("Team Leader Kim," "Director Park"), not first names | Use first names with seniors, even informally, unless explicitly invited to |
| Treat silence from a senior person as data — it may mean disagreement, or that the decision was already made elsewhere | Fill an awkward silence with more talking; it reads as impatience with the room, not helpfulness |
| Save real pushback for a private conversation after the meeting | Publicly contradict or challenge a superior's proposal during the meeting itself |
| Arrive early and let the seating sort itself out — the most senior person sits facing the door | Sit in the senior-designated seat, even if it looks empty and available |
| Treat the after-meeting dinner (*hoesik*) as where things actually get settled | Assume the official minutes capture what was really decided |

#### 🇬🇧 United Kingdom

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Expect five to ten minutes of small talk — weather, commute, weekend — before business starts | Launch straight into the agenda; it reads as brusque, even rude |
| Learn to hear "that's an interesting idea" or "let's park that" as a polite no | Take these phrases literally and expect the idea to resurface later |
| Frame disagreement as a question — "Have we considered…?" — rather than a flat objection | Say "I disagree" bluntly in front of the group; save direct language for one-on-ones |
| Show up a few minutes early, every time | Assume lateness will be addressed directly — it will be noted and never mentioned |
| Send a follow-up email restating what you understood was agreed | Leave assuming consensus was reached just because nobody objected out loud |
| Use dry, self-deprecating humour to soften a strong position | Mistake politeness for the absence of a firm opinion — it is usually still there, just wrapped |

### South Korea: The Meeting as Ceremony

South Korea scores 60 on Hofstede's power distance index against the UK's 35, and 18 on individualism against the UK's 89 — numbers that, translated into office life, mean rank is not just respected in Korean meetings, it is choreographed. According to the Cultural Atlas's business culture guide, the most senior person typically sits facing the door, speaks first, and speaks without interruption; more junior staff are present largely to demonstrate attentiveness rather than to debate. This is not, contrary to outsider assumption, a system without dissent — it is a system where dissent has been relocated. Disagreement in a Korean meeting travels through *nunchi*, the skill of reading unspoken cues, and resurfaces later in a private conversation, rarely in the room where it was first provoked.

The workweek supplies the raw material for a great deal of this ceremony. Even with the government's 2018 cap of 52 hours a week (40 regular plus 12 overtime), enforced by the Ministry of Employment and Labor, South Korean employees still logged 1,872 hours in 2023 — enough hours, and enough meetings, that efficiency has had to be manufactured elsewhere: in the informal *hoesik* dinner that follows the formal meeting, where rank briefly loosens and the actual negotiating happens over grilled meat and poured drinks. A Korean meeting, in other words, is often less a forum for decision-making than a scheduled performance of hierarchy, with the substance handled somewhere the minutes will never mention.

### United Kingdom: The Meeting as Diplomacy

British meetings look, on the surface, far more democratic. Per Commisceo Global's analysis of UK communication norms, British professionals mix direct and indirect styles depending on the stakes — clear and efficient when the topic is neutral, deliberately vague when it might cause offence. Decisions, according to multiple UK business-culture guides, are framed as consensus even when a manager has already decided the outcome; instructions arrive dressed as suggestions ("perhaps we might," "would it be worth"). A 2024 British Council report on foreign professionals in UK workplaces found that indirect feedback was one of the single biggest adjustment hurdles for newcomers, with proposals not rejected outright but "gently parked" — a phrase that does a remarkable amount of quiet work.

None of this happens quickly. UK meetings routinely open with several minutes of small talk before business begins, a ritual that can read as inefficiency to newcomers from more transactional cultures but functions as social lubrication that makes the coming diplomacy possible. Silence in a British meeting does not carry the same hierarchical weight it does in Seoul — nobody present is likely reading it as a verdict from the most senior person — but it does not mean agreement either. It just means nobody has yet found a sufficiently polite way to say what they think.

### The Reckoning

Put side by side, the two systems solve the identical problem — how to disagree with someone without a confrontation — through nearly opposite mechanics. Korea manages it vertically: rank tells you exactly who may object and when, and the tension is resolved off-site, later, privately. The UK manages it horizontally: everyone appears to be participating in the same conversation, but the actual objection is embedded inside the vocabulary itself, and figuring out who has just been told no requires fluency in a dialect of politeness rather than an org chart.

The counterintuitive part is which system is easier for an outsider to learn. Korea's meeting hierarchy is rigid, but it is legible — a newcomer can be handed the rules (senior speaks first, silence means wait, disagreement goes private) and follow them, however uncomfortably, from day one. The UK's meeting culture looks looser and more welcoming, which is precisely what makes it harder to decode: there is no chart to memorise, only years of exposure to phrases like "not unreasonable" and "we'll see," and a newcomer can sit through a dozen meetings genuinely unsure whether their idea was accepted, rejected, or simply never processed as an idea at all.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — Under Korea's ranking system, raising a concern with a manager can be shut down flatly, with no real space for discussion; the expectation is that you simply do as instructed, even when you can see a better way, and pushing further is read as a discipline problem rather than initiative.*

> *Blind (teamblind.com) — A user considering a move to Korea was warned off by a commenter relaying a Korean partner's account of corporate life there: brutal hours, gapchil (superiors abusing rank), and mandatory hoesik dinners that eat into what little personal time remains. The verdict offered: Korea is a wonderful place to visit, considerably less wonderful to work in.*

> *Hacker News — In a thread on Korean corporate culture, several commenters converged on the same practical point: multinational firms in Korea struggle to retain foreign hires specifically because of the reporting and meeting hierarchy, which imports the same seniority-first structure regardless of the company's nominal "global" culture on paper.*

> *Quora — Responding to a question on British office etiquette, one answer named indirect feedback as the single hardest habit for a foreign professional to acquire in the UK — ideas are rarely rejected outright, just quietly reframed as "an interesting angle," and learning to hear that as a no takes real time.*

> *AskAManager.org — A reader comparing international office cultures noted that UK meetings feel flat and consultative on the surface, with input nominally invited from everyone in the room, but that the real test of whether your view landed happens afterward — at the pub, on a Friday, informally, where people say what the meeting room made them too polite to say.*

### Conclusion

The practical, decision-relevant difference for anyone weighing Seoul against London comes down to where the real conversation is hidden, not whether one culture is more honest than the other. In South Korea, disagreement is legal, but only off-stage — you will need to learn who outranks you, defer accordingly in the room, and raise anything that matters in a private conversation or over dinner afterward. In the UK, disagreement is technically permitted anywhere, but you will need years of exposure to a vocabulary of hedges before you can reliably tell a genuine invitation to speak from a polite request to stop. Neither system rewards bluntness; they just bury it in different places — one in rank, one in phrasing.

If you need rules you can be handed on your first day, Korea's hierarchy, however rigid, is at least written down somewhere. If you can tolerate months of not being entirely sure what just happened in a meeting, the UK will eventually reward your patience with fluency in a very particular kind of English. Either way, book the after-hours socialising into your calendar before you book the meeting — that's where the actual business gets done in both places, they just serve different drinks.
