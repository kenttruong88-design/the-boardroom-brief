# America Will Give You Eleven Vacation Days If You're Lucky. Germany Gives You Thirty By Law and Judges You for Not Taking Them.

**🇺🇸 USA · 🇩🇪 Germany**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

The average American worker receives about 11 paid vacation days a year, and nearly a third of US employees get no paid time off at all, since the United States remains the only advanced economy on earth with no federal law requiring it. German employees, by contrast, get a statutory minimum of 20 working days, which in practice usually stacks up to 28 to 30 days once public holidays and standard company policy are factored in — roughly six to seven weeks of annual time off, guaranteed regardless of employer goodwill. Americans leave 765 million vacation days unused every year. Germans would consider that a personal and organizational failure, not a sign of dedication.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇺🇸 USA

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Negotiate PTO explicitly during hiring — it's a benefit you secure for yourself, not a legal guarantee | Assume any given offer includes generous leave — nearly a third of US workers get no paid vacation at all |
| Check your state's specific leave laws — protections vary significantly since there's no federal floor | Assume federal law protects your time off — it doesn't, and state-level rules differ widely |
| Actually use the PTO you're granted — unused days don't roll over indefinitely at most companies and rarely convert to real value | Bank vacation days as a show of commitment — Americans forfeit an average of 9.5 unused PTO days a year for essentially nothing |
| Ask directly about unwritten norms around checking email while on leave — they vary sharply by company and manager | Assume "unlimited PTO" policies mean more time off in practice — research shows they often result in less vacation actually taken |
| Build your case for more leave around retention and performance data if negotiating — US employers respond to business framing | Expect vacation policy to improve automatically with tenure alone — the 5-year and 20-year bumps exist but are modest |

#### 🇩🇪 Germany

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Plan your full 28 to 30 days early in the year — Germans treat annual leave as something to schedule deliberately, not squeeze in | Let vacation days lapse unused — doing so is read as a planning failure on your part, not evidence of dedication |
| Fully disconnect while on leave — checking email or Slack during vacation is genuinely unusual and not expected | Feel guilty about being unreachable while on leave — no one else feels guilty about it either |

### USA: Vacation as a Negotiated Perk, Not a Guarantee

American leave culture is defined less by what's offered than by what isn't legally required. With no federal law mandating any paid vacation, per the Center for American Progress, employers set their own policies entirely at their discretion — average tenure-based accrual gets a new hire to roughly 11 days, rising to 15 after five years and 20 after two decades, according to Bureau of Labor Statistics data, but 31 percent of employees receive no paid time off whatsoever, and 28 million American workers lack paid vacation or holidays entirely.

The deeper pattern is behavioral, not just structural: even where PTO exists, Americans routinely don't take it. Roughly 765 million vacation days go unused annually, with the average worker forfeiting 9.5 days a year — about 55 percent of what they're granted — and a FlexJobs survey found 23 percent of American workers took zero days off in 2025 entirely. Roughly a quarter of American work happens outside normal hours, reflecting a culture where being reachable, even on nominal time off, still functions as an implicit expectation in many workplaces.

### Germany: Vacation as Statutory Infrastructure

German leave policy runs on the opposite assumption — that adequate rest is a baseline entitlement, not a negotiated extra. The Federal Vacation Law guarantees a statutory minimum of 20 working days for a five-day week, but the effective floor is considerably higher once public holidays (9 to 13 depending on the federal state) and standard company practice are included — most German employees receive 28 to 30 days in contractual practice, and workers with recognized disabilities receive an additional statutory five days on top of that.

Crucially, the culture around actually using that leave matches the legal generosity. Per multiple accounts of German workplace norms, the operating principle is clean and bidirectional: when you're in the office, you're working, and when you're off, you're genuinely off — there's no comparable expectation of quiet availability during vacation, and unused leave reads as a personal or managerial failure to plan properly, not as evidence of commitment. Germany's higher Hofstede uncertainty avoidance score (65) shows up here as a structured, rule-bound approach to rest that Americans, operating in a far more individually negotiated system, tend to find almost suspiciously generous at first.

### The Reckoning

The clearest inversion: in America, not taking your vacation can function as a quiet performance of dedication, even though the data shows it mostly just evaporates unused and unrewarded. In Germany, not taking your vacation is a mark against you — evidence you can't manage your own workload or time, the same logic that governs German attitudes toward staying late at the office. Rest, in the German system, is infrastructure to be used, not a reserve to be hoarded for appearances.

The second gap is portability. German leave protections travel with the worker regardless of employer, embedded in national labor law — a German employee changing jobs carries the same baseline guarantees forward. An American employee negotiating a new PTO policy at each new employer starts from scratch every time, which is part of why US vacation culture varies so wildly company to company, while German vacation culture stays comparatively stable nationwide.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *r/antiwork (via dailydot reporting) — A German professional whose company was acquired by a US firm went viral describing his "work culture shock" at discovering he couldn't find an American job offering more than two weeks of paid time off, calling the entire structure "mind-blowing" once he saw it up close as an actual employment term rather than a stereotype.*

> *Quora — A German respondent comparing work-life attitudes across countries noted that in Germany, status is measured partly by visible civic and family engagement outside of work, a value system that treats the American focus on financial maximization through long hours as somewhat puzzling rather than admirable.*

> *Blind (TeamBlind) — An American tech worker who transferred to a German office of the same company described feeling a lingering compulsion to check Slack during their first proper two-week vacation there, only to realize no German colleague had messaged at all, not out of unusual courtesy, but because that was simply the norm.*

> *Internations — An American expat in Berlin recounted a German colleague expressing genuine concern, not admiration, upon learning the American had over a dozen unused vacation days sitting in their account near year's end, treating it as a scheduling problem to be fixed rather than a virtue.*

> *Quora — A German worker who spent a year on assignment in the US wrote that the hardest adjustment was realizing "unlimited PTO," offered as a selling point by their American employer, actually resulted in colleagues taking less vacation than a fixed, guaranteed allotment would have, since the ambiguity discouraged people from ever feeling fully entitled to take time off.*

### Conclusion

If you're moving from Germany to the US, negotiate hard for explicit, fixed vacation days rather than trusting vague policies like "unlimited PTO," and expect to advocate for time off that would have simply been guaranteed by law at home. If you're moving from the US to Germany, unlearn the instinct to treat unused vacation as a virtue — plan your full allotment early, disconnect completely when you take it, and understand that in this system, rest is not something you have to earn back.

My honest advice, over a drink: in America, ask for more than you think you can get, because nobody else is going to hand it to you. In Germany, take all of what you're already owed, because leaving it on the table won't impress anyone — it'll just look like you can't plan your own calendar.
