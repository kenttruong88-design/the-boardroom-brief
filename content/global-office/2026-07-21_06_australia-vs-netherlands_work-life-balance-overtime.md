# Australia Legislated the Right to Ignore Your Boss. The Dutch Never Needed the Law — They Just Leave.

**🇦🇺 Australia · 🇳🇱 Netherlands**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

Australian workers put in almost five weeks of unpaid overtime a year, according to Unions NSW research, worth an estimated A$92 billion in wages nobody collects — a problem serious enough that the government passed a "right to disconnect" law in 2024 with fines attached. The Netherlands has the shortest average working week in the EU at 32.1 hours, achieved not through legislation forcing anyone to stop working, but through nearly 40 percent of the workforce simply choosing part-time contracts as a default, unstigmatized life setting. One country had to write a law to get people to log off. The other built an economy where logging off was never really up for debate.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇦🇺 Australia

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Know your right to disconnect — since 2024 you can lawfully ignore unreasonable after-hours contact from your employer | Assume the law bans all after-hours contact — "unreasonable" is judged case by case, factoring in your seniority and role |
| Negotiate any regular overtime explicitly — anything beyond the standard 38-hour week should be agreed, not assumed | Accept open-ended "reasonable additional hours" without clarifying what that means in practice at your specific employer |
| Take your full leave entitlement — Australians who don't are quietly seen as poor at managing workload, not as heroic | Treat unused annual leave as a badge of dedication — it isn't, and it won't be rewarded |
| Expect flexible hours to be genuinely usable, not just written into policy | Assume flexibility is universal — it varies sharply by industry, and unpaid overtime remains common in several sectors |
| Push back on scope creep early — Australia's overtime culture tends to build gradually rather than arrive all at once | Wait until burnout to raise workload concerns — by Australian workplace norms, raising it early is expected, not weak |

#### 🇳🇱 Netherlands

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Ask about part-time or four-day arrangements when negotiating your contract — it's culturally normal, not a red flag | Assume requesting reduced hours signals low ambition — nearly 40 percent of the Dutch workforce works part-time by choice |
| Leave on time, every time, without an excuse | Feel obliged to explain why you're leaving at 5pm — no one else does |
| Expect colleagues, including parents, to leave for school pickup around 5pm as routine, not an exception | Schedule late-afternoon meetings assuming full attendance — many colleagues will have already blocked that time |
| Trust that shorter hours reflect efficiency, not lower output — Dutch productivity per hour worked ranks among the OECD's highest | Equate hours worked with value delivered — in Dutch offices that correlation is explicitly rejected |
| Raise a heavy workload directly with your manager — it's treated as a fixable scheduling problem, not a personal failing | Push through consistent overtime quietly — colleagues are more likely to read it as a sign something's wrong with your workload, not your dedication |

### Australia: Legislating a Fix for a Cultural Drift

Australia's self-image as a relaxed, work-to-live culture has been quietly eroding at the numbers level. Unions NSW research found Australian employees performing close to five weeks of unpaid overtime annually, roughly 10 percent of total working hours, and the OECD's Better Life Index shows 13 percent of Australian employees working 50-plus-hour weeks, above the OECD average of 10 percent. Australia dropped from fourth to eighth in a 2024 global work-life balance ranking, overtaken by Ireland, Denmark, Norway, Canada, and — notably — Germany.

The government's response, the 2024 "right to disconnect" law, gives employees a protected ability to ignore after-hours calls and messages from employers without penalty, with fines for employers who retaliate. It's a meaningful legal backstop, but it's also a tacit admission that the informal, egalitarian workplace culture Australia is known for — flexible hours, standard 38-hour weeks, relatively low Hofstede power distance — had stopped self-correcting on its own, and needed statutory teeth to hold the line.

### Netherlands: Structural, Not Legislative, Restraint

The Dutch approach didn't require a comparable disconnect law because the underlying structure of work already discourages overtime by default. Eurostat 2023 data puts the Netherlands' average working week at just 32.1 hours for workers aged 20 to 64 — the shortest in the EU — driven by a labor market where part-time work has been institutionalized since policy reforms in the 1980s and 90s, rather than treated as a lesser or stigmatized arrangement. More than half of Dutch women and around a quarter of Dutch men work part-time, and per OECD analysis, this has become a genuinely gender-equalizing feature of the labor market rather than a niche accommodation.

Crucially, shorter hours haven't come at the cost of output: Dutch GDP per hour worked sits around USD 97 (PPP), among the global top tier, well above the OECD average. Consistently staying late in a Dutch office doesn't read as commitment — per multiple workplace guides, it more commonly prompts quiet concern about whether someone is managing their workload efficiently, echoing the same social logic found in Germany's Feierabend culture, though achieved through a different mechanism: normalized part-time contracts rather than statutory hour caps.

### The Reckoning

The most counterintuitive divergence: Australia, culturally coded internationally as the laid-back, beach-and-barbecue alternative to grinding Northern hemisphere work culture, actually logs more unpaid overtime and ranks worse on work-life balance than the buttoned-up, efficiency-obsessed Netherlands. The stereotype and the statistics have quietly diverged.

The second irony is mechanism. Australia's fix is legal and reactive — a right you have to know about and actively invoke against an employer already contacting you. The Dutch fix is structural and preventative — reduced hours built into the default employment contract before the overtime problem ever gets a chance to start. One country protects you after the fact; the other mostly prevents the fact from happening.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — An Australian who relocated to Amsterdam for a marketing role wrote that the strangest first month was realizing four-day work weeks weren't a special accommodation for parents or a perk you had to earn — colleagues at every level, including a senior director, worked reduced schedules as a simple lifestyle default.*

> *Reddit — A Dutch professional who spent two years working for an Australian firm remotely described being baffled by a colleague who bragged about answering emails during a family holiday, noting that in their own office such a habit would have prompted a concerned conversation with HR, not admiration.*

> *Internations — An expat in Sydney recounted discovering Australia's right-to-disconnect law only after a manager apologized for a weekend text, adding that the fact the law needed to exist at all told them more about the real state of Australian overtime culture than any recruiter's pitch about "laid-back Aussie work-life balance" ever had.*

> *Fortune/Good.is reporting (cited via a relocated professional's account) — A parent who moved from Melbourne to Rotterdam described the adjustment to a 5pm school-pickup culture as almost disorienting at first, since leaving that early in an Australian office, even for childcare, had usually required a quiet, apologetic explanation.*

> *Quora — A Dutch worker who took a contract role with an Australian company noted that despite Australia's reputation abroad for a relaxed lifestyle, the actual unpaid overtime expected in their specific industry exceeded anything they'd experienced at home, and they wished they'd checked sector-specific norms rather than trusting the national stereotype.*

### Conclusion

If you're moving from the Netherlands to Australia, don't assume the "laid-back" reputation extends automatically to your specific employer or industry — check actual overtime norms, and know your right-to-disconnect protections exist precisely because the informal culture needed backup. If you're moving from Australia to the Netherlands, expect a genuine structural shift: shorter contracted hours, normalized part-time arrangements at every level, and a workplace that treats staying late as a minor red flag rather than a compliment.

My honest advice, over a drink: Australia's relaxed reputation is a starting assumption you should verify, not trust. The Netherlands' efficiency reputation, unusually, turns out to be true. Pack accordingly.
