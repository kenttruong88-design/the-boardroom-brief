**Nobody Will Tell You Your Presentation Was Bad. They'll Just Never Invite You to Give Another One.**

**🇩🇪 Germany · 🇰🇷 South Korea**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

In Frankfurt, a manager will tell you, without ceremony, that your slide deck was unfocused and your Q3 numbers were soft — then ask if you'd like a coffee. In Seoul, a manager may say nothing at all, and the silence will be the review. According to Hofstede Insights, Germany scores 67 on individualism against South Korea's 18, and that fifty-point gap is precisely the distance between a culture that evaluates your work out loud and one that evaluates it through what doesn't get said. Anyone relocating for a corporate role should budget time to relearn what "no news" means.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇩🇪 Germany

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Prepare for direct, itemized criticism of your actual output — it isn't personal | Don't read directness as hostility; it's the German version of respect |
| Bring data to your own defense — Germans argue with evidence, not charm | Don't get defensive in the moment; ask clarifying questions instead |
| Expect scheduled, structured *Mitarbeitergespräche* (employee talks), often annual or biannual | Don't expect praise sandwiches — German feedback skips the bread |
| Push back on a rating you disagree with — it's expected and respected | Don't assume silence between reviews means all is well; ask |
| Document your own achievements before the meeting | Don't wing it — an unprepared employee looks unserious |

#### 🇰🇷 South Korea

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Watch for indirect signals — fewer assignments, less eye contact, exclusion from meetings | Don't expect your manager to say "you underperformed" outright |
| Read feedback delivered in group or team-achievement framing as the real message | Don't take critique privately if it's framed around the team — it's still about you |
| Ask a trusted senior colleague (*sunbae*) to translate what a review "really meant" | Don't challenge a superior's assessment in front of others |
| Treat *hoesik* (team dinners) as informal extensions of the review cycle | Don't skip them — absence gets noted and remembered |
| Expect performance to be judged partly on hours present, not just output | Don't assume leaving on time signals efficiency to your boss |

---

Germany's feedback culture is built on the same low-context directness that governs its trains and its tax forms: say the thing, cite the evidence, move on. The *Mitarbeitergespräch* is often a formally scheduled, semi-legal document — HR-templated, signed by both parties — and German employees are trained from their earliest jobs to treat criticism as information rather than insult. This is consistent with Germany's Hofstede uncertainty avoidance score of 65: structure and clearly defined roles are comforting, not bureaucratic overkill. The flip side, often reported by newcomers, is that the bluntness lands harder than intended, especially for anyone raised in a culture where feedback is cushioned. Pitt Business to the World's writing on adjusting to workplace norms abroad notes that directness elsewhere is frequently mistaken for coldness when it's actually meant as efficiency.

South Korea inverts the entire model. With an individualism score of just 18 against Germany's 67, feedback is filtered through group harmony — *nunchi*, the skill of reading a room without being told anything explicitly, is arguably a more important professional competency than whatever appears on a formal review form. Direct criticism, especially from a senior to a junior in front of others, risks causing loss of face on both sides, so managers route disapproval through omission: fewer strategic projects, less facetime, a shorter conversation than usual. South Korea also carries a longer working-hours culture — Wikipedia's compilation of OECD-adjacent labor data puts South Korea meaningfully above Germany's roughly 1,340 average annual hours, among the lowest in the OECD — and presence itself becomes a silent performance signal that has nothing to do with your actual review.

**The Reckoning.** The counterintuitive part is that Germany, the culture stereotyped as cold, is actually the one that tells you plainly where you stand, while South Korea, often described as warm and communal, is the one that leaves you guessing. A German employee walks out of a review knowing exactly what to fix. A South Korean employee may walk out of the identical meeting having heard only compliments about the team, and later discover through a reassignment that something went wrong. For someone used to American-style "constructive feedback" sandwiches, neither system is comfortable — Germany will feel too blunt, Korea will feel like reading tea leaves. The practical skill to build, in either country, is the same: stop expecting the review meeting to be where you learn the truth, and start reading the actual channel your new employer uses to communicate it.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — A foreign employee working under Korea's ranking system described being shut down mid-discussion by a manager who treated any pushback as insubordination, regardless of whether the underlying point was correct — a dynamic they said no onboarding document had prepared them for.*

> *teamblind.com — One tech worker described performance reviews less as objective evaluation and more as "a mood test in disguise," where a manager's read of your attitude during the quarter mattered more than the metrics on the page.*

> *Pitt Business to the World — A student on a work placement in Korea wrote that the hardest adjustment wasn't the language, it was realizing that not being assigned new work was itself a form of feedback nobody would ever say out loud.*

> *Quora — An expat who moved from a Western multinational into a Korean firm advised newcomers to find one trusted senior colleague early — not for friendship, but as a translator, since direct questions to management about "how am I doing" often produced vague, unusable answers.*

> *Quora — A commenter reflecting on German versus other European offices noted that German colleagues would openly critique a specific slide or number in a meeting, and it took months to stop internally flinching before realizing no one else in the room thought it was rude.*

### Conclusion

If you're moving to Germany for work, the operating principle is: assume every silence is neutral and every stated criticism is solvable. If you're moving to South Korea, invert that entirely — assume every silence carries information and treat the absence of direct criticism as the actual data point. Both systems produce competent, functioning workplaces; they just define honesty differently, one through words, one through signals. The honest thing I'd tell a friend over a drink: in Germany, worry when they stop giving you notes; in Korea, worry when they stop inviting you to dinner.
