# Jeans Are Not a Statement in Amsterdam. In Sydney, Everything Is.

**🇦🇺 Australia · 🇳🇱 Netherlands**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

A Dutch office worker owns, on average, 5.4 pairs of jeans and will wear whichever pair draws the least attention. An Australian office worker owns roughly the same number of pairs of jeans and will wear whichever pair says something about who they are. Both will call the result "smart casual." Only one of them means it as a compliment to the outfit; the other means it as a description of the weather.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇦🇺 Australia

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Invest in one structured blazer — it upgrades a t-shirt for client meetings and costs less than a full suit rotation | Assume "smart casual" means the same thing in Sydney, Melbourne, and Brisbane — it doesn't, and each city will judge you for guessing wrong |
| Dress slightly more formally in the first week, then calibrate down | Wear a full suit to a Melbourne startup on day one — you will be the only one, and not in a good way |
| Keep sun-appropriate fabrics in rotation — offices run on natural light and open windows more than you'd expect | Confuse "casual" with "careless" — thongs (flip-flops) and boardshorts are a beach thing, not a Friday-in-the-office thing outside a handful of industries |
| Notice that seniority in Australia doesn't reliably predict formality — a director in jeans is common | Assume the most senior person in the room is the best-dressed one — you'll misjudge who's in charge |
| Buy quality sneakers — they've become acceptable with tailored trousers in most non-finance offices | Overdress for a "casual Friday" — Australians treat it literally |

#### 🇳🇱 Netherlands

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Buy a good coat and comfortable shoes before anything else — most people cycle to work regardless of rank or weather | Wear anything that can't survive a bike ride, rain, or a lock on a rack |
| Default to neutral colours and quality basics — trench coats, wool jumpers, dark denim | Wear anything designed to stand out — the instinct here is to blend, not perform |
| Ask a colleague directly what the dress code is if unsure — Dutch directness makes this an easy, welcome question | Assume rank dictates formality — it's common for the most senior person in the room to be the most casually dressed |
| Keep a blazer at your desk for the rare client-facing day in finance or law | Overdress for a first day in tech, IT, or creative sectors — you'll look like you're trying too hard |
| Watch what your team actually wears before buying anything | Buy a full formal wardrobe assuming "the Netherlands" has one dress code — banking and hospitality couture are worlds apart |

### Australia: Dressing for Geography, Not Hierarchy

Australian workplace dress is less a code than a regional dialect. According to a SEEK Employer survey of Australian workers, uniform (26 percent) and smart casual (25 percent) are the two most common workplace dress standards nationally, with smart casual consistently rated the "ideal" arrangement by employees surveyed. But what counts as smart casual in Sydney — closer stylistically to American business fashion — is not what counts in Melbourne, where the sensibility leans British and more conservative, or in Brisbane and Perth, where climate does most of the deciding and brighter, lighter clothing dominates.

What's more diagnostic than the clothes themselves is what they don't signal. Australia is, per Hofstede Insights' cultural dimension scores, a relatively egalitarian, high-individualism society, and workplace hierarchy is famously loose by international standards. Dress follows suit: seniority doesn't reliably predict formality, and it isn't unusual to find a founder or director in jeans while junior staff dress up for client meetings. Younger workers, per industry surveys from Talent Hub Australia, report feeling the most pressure to look "on trend" — an anxiety senior colleagues have largely opted out of.

### Netherlands: Dressing to Disappear, Politely

Dutch fashion sensibility runs almost opposite. Business attire is described by Expatica and Holland Expat Center guidance as "fairly conservative" in aggregate but sharply segmented by industry: dark suits and white shirts in banking, open-neck shirts and jeans in IT and the arts. The unifying thread, per Dutch style commentary, is a preference for quality basics over statement pieces — trench coats, wool sweaters, tailored trousers built to last rather than to be noticed.

The Netherlands also scores 14 on Hofstede's masculinity dimension against Australia's 61, one of the largest gaps in the comparison — a data point that tracks neatly onto dress. Dutch offices reward blending over distinguishing, and the culture's famous directness extends to sartorial feedback: asking a colleague "what should I actually wear" is normal and welcomed, not a sign you don't belong. Cycling infrastructure does the rest of the work — clothing has to survive a commute by bike in all weather, which is why practicality consistently beats formality even in traditionally conservative sectors.

### The Reckoning

Here is the inversion that catches people out: in Australia, the more senior you become, the more permission you have to dress down, and it reads as confidence. In the Netherlands, the same casual dressing at the top isn't a display of confidence at all — it's an active refusal to distinguish yourself, in a culture where standing out for its own sake is mildly suspect. Both cultures end up with senior people in jeans. Only one of them is making a point by doing it.

The other quiet trap is regional assumption. Newcomers research "Australia" or "the Netherlands" as single dress codes and get burned twice: once by assuming Sydney's American-inflected style applies in Perth, and again by assuming Amsterdam's tech-casual norms apply in a Rotterdam bank. The safer heuristic in both countries is the same — dress one notch more formal than you think for week one, then watch, then match.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — An Australian who relocated to the Netherlands for a technology role wrote that the disorienting part wasn't the cold or the cycling — it was realizing that dressing well in Amsterdam meant dressing to not be noticed, the exact opposite instinct from home, where a good outfit was supposed to say something about you.*

> *r/Netherlands — One expat described buying a sharp new blazer for their first week at a Rotterdam firm, only to spend the next two months feeling overdressed next to a managing director who wore the same grey jumper on rotation, before realizing the jumper was the actual power move.*

> *Internations Amsterdam — A member of the expat community noted that the fastest way to identify a newcomer in a Dutch office wasn't the accent, it was the shoes — anything that looked like it couldn't survive a bike ride marked you as someone who hadn't yet adjusted.*

> *TogetherAbroad.nl — A contributor working in Dutch fashion and creative industries advised newcomers to treat their first week of office observation as unpaid research: watch what the most senior, most comfortable-looking person wears, and copy that, not the job posting's suggested attire.*

> *r/AskAnAmerican-adjacent expat thread — A Melbourne transplant originally from the UK noted that "business casual" in Australian job postings was functionally meaningless until they saw the actual office, since the same phrase produced a full suit at one firm and boardshorts-adjacent chinos at another three blocks away.*

### Conclusion

If you're weighing Sydney against Amsterdam, the practical takeaway is this: Australia will ask you to dress for the room you're walking into, and that room changes by city and industry more than most newcomers expect. The Netherlands will ask you to dress for the bike ride and then disappear into the crowd once you arrive. Neither is more "professional" than the other — they're solving different problems, one social and climate-driven, one cultural and allergic to hierarchy on display.

My honest advice, over a drink: pack fewer statement pieces than you think you need for Australia, and more good, boring basics than you think you need for the Netherlands. You'll be underdressed for a week either way. Better that than the alternative.
