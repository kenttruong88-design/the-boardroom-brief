# Say It With Your Chest or Say It With a Smile: China and Germany Disagree Violently on How to Tell Someone They Are Bad at Their Job

🇨🇳 China · 🇩🇪 Germany

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

In Germany, a manager will tell you, without preamble, that your presentation was weak, and will consider this an act of respect. In China, a manager will tell you your presentation was "very interesting, very hardworking, keep growing" — and the entire meaning of the sentence will live in what got left out. Both countries produce competent, functioning economies staffed by people who somehow figure out whether they are doing a good job, which suggests that "clarity" and "feedback" are not, as HR departments like to claim, the same thing.

#### 🇨🇳 China

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Read the meeting structure: praise, then a "small suggestion," then praise again — the suggestion is the point | Ask your manager to "just be straight with me" — this puts them in an impossible position |
| Take a public compliment from your boss seriously; it's a genuine signal of standing | Correct your manager's feedback in front of colleagues, even if you think it's wrong |
| Ask for clarification privately, after the meeting, one-on-one | Assume silence on a topic means there's no problem — it may mean the opposite |
| Watch your peers' body language in review meetings; group cues carry information words don't | Push for a written, itemized list of your shortcomings — it can read as confrontational |
| Build guanxi (relationship credit) with your manager before review season, not during it | Expect a Western-style 360 form to capture what actually determines your standing |

#### 🇩🇪 Germany

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Expect criticism to be about the work, not you, and respond to it as such | Interpret directness as dislike, hostility, or a prelude to being fired |
| Bring your own assessment of your weak points — Germans respect self-critique | Pad your self-review with unearned praise; it reads as evasive, not confident |
| Ask clarifying questions immediately in the room — it's expected, not rude | Get defensive or emotional in the meeting; it undermines the substance of your case |
| Treat the *Mitarbeitergespräch* (staff appraisal) as a real planning document, not a formality | Expect a compliment sandwich; the compliment and the critique arrive unwrapped |
| Follow up afterward with concrete goals in writing — Germans value the paper trail | Assume a good review means informal chat afterward; feedback and socializing stay separate |

## China

According to Hofstede Insights' country comparison, China scores 80 on Power Distance against Germany's 35, and 20 on Individualism against Germany's 67 — numbers that map almost too neatly onto how the two countries handle a bad quarter. In a high power-distance, collectivist culture, negative feedback delivered bluntly and publicly doesn't just sting; it disrupts the hierarchy and the group harmony the hierarchy is meant to protect. Managers in China, as reporting from the South China Morning Post and academic literature on Chinese workplace culture both note, tend to deliver criticism privately, indirectly, and often wrapped in a structure of praise — not out of vagueness for its own sake, but because a direct public correction can cause a subordinate to "lose face" (mianzi), a cost that outlasts the quarter it was meant to fix.

This produces a review culture where the official record and the actual message frequently diverge. A comparative academic study of performance appraisal in China's public sector found that evaluations there weight political and moral qualities alongside output in ways American-style appraisal systems don't attempt to measure, and that quantifying performance cleanly remains a persistent institutional challenge. The practical result, described consistently across accounts of Chinese office life, is that the real feedback lives in what's implied: a meeting that opens and closes with praise, with the substantive note buried in the middle, or a task quietly reassigned rather than a competency questioned aloud.

None of this means Chinese employees are worse informed about their standing — only that the information travels through relationship and context rather than through a form. Foreign hires who expect an itemized list of failures, in the manner of a Western performance improvement plan, tend to be the ones most confused come review season, having missed every signal along the way.

## Germany

Germany's approach runs on what Germans call *Sachlichkeit* — a preference for factual, depersonalized directness that treats withholding a hard truth as more disrespectful than delivering it. The Local's reporting on workplace culture describes German managers as expected to say exactly what they mean, with praise given only when genuinely earned rather than as routine morale maintenance — the opposite instinct from a culture built around softening the blow. An academic comparison of German and Brazilian banking teams, published via NCBI, found that Germany's low-context communication style changes how conflict itself functions inside teams — direct disagreement is processed as information, not as a relationship threat, which is precisely the inversion of the Chinese system.

Harvard Business Review's widely cited 2019 piece on the limits of feedback argued that criticism, however well-intentioned, often fails to produce the improvement it's aiming for — a caution that applies globally, Germany included. But German workplace norms treat that risk as worth taking anyway: better an accurate, uncomfortable assessment than a comfortable, inaccurate one. Korn Ferry's research on performance management found that 96% of employees surveyed rated real-time, ongoing feedback as more useful than an annual review alone — a preference Germany's culture is structurally well suited to satisfy, given how unremarkable it is there to hear about a problem the day it happens rather than months later in a formal sit-down.

The irony, per Gallup's State of the Global Workplace research, is that this clarity has not translated into enthusiasm: Germany has for years ranked among Western Europe's least-engaged workforces even as it maintains comparatively strong measures of overall employee wellbeing. Knowing exactly where you stand, it turns out, is not the same as being thrilled to be there.

## The Reckoning

Put the two systems side by side and the counterintuitive part isn't that they differ — it's what each culture optimizes for at the other's expense. China's system protects relationships at the cost of clarity; an employee can leave a review meeting technically informed and practically unsure whether they're about to be promoted or quietly managed out. Germany's system protects clarity at the cost of comfort; an employee leaves fully informed and, per Gallup's numbers, not obviously happier for it. Neither trade produces a workforce that feels universally well-served, which is either a damning indictment of the very concept of the performance review or simply proof that no culture has solved a problem every manager everywhere still finds excruciating.

The deeper irony is generational rather than national. Younger workers in both countries, per HR industry commentary from Korn Ferry and others, are converging on the same complaint about their own systems: China's white-collar workforce increasingly wants more explicit, actionable feedback than the harmony-preserving structure typically offers, while younger German employees, raised on more collaborative Anglo-American management writing, sometimes find their own country's bluntness excessive even by German standards. Both systems, in other words, are being quietly renegotiated by the people currently living inside them.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Blind — an engineer of Taiwanese-American background, working under mainland Chinese management, described being told in a one-on-one that he needed to "be more Chinese" and speak Mandarin to get ahead — rare directness on an indirect culture's usual terms, and feedback he had no way to act on.*

> *Quora — a respondent advising foreigners on adapting to China's indirect communication style described learning to watch for a specific meeting shape: genuine praise, a "small suggestion," then more praise — and treating the middle as the entire point of the conversation.*

> *The Local (Germany) — an American marketing hire at a German auto company described being blindsided by blunt criticism of his campaign presentation in front of the team, only to be taken to lunch by the same colleague minutes later — the moment he understood the critique wasn't personal, just procedural.*

> *Quora — in a thread on why Germans are perceived as unpolite, a respondent argued that German culture rewards open disagreement and controversy over diplomatic hedging, and that what outsiders read as rudeness is closer to a form of respect for the other person's ability to handle the truth.*

### Conclusion

The single fact worth carrying into either country is this: in China, the review meeting is not where you learn the news — it's where the news gets confirmed, if you've been reading the signals correctly all along. In Germany, the review meeting is the news, delivered once, plainly, and not repeated. Prepare accordingly, and stop expecting the other culture's meeting to feel like the one you're used to.

If a friend asked me over a drink, I'd say: in Germany, worry when the feedback stops coming, because silence there is the actual bad sign. In China, worry when the praise gets suspiciously uniform, because that's when the real message is hiding. Neither is crueler than the other — they're just lying to you in different directions.
