# German Bosses Delegate and Disappear. Korean Bosses Delegate, Then Take You to Dinner Whether You Like It or Not.

**🇩🇪 Germany · 🇰🇷 South Korea**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

A German manager hands you a task, gives precise and unambiguous instructions, and then largely leaves you alone to execute it — interference reads as a lack of trust in your competence. A Korean manager hands you a task within a rank system so granular that your job title alone signals your pay band, your seniority, and how much deference you owe the person sitting across the table — and then invites the whole team to a hoesik dinner that isn't really an invitation. Germany scores 35 on Hofstede's power distance index; South Korea scores 60. The gap shows up everywhere except, oddly, in how much either boss actually trusts you to do your job.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇩🇪 Germany

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Expect precise, unambiguous instructions from your manager, and ask for clarification upfront if anything's vague | Expect your manager to check in frequently once a task is assigned — ongoing supervision reads as distrust of your competence |
| Treat direct, blunt feedback as professional respect, not personal criticism | Take German directness personally — it's aimed at the problem, not you |
| Expect authority to be grounded in demonstrated expertise, not just title or tenure | Assume a title alone commands automatic deference — competence is what actually earns respect |
| Build a working relationship that's collegial but professionally bounded | Expect a close, informal, or emotionally warm relationship with your manager quickly — German boss-employee dynamics run more distant than many cultures |
| Collaborate across hierarchical lines when the task calls for it — Germans generally do this comfortably | Assume rigid hierarchy blocks cross-level teamwork — in practice it's more flexible than the structure suggests |

#### 🇰🇷 South Korea

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Learn the rank system (사원, 대리, 과장, 부장, 이사) and use title-plus-nim when addressing seniors | Address a senior colleague by first name, even informally, unless the company has an explicit English-name culture |
| Attend hoesik team dinners, at least some of the time — they're where real trust and relationship capital are built | Decline every hoesik invitation without ever building rapport another way — repeated declines are read as distance, fairly or not |
| Pay attention to nunchi — unspoken social cues — as seriously as verbal instructions | Assume clear verbal communication is the only channel that matters — much of what's expected is read, not said |
| Show visible deference to rank in small behavioral ways — where you sit, who pours drinks, how you greet a senior entering a room | Challenge your boss directly or publicly, even when you disagree substantively — it reads as disrespect regardless of the point's merit |
| Recognize hoesik culture is shifting toward shorter, more optional formats at many companies | Assume every company has modernized — the traditional, harder-to-opt-out-of version is still common, especially at older firms |

### Germany: Hierarchy That Delegates Trust, Not Warmth

German management style is, per cross-cultural management guides, hierarchical in structure but oriented around expertise rather than status theater: decision-making is often top-down, but authority is expected to be grounded in genuine professional competence. Once a task is delegated, the German manager largely steps back — instructions are meant to be clear, precise, and unambiguous up front, and further supervision is treated as evidence the manager doesn't trust the employee to do the job, not as diligent oversight.

This produces a boss-employee relationship that can read as cold to people from more relationally warm cultures: communication is collegial but direct and blunt, treated as a mark of professionalism and problem-solving focus rather than a personal jab. German managers are described as favoring high autonomy and high employee participation once delegation has happened — a structure that pairs a formally hierarchical org chart with a surprisingly flat, trust-based day-to-day working relationship.

### South Korea: Hierarchy as a Continuous, Legible Performance

Korean workplace hierarchy operates on an entirely different register — not primarily through org-chart authority, but through what one guide describes as "a continuous stream of small behavioral signals": how people position themselves when a senior enters a room, who pours water for whom, the exact honorific attached to a colleague's title. The rank system itself — 사원 (staff), 대리 (assistant manager), 과장 (manager), 부장 (department head), 이사 (director) — functions almost like military rank, determining pay band, seniority, and the level of deference owed, with titles used as the default form of address rather than first names.

Central to navigating this is nunchi, the ability to read social atmosphere — described by workplace guides as arguably more important than grammatically perfect Korean. And the hoesik team dinner remains, per multiple accounts, less an invitation than an expectation: relationships and trust with a boss are substantially built there, not in the office, though the format is genuinely evolving toward shorter, more optional, less alcohol-heavy gatherings at more modern companies.

### The Reckoning

The counterintuitive overlap: despite Germany's much lower Hofstede power distance score, German employees actually get less day-to-day contact with their boss once a task is assigned — delegation there means genuine hands-off autonomy. In South Korea, despite the higher formal hierarchy, employees often have more frequent, higher-touch contact with their manager, just channeled through rank-appropriate rituals like hoesik rather than casual daily check-ins.

The deeper trap for newcomers is mistaking the visible signal for the underlying trust. German hierarchy looks minimal but demands you earn autonomy through demonstrated competence before you get left alone. Korean hierarchy looks maximal but is, per current workplace commentary, actively becoming more negotiable — declining a hoesik once is usually fine, and modern managers increasingly gauge genuine inclusion over rigid compliance. Reading either system at face value, rather than watching how it actually plays out with your specific manager, is the more common mistake.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — An expat working in Germany described struggling with what they called the "strongly hierarchical nature of work relations," but clarified after more time that the hierarchy was mostly formal — their manager delegated a project and then essentially disappeared for weeks, trusting them to flag problems rather than checking in, which felt like abandonment before it started to feel like respect.*

> *Blind (TeamBlind) — A foreign employee at a large Korean company described being confused when their boss's dinner invitation was phrased as a casual suggestion but functioned, in practice, as a mandatory team event, and noted that declining once without a clear excuse quietly cost them inclusion in a later project decision.*

> *Internations — A German manager working at a Seoul subsidiary said the adjustment that took longest wasn't the language, it was learning to read unspoken cues from junior staff — nunchi — since direct German-style verbal feedback sometimes missed signals that Korean colleagues expected to be picked up without being said outright.*

> *IamExpat.de — A contributor with a low tolerance for bureaucracy admitted German hierarchy initially frustrated them, until they realized that once a task was assigned, no one checked their work at all, and the freedom that came with that trust ended up outweighing the initial formality of the structure.*

> *Quora — A Western professional working at a modernizing Korean tech firm noted that their team had switched to English first names as an official policy, but colleagues still reverted to formal titles with the "-nim" honorific whenever discussing that same person with someone senior, proof that the underlying hierarchy hadn't actually gone anywhere, just changed its vocabulary.*

### Conclusion

If you're moving from Korea to Germany, prepare for a hierarchy that looks flatter on paper but demands you prove competence before your manager backs off and leaves you alone — don't mistake that early distance for coldness, and don't wait for check-ins that won't come. If you're moving from Germany to Korea, prepare for a hierarchy that's more visible and ritualized, where the org chart and the honorifics matter more day to day, but where declining a dinner invitation occasionally is more survivable than the stereotype suggests.

My honest advice, over a drink: in Germany, earn the silence — it means you're trusted. In Korea, show up to at least some of the dinners — they mean more than the invitation lets on.
