**Germany's Generational Fight Is About Whether You're Ambitious Enough. Korea's Is About Whether You'll Show Up to Dinner.**

**🇩🇪 Germany · 🇰🇷 South Korea**

<br>

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

Germany's Institute for Employment Research found that stereotypes about Gen Z preferring private life over work are largely inaccurate — young Germans aren't job-hopping more or demanding radically different hours than previous generations. South Korea's generational rift looks nothing like that quiet non-story: it has its own vocabulary, *kkondae*, for an older figure who expects unquestioning obedience, and younger employees are openly refusing the mandatory dinners and weekend team-building that generation was raised to accept without comment. Anyone managing across generations in either country needs to know which fight they've actually walked into.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇩🇪 Germany

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Expect younger and older colleagues to broadly share views on hours and loyalty | Don't assume Gen Z German employees want radically different schedules — data says otherwise |
| Note that flexibility (remote work, scheduling) is the actual generational flashpoint | Don't mistake demand for flexibility as disinterest in career advancement |
| Respect that ambition differences (18-34 vs. 55+) are about advancement pace, not effort | Don't assume older German workers are automatically more traditional about hierarchy |
| Recognize German work culture rarely functions as a source of close friendship across any generation | Don't expect generational bonding rituals — Germans compartmentalize work and personal life broadly |
| Ask directly about scheduling expectations rather than assuming generational stereotypes | Don't lean on "Gen Z work ethic" tropes — the German data actively contradicts them |

#### 🇰🇷 South Korea

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Expect real friction over mandatory dinners, weekend team-building, and after-hours messages | Don't assume younger employees will attend optional-but-not-really-optional gatherings |
| Recognize *kkondae* as a specific, named cultural pattern — not a vague complaint | Don't dismiss junior pushback on hierarchy as simple disrespect |
| Note MZ-generation employees prioritize on-time arrival over early, face-time-driven starts | Don't schedule unpaid extra tasks assuming automatic compliance |
| Expect labor unions increasingly led by younger workers to resist longer-hours proposals | Don't assume hierarchy-based authority alone will settle a scheduling dispute |
| Treat fairness in pay and advancement as the actual currency of loyalty now | Don't expect blind loyalty in exchange for job security alone, as older generations gave |

---

Germany's generational story, on close inspection, is oddly undramatic. The Local Germany's coverage of workplace culture notes German offices generally aren't where people build close friendships regardless of age cohort — work stays compartmentalized across generations alike. Where a real gap shows up is in ambition: earlier Local Germany reporting found workers aged 18-34 more focused on career advancement than the over-55 cohort, who prioritized contentment and job security. That's a meaningful difference, but it's a difference in degree, not in the basic social contract — nobody's refusing mandatory rituals, because German work culture was never built heavily around them to begin with.

South Korea's version is structural and named. Research published in the Journal of General Management on "kkondae bullying and power distance" documents a specific pattern: older, typically male figures expecting unquestioning obedience from juniors, a dynamic long tolerated as simply how Korean hierarchy worked. KOISRA's research on the MZ generation — nearly a third of South Korea's population — describes this cohort actively rejecting forced gatherings, mandatory team dinners, and weekend team-building sessions their predecessors would have attended without protest. This isn't a preference shift at the margins; it's younger workers declining rituals that were previously treated as non-negotiable proof of commitment, while unions increasingly led by MZ voices resist proposals like extended work weeks that older labor negotiators might once have accepted.

**The Reckoning.** The surprising contrast is that Germany, often stereotyped abroad as rigidly hierarchical, has one of the quieter generational stories in this comparison — the fight is mostly about pace of ambition, not the legitimacy of the system itself. South Korea, whose corporate culture is comparatively younger in its modern form, is having the louder and more structurally disruptive argument, because the previous generation's entire model of loyalty — long hours, mandatory socializing, deference to age — is being actively renegotiated by a generation with both the numbers and the economic leverage to make it stick. If you're managing a team in Germany, budget for generational nuance. If you're managing one in South Korea, budget for generational negotiation.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *teamblind.com — A manager posting about Gen Z employees having "a bad reputation" described repeated friction over unpaid extra tasks and after-hours messages, noting younger hires increasingly declined both outright rather than quietly complying the way earlier hires had.*

> *Quora — Someone answering how different generations perceive workplace norms noted the friction rarely comes from laziness on either side, but from older employees reading boundary-setting as disengagement, and younger employees reading long-hours expectations as exploitation.*

> *Quora — A respondent explaining why young adult Germans behave differently from older generations pointed to broader shifts in education and social norms rather than any single workplace grievance, framing it as generational drift rather than active workplace conflict.*

> *The Local Germany — Coverage of Germany's aging workforce and pension pressures noted rising numbers of workers staying employed past retirement age, occasionally producing friction over scheduling and technology fluency with much younger colleagues in the same department.*

### Conclusion

If you're managing across generations in Germany, the useful move is to stop assuming a culture war and start asking concrete questions about flexibility and advancement pace — the disagreement is narrower than the stereotypes suggest. If you're managing in South Korea, prepare for something closer to an actual negotiation over what loyalty even means now, because the MZ generation isn't asking for better snacks in the break room — they're declining the entire ritual structure their bosses were promoted for enduring. The line I'd give a friend: in Germany, the generations mostly agree on the rules and argue about the pace; in Korea, they're rewriting the rules in real time.
