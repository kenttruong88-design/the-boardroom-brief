# Yes, Chef: How France Guards Its Hierarchy While Australia Just Calls the Boss Dave

🇫🇷 France · 🇦🇺 Australia

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

In France, an employee will spend part of a legally capped 35-hour week working out exactly how to address their manager — *vous*, never *tu*, without an invitation — while in Australia the chief executive's first name is common property by lunchtime on day one. According to Hofstede Insights' country comparison tool, France scores 68 on the power distance index against Australia's 36, a gap wide enough that an org chart drawn in Paris looks faintly aristocratic next to one drawn in Melbourne. Both countries run the same basic transaction — an employee taking direction from a superior — through entirely different operating systems, and only one is willing to admit, out loud, that a hierarchy exists at all.

| Defend your opinion when questioned; silence reads as having nothing to add | Don't arrive late to a meeting with a superior — punctuality signals rank |
| Expect a fixed daily rhythm: coffee break, lunch window, coffee break, home | Don't assume 35 hours means a relaxed pace — the structure is rigid |

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Use your manager's first name from day one, up to the CEO | Don't mistake informal address for equal say in the final decision |
| Speak up in meetings regardless of seniority | Don't visibly self-promote; tall poppy syndrome punishes bragging fast |
| Match the office's casual, self-deprecating humour | Don't assume a title-free culture means titles carry no weight upstairs |

### France

France's workplace hierarchy is less a cultural accident than a designed feature. Wages remain closely tied to educational pedigree, seniority and formal position, and the distance between manager and managed is meant to be visible rather than smoothed over. The Local's survey of foreign executives working in France found that 67 percent used the word "hierarchy" unprompted to describe French management, while others reached for "authoritarianism" and a persistent "glass ceiling" that non-French staff struggle to see past. None of this is coded as a national embarrassment; it is closer to a settled operating assumption.

Yet the formality cuts both ways. According to Gallup's State of the Global Workplace research, 93 percent of French workers say they are not willing to offer discretionary effort beyond what their role strictly requires — not for lack of debate (French employees are, by most accounts, unusually willing to argue with a superior to their face) but because effort is understood as a transaction bounded by contract, not a gift extended on faith. The OECD's statutory 35-hour week, generous *congés payés*, and the rigid CDI/CDD contract system reinforce the same logic: hierarchy grants the boss authority, and in exchange the employee is owed clearly defined limits the boss is not supposed to erode.

### Australia

Australia runs the opposite experiment. Cultural Atlas and Global Business Culture both describe an explicitly egalitarian environment in which respect attaches to the person and their contribution rather than the position, first names are used in essentially all business contexts, and managers actively cultivate an "approachable" image rather than a commanding one. The ABS's Employee Earnings and Hours release puts average full-time hours at 38.5 a week — longer, on paper, than the French legal maximum, achieved with none of the formal apparatus that would make a French employee feel the hierarchy pressing on them.

Gallup's data suggests the informality is not merely cosmetic: Australia posts lower active-disengagement figures than the global average and leads on measures of workers "thriving" in their broader life evaluation, consistent with a workplace where authority is meant to be earned and challenged rather than simply obeyed. The trade-off, largely invisible in the official statistics, is that a flat-looking structure still has a top, and nobody is required to tell you where it is.

### The Reckoning

Here is the first irony: France's visible, almost theatrical hierarchy comes bundled with some of the strongest formal protections a worker can have — a capped week, defended leave, a right to disconnect, and a contract system that makes the boundaries of the job explicit. Australia's egalitarian, first-name, mate-to-mate culture, ostensibly the friendlier system, offers no equivalent formal ceiling; the authority is real, simply undocumented, which makes it harder to push back against something you were told doesn't officially exist.

The second irony follows: French employees, protected by contract, feel entitled to withhold discretionary effort — hence Gallup's 93 percent figure — while Australians, who owe their boss nothing more than a first name, routinely donate unpaid overtime to a workplace that insists it has no hierarchy to please. Formality is not the enemy of the worker; ambiguity might be.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Reddit, r/australia — a corporate tax professional who worked across Australia, London, Frankfurt and New York recalled a London manager who said he preferred hiring Australians and Kiwis over locals; the writer warned that Australians rarely notice themselves crossing the line from occasional extra hours to it quietly becoming the expectation.*

> *Quora, on a thread about how Western employees address their superiors — a respondent noted that first-naming a boss is common across most English-speaking countries, but says nothing about how much real influence a junior employee has, a distinction newcomers to egalitarian-looking offices often miss.*

> *The Local (thelocal.fr) — foreign executives working in French firms described a persistent "glass ceiling" keeping non-French staff from senior roles, alongside management habits some called authoritarian, even as they conceded the directness meant they always knew exactly where they stood.*

> *Expat.com forum — an American who spent seven years working in southwest France described its fixed rhythm (arrive on time, two coffee breaks, lunch strictly noon to two, leave on time) and how a title bought real social standing; a French commenter pushed back, saying that after nearly fifteen years working there, some jobs left barely three minutes free for the toilet.*

> *ExpatForum.com, a thread titled "French bosses are weird" — a poster explained that calling a boss by their first name has become trendy in French offices, but switching to the informal *tu* is still rare, since deference in France attaches to the role someone holds rather than the person filling it.*

### Conclusion

The practical difference that matters most to anyone relocating is not who you're allowed to call by their first name — it's who is actually protecting your evenings. In France, the hierarchy is loud, visible, and comes with paperwork on your side; you'll spend a few uncomfortable months mastering *vous* and lunch timing, but the 35-hour week and your *congés* are not up for negotiation. In Australia, the hierarchy is quiet, informal, and largely on the house's side; nobody will make you call anyone "sir," but nobody has promised you'll clock off at a sensible hour either.

If a friend asked me which one to take a job in, I'd say: pick France if you want to know exactly where you stand and don't mind being told so formally; pick Australia if you'd rather be liked by your boss than protected from him — just don't confuse the two for the same thing.
