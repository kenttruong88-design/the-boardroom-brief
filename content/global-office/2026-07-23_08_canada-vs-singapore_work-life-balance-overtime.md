**Canada Clocks Out at Five. Singapore Waits for the Boss to Leave First. Guess Which One Has a Word for It.**

**🇨🇦 Canada · 🇸🇬 Singapore**

***By Priya Mehta, The Global Office***

Singapore has a term, "wayang," borrowed from Malay theatre, for the performance of staying late at your desk after the actual work is done — a national tradition of looking busy for an audience of one departing manager. Canada, by contrast, has Statistics Canada dutifully reporting that just 3 percent of its employees work very long hours, a figure so unremarkable that nobody has bothered inventing a word for it. One country has built an entire etiquette around the appearance of overtime; the other has mostly just declined to have any. Both call themselves competitive economies, which should tell you that "hard work" and "long hours" are not, whatever your last manager implied, the same thing.

### Do's & Don'ts — First Weeks on the Job

#### 🇨🇦 Canada

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Leave at or near your contracted hours as a matter of course | Assume staying late signals ambition — it mostly signals poor planning |
| Use your vacation days; colleagues genuinely expect you to | Feel obligated to check email on evenings or weekends by default |
| Raise workload concerns directly with your manager — it's an accepted conversation | Wait to be told your hours are unreasonable; nobody will volunteer that for you |
| Expect meetings to start and end on time | Schedule "quick syncs" that run over — it reads as disorganised, not dedicated |
| Negotiate flexible or hybrid arrangements openly; it's now standard practice | Assume remote-work norms from 2021 still apply everywhere without checking |
| Take short-term illness or mental health days without over-explaining | Treat a sick day as something that requires a doctor's note and an apology |

#### 🇸🇬 Singapore

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Notice what time your manager and senior colleagues leave before you do | Be first out the door in your first few weeks, regardless of workload |
| Expect after-hours WhatsApp or email messages from some employers | Assume silence after 6 p.m. is guaranteed, even where it's legally encouraged |
| Clarify your contracted hours and overtime eligibility before you sign | Assume your "expected hours" on paper reflect actual office norms |
| Observe how colleagues use annual leave before booking large blocks yourself | Book extended leave without checking how it's typically staggered on your team |
| Ask directly about a company's real culture during interviews — it's expected | Take a glossy careers page at face value; ask current employees instead |
| Build relationships with teammates from different national backgrounds early | Assume every "Singapore office" runs on the same norms — they vary sharply by ownership |

### Canada: The Quietly Unremarkable Option

Canada's work-life balance case doesn't rest on generous vacation law — it can't. According to comparative OECD leave data, Canadian workers get roughly 10 statutory paid vacation days plus 9 public holidays, tying for near-last place among 21 OECD countries and trailing France's 30 days by a wide margin. What Canada offers instead is cultural restraint around hours actually worked. Statistics Canada's most recent Survey on Working Conditions found only 3 percent of employees logging very long hours, against an OECD average of 10 percent, and the OECD's broader work-life balance ranking placed Canada fifth globally. A full-time employee averages about 38.8 hours a week, a figure that has barely moved since 1980.

That doesn't mean the picture is uniformly rosy: Statistics Canada also reports that roughly one in three Canadians say work demands interfere with home and family life, and unpaid overtime persists in specific sectors — nearly two-thirds of workers in natural resources and agriculture report regularly working over 10-hour days. But the default cultural expectation, reinforced by relatively strict provincial employment standards, is that five o'clock means five o'clock, and that nobody is silently judging you for believing it.

### Singapore: Efficient, Ambitious, and Allergic to Being First to Leave

Singapore's numbers tell a starker story. MOM (Singapore's Ministry of Manpower) reported average usual weekly hours falling to 41.4 in 2025 — itself a improvement on prior decades — but that official figure understates the lived reality. ADP Research Institute survey data finds Singaporean workers reporting an average of 8.19 unpaid hours weekly, the second-highest figure in the Asia-Pacific region after India, and roughly 40 percent of workers say they routinely put in up to 10 hours of unpaid time. Presenteeism researchers and local outlets have documented the specific mechanism: staying visibly at your desk after your work is finished, waiting for a manager to leave first, a habit widely tied to Confucian-influenced norms around visible diligence as a marker of loyalty.

Hofstede Insights data offers a partial explanation: Singapore scores notably low on individualism relative to Canada (a dimension where personal boundaries carry less automatic social weight than group harmony and hierarchy), which helps explain why declining unpaid extra hours can feel, to many Singaporean employees, less like asserting a right and more like a small act of defection from the team. To its credit, the city-state's Tripartite Alliance has pushed overtime compensation rules — capped at 72 hours a month, paid at 150 percent — and there are visible signs of pushback, including reports of workers increasingly logging off at 6 p.m. on principle. But the base expectation, especially at multinational offices with headquarters cultures imported wholesale, still runs considerably hotter than Canada's.

### The Reckoning

The paradox worth sitting with: Canada's legal protections for time off are thin by rich-world standards, yet its lived culture treats reasonable hours as the unremarkable default. Singapore's legal protections are, on paper, reasonably firm — overtime caps, mandated compensation, an active labour ministry — yet its lived culture routinely produces hours well beyond what the law anticipates, because the pressure isn't legal, it's social. This is the trap of judging a country's work-life balance by statute alone: law describes the floor, not the temperature of the room.

For a relocating professional, that means the more useful diagnostic question isn't "what does the employment law say" but "what does my prospective manager do at 6:15 p.m." In Canada, that answer is almost certainly: goes home. In Singapore, the honest answer is: it depends entirely on whether the office in question is Singaporean, American, Japanese, or mainland Chinese in its operating culture — a distinction serious enough to be worth asking about directly in the interview, which, refreshingly, is considered completely normal to do.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

*Blind (work-life balance in Canadian offices thread) — a Bay Area tech veteran considering a move back to Toronto was told bluntly by other Canadians in tech: expect 40 to 60 percent of Bay Area compensation for a modest, not dramatic, reduction in hours — people work "a bit less hard," not two or three hours a day, and ambitious workers still gravitate toward US-headquartered employers operating out of Canada rather than domestic firms.*

*Blind (Bytedance Singapore culture thread) — a poster researching offers at Chinese tech firms' Singapore branches was told the notorious "996" schedule is generally reserved for teams reporting directly into China, while local Singapore teams keep more standard hours — though contributors flagged that Japanese-headquartered employers in the same city had a reputation for some of the worst conditions of all.*

*Quora (what is Singaporean work culture like) — a respondent described the office functioning as something close to a second home for many Singaporean employees, with a strong, largely unquestioned expectation that the job simply gets done regardless of the hour, framing long hours less as exploitation than as an accepted baseline.*

*Quora (how does Canada do in terms of work-life balance) — a contributor noted that Canadian employers broadly hold to the standard 40-hour week across sectors, and that family members working in Canada consistently reported having genuinely sufficient time at home, a contrast the poster drew explicitly against experiences in Singapore, India, and the US.*

*Blind (Singapore relocation tips thread) — a newcomer weighing a Singapore fintech offer was warned by several respondents to visit before committing, with more than one contributor stressing that laws in Singapore are actually enforced in ways newcomers from more lax jurisdictions tend to underestimate — advice offered about daily life generally, but one that colours workplace formality too.*

### Conclusion

If your priority is a predictable evening, Canada wins on cultural default even though its statutory leave is stingy by European standards — you are simply less likely to be the last one in the office wondering whether that's a problem. If your priority is maximizing income and career velocity, especially in finance or tech, Singapore's proximity to Asian markets and lighter tax burden are real, but they come bundled with a workplace culture where visible hours still function as a loyalty signal in a meaningful share of employers, official statistics notwithstanding. Ask about actual departure times before you ask about the ping-pong table.

If a friend asked me over a drink: move to Canada if you want your evenings back by default, move to Singapore if you're prepared to negotiate for them, company by company, manager by manager — and don't expect the employment contract to do that negotiating for you.
