# Germany Negotiates Home Office, China Still Wants to See You at 9pm

**🇩🇪 Germany · 🇨🇳 China**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

A German employee threatening to quit over lost remote-work flexibility is, per a widely cited Continental survey, one of nearly half the country's workforce making the same threat — a genuinely mainstream position, not a fringe complaint. A Chinese tech employee threatening the same thing over a return-to-office mandate is having a far more marginal conversation; some AI startups in China have been reported requiring a six-day office week even as the country's Supreme People's Court has, on paper, ruled the notorious 996 schedule illegal since 2021. Both countries have hybrid work. They disagree almost completely about how much leverage the employee actually has in that negotiation.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇩🇪 Germany

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Request mobile work directly with your employer — there's no formal law against it | Assume remote work is a legal entitlement; it's still discretionary, though widely granted |
| Expect roughly 1.4 days a week at home to be a realistic norm in office-based roles | Assume every sector offers the same flexibility — healthcare and manufacturing lag far behind IT |
| Negotiate hybrid terms explicitly during hiring — it's now a standard conversation | Treat a fully remote arrangement as guaranteed; hybrid has become the dominant model |
| Use remote flexibility as genuine leverage — a large share of employees would quit without it | Skip in-person days without checking team expectations first |
| Respect scheduled in-office days as real commitments, not optional | Assume flexibility means unstructured — most German hybrid setups are clearly defined |

#### 🇨🇳 China

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Clarify your company's actual office-presence expectations before assuming flexibility | Assume a stated hybrid policy protects you from informal pressure to be visible |
| Understand that legal limits on hours are inconsistently enforced, especially in tech | Rely on labor law alone to guarantee reasonable hours — enforcement is uneven |
| Recognize that results-oriented flexibility is growing, especially post-pandemic | Assume flexibility has become the norm nationally — it varies enormously by company and sector |
| Ask directly and early about a specific employer's remote culture, not the national trend | Take a job assuming remote-friendliness without confirming it with your actual team |
| Expect younger colleagues and Gen Z hires to be actively pushing for more balance | Dismiss generational pressure for flexibility as marginal — it's reshaping expectations fast |

---

Germany's remote-work culture has shifted decisively even without a comprehensive legal mandate behind it. Statista's tracking shows the share of German employees working from home at least occasionally nearly doubled from 2019 to 2024, and while fully remote work has receded from its pandemic peak, hybrid models now dominate at an average of about 1.4 days a week from home. Crucially, this remains a voluntary employer decision rather than a legal right — Germany has no formal statute mandating remote work — yet the cultural expectation has hardened into something close to one anyway: a widely cited Continental survey found 47% of German employees would consider quitting if remote flexibility were significantly reduced. Industry variation is stark, with IT and administrative roles offering the flexibility around 70-75% of the time, against near zero in manufacturing and trades.

China's picture is messier and more sector-dependent. The country's legal workweek is capped at 40 hours with mandated overtime pay beyond that, and the Supreme People's Court formally struck down 996 as illegal in 2021 — but reporting from Foreign Policy and other outlets describes labor law enforcement as inconsistent at best, particularly for lower-status workers, and Statista data shows urban Chinese employees still average well above 48 hours a week. Remote work did expand meaningfully during the pandemic and hybrid arrangements have persisted in its wake, but Western tech employers operating in China frequently treat the country as an exception to global remote policies altogether, citing data-security and IP concerns, while some domestic AI startups have reportedly reinstated demanding six-day office weeks even as flexibility trends elsewhere in the economy move the opposite direction.

The reckoning is that Germany's remote-work culture rests on genuine employee leverage even without much legal backing, while China's rests on inconsistent, employer-by-employer discretion even where the law is technically on the worker's side. In Germany, the threat of losing your best people is enough to keep flexibility broadly protected as a cultural norm. In China, the gap between what labor law promises and what gets enforced means flexibility depends far more on which specific company and sector you land in than on any national baseline you can count on. A remote-friendly job offer means something close to guaranteed in Germany; in China, it means you still need to ask the team, not just the recruiter.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — Someone considering a German remote arrangement while living abroad asked detailed questions about tax residency and contract structure, and the most useful responses noted that German companies are increasingly comfortable with flexible arrangements, but formalizing the tax and visa side takes real, deliberate paperwork most newcomers underestimate.*

> *teamblind.com — A tech worker researching remote-work-friendly companies noted that China is treated as a structural exception even at firms with otherwise global remote policies, citing IP-theft and data-security concerns as the recurring justification rather than anything about the employee's actual role.*

> *teamblind.com — Another contributor mentioned a colleague working full-time from China for a US-headquartered company, but noted their compensation had been quietly adjusted down to match local Chinese-office pay scales — a reminder that remote flexibility and pay parity don't necessarily travel together.*

> *Continental survey (via timedoctor.com) — Reporting on the German workforce data behind the 47% figure framed it not as a fringe preference but as a mainstream expectation now baked into how German employees evaluate job offers, comparable in weight to salary itself for many respondents.*

> *Quora — A professional working remotely for a German employer while living outside the country described German management as notably comfortable trusting output over visible hours, contrasting it favorably against previous experience with a US employer they felt tracked activity far more closely.*

### Conclusion

The practical move is to verify rather than assume. In Germany, remote flexibility is close enough to a given that you can negotiate it directly and expect it to hold — the leverage is real and broadly shared. In China, treat national trend pieces as background noise and get specific: ask the team you'd actually be joining what their real expectations are, because the law and the lived reality frequently diverge. If a friend asked me over drinks, I'd say: in Berlin, ask for the home office days — you'll probably get them. In Shanghai, ask the same question twice, to two different people, before you believe the answer.
