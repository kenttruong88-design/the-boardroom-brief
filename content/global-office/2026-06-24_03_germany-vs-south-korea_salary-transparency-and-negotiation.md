# Show Me the Money (or Don't): Germany and South Korea on Salary Transparency

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

In Germany, nearly half of employees now discuss their salaries with colleagues — a development so noteworthy that it was reported as news, which tells you something about the baseline from which they started. In South Korea, most large employers set salaries by formula, which means negotiation is not so much a cultural norm as a mild conceptual confusion. Two economies, two approaches to the information that arguably matters most in the employer-employee relationship, and two very different results for the people who happen to be women.

## Germany: Transparency Arrives, Cautiously and With Paperwork

Germany's approach to salary transparency is characterised by the same quality as most German policy initiatives: thoroughness applied in measured increments. The Entgelttransparenzgesetz, or Pay Transparency Act, has since 2017 given employees at companies with more than 200 staff the right to request information about how their compensation compares to colleagues in equivalent roles. Companies with more than 500 employees must report regularly on equal pay measures — every five years if they have collective bargaining agreements, every three years if they don't.

The results of this regulatory framework have been, by official measures, positive and, by employee experience, somewhat uneven. A 2024 survey by Figures.hr found that 47% of German employees now discuss salary with colleagues more frequently than two years prior — a meaningful shift in a country where salary secrecy was, for decades, considered a professional norm roughly on par with not commenting on a colleague's weight. The cultural loosening is real. It is just slow.

What Germany's transparency framework has not solved is the gender pay gap, which the Federal Statistical Office places at 18% — meaning women earn on average €0.18 less per hour than men. This figure, adjusted for sector, hours, and qualification differences, narrows but does not disappear. The gap is smaller in eastern Germany than in western Germany, a legacy of the GDR's different approach to women in the workforce. Germany's new pay transparency regulations, strengthened in recent years, now require gender-differentiated salary reporting, with the stated goal of making the gap visible enough to become politically unsustainable. Whether visibility translates to change remains, as always, the open question.

Salary negotiation in Germany, where it occurs, tends toward the direct and evidence-based. Candidates are expected to know their market rate and state it clearly — the circling around the topic common in British or American interviews is not culturally native here. Asking for more money is not considered aggressive; arriving without a number is considered underprepared.

## South Korea: The Formula Decides

South Korea operates on a different philosophical axis entirely. At most large Korean companies — the chaebols that form the backbone of the economy, and the large public-sector institutions that employ a significant share of the workforce — salaries are determined by a standardised formula incorporating seniority, education level, and years of service. The formula does not negotiate. You do not negotiate with the formula. The formula simply is.

This produces a workplace where salary conversations between colleagues are unusual, individual negotiation is rare outside senior roles, and career progression is tied less to skill demonstration than to time served and credentials held. Starting salaries vary by company tier and university prestige in ways that are well-understood by Korean job-seekers before they apply. The information asymmetry that salary transparency advocates rail against in Western contexts is, in Korea, partially replaced by a different kind of predictability — you know roughly what you will earn, not because salaries are published but because the formula is an open secret everyone has done the maths on.

The cost of this system is significant and sits almost entirely with Korean women. South Korea recorded the largest gender pay gap among all OECD member states in 2024, with women earning 29% less than men — a figure that has barely moved in a decade. In 2024, 23.8% of female workers in Korea were classified as low-wage earners, compared to 11.1% of men. The OECD has repeatedly flagged pay transparency and stronger anti-discrimination enforcement as priority interventions. Korea has begun discussing them, at roughly the pace Germany began discussing pay transparency reforms fifteen years ago.

## The Reckoning: When Knowing Isn't Half the Battle

The comparison illuminates a paradox that salary transparency advocates sometimes gloss over. Germany has significantly more formal transparency infrastructure than South Korea — legally mandated reporting, individual rights to comparative information, an evolving cultural norm around discussing pay — and still has an 18% gender pay gap. South Korea has a rigid formula-based system with minimal transparency and a 29% gap. More transparency helps, but transparency alone, in the absence of structural reform to how careers are structured for women, does not close the gap.

One thread in r/germany captured the German experience with characteristic wryness: "We now talk about salaries openly. We have now found out we are all underpaid." One commenter from Seoul, in a separate r/korea discussion, noted that the formula at least had the virtue of removing the anxiety of negotiation — "you know what you will be paid, you just can't change it," which may be the definition of a stable but inflexible system.

## Priya's Take

Germany and South Korea represent two distinct failure modes in salary culture: one where information exists but structural inequity persists, and one where structure exists but information is suppressed. The most effective systems appear to need both — transparency about what people earn and flexibility about what they can earn. Neither country has fully managed this combination. Both have, however, produced enough data on the problem to keep a generation of labour economists busy.
