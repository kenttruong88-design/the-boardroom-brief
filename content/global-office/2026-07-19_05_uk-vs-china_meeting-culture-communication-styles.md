# A Meeting in London Ends When the Clock Says So. A Meeting in Shanghai Ends When the Senior Person Stops Talking.

🇬🇧 UK · 🇨🇳 China

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

In London, a meeting scheduled for thirty minutes will end in twenty-nine, because someone has another meeting, and the fear of being seen to overrun is a stronger cultural force than whatever was actually being decided. In Shanghai, a meeting scheduled for thirty minutes may end whenever the most senior person present has finished speaking, which could be in ten minutes or ninety, and questioning the discrepancy is itself the faux pas. Both countries call this "having a meeting." Both would be baffled to watch the other's version.

#### 🇬🇧 UK

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Send an agenda in advance and stick roughly to it | Assume "that's an interesting idea" is praise — it usually isn't |
| Arrive on time, even if the meeting itself starts loosely | Interrupt; wait for the pause, however long it takes |
| Decode qualifiers like "perhaps" or "I wonder if" as firm positions | Take silence after a proposal as agreement |
| Use self-deprecating humour to soften disagreement | Push for an immediate yes/no answer in the room |
| Follow up in writing to confirm what was actually decided | Assume the loudest voice in the room has the most authority |

#### 🇨🇳 China

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Greet and seat people in order of seniority | Address a junior colleague's idea before the senior one has spoken |
| Expect small talk and tea before the agenda starts | Rush straight to business — it reads as transactional and rude |
| Treat "we will consider it" as a likely no | Assume a nod or "yes" during the meeting means final agreement |
| Direct proposals to the most senior person first | Publicly contradict a superior, even when they are wrong |
| Accept that real decisions may be confirmed after the meeting, not during it | Expect the meeting itself to be where the decision gets made |

### UK: The Art of Meaning No While Saying Perhaps

British meeting culture runs on a paradox: it looks informal — flat-ish hierarchies, first names, self-deprecating jokes — while actually encoding a dense set of indirect signals that outsiders routinely miss. According to cultural communication research summarised by Commisceo Global and the Cultural Atlas, British professionals prioritise diplomacy and emotional restraint over explicit statement, softening disagreement through qualification, understatement and humour rather than direct pushback. The phrase "that's an interesting perspective" is not, in practice, praise; it is frequently the polite wrapper around "this will not work and we need another plan." Understatement functions as a signal, not a hedge — "not ideal" means genuinely bad.

This indirectness coexists with a genuine institutional preference for structure. The ONS reports that by October 2025, 85% of UK job postings requiring hybrid arrangements specified at least two in-office days a week, with two-day patterns the most common single arrangement — meetings, in other words, are increasingly something people travel in specifically to attend, which raises the social cost of them running long or achieving nothing. Hofstede Insights scores the UK at 35 on Power Distance, reflecting a workplace culture where subordinates are nominally expected to challenge superiors and hierarchy is downplayed even when it quietly persists in who actually gets listened to.

The result is a meeting culture that performs egalitarianism while running on a code only the initiated can read. Gallup's 2026 State of the Global Workplace notes UK engagement sitting well below the North American benchmark of 31%, a gap some occupational researchers attribute partly to meeting fatigue and the ambiguity of outcomes — nobody is quite sure what was agreed, because nobody said it plainly.

### China: The Meeting as Ritual, the Corridor as Where Business Happens

Chinese meetings operate on what communication researchers, per Darden's Ideas to Action and multiple cross-cultural business guides, describe as a high-context model: shared understanding, non-verbal cues and relationship history carry as much weight as spoken content. Hofstede Insights puts China's Power Distance score at 80 against the UK's 35 — among the largest single gaps in the standard six-dimension comparison — meaning hierarchy is not merely present but structurally load-bearing. Entry into the room, seating order and who speaks first all follow rank; the senior figure is greeted and addressed before anyone else, and junior employees are not expected to have opinions that outrank their position.

Guanxi — the accumulated web of relationship and reciprocal obligation — means the meeting itself is frequently ceremonial, confirming a decision reached elsewhere: over tea beforehand, at a dinner, or in a private conversation between principals. Business etiquette guides note that Chinese counterparts often require internal consensus before committing, and that pressuring for an immediate answer is counterproductive. Meanwhile, the SCMP has tracked years of controversy over "996" culture — 9am to 9pm, six days a week — a norm the Supreme People's Court declared illegal in 2021 but which, per reporting through 2025 into 2026, persists informally in the expectation that employees remain visibly present until superiors log off, meetings included. China's Long-Term Orientation score of 87 (versus the UK's 51) reflects a broader institutional patience with process that Western counterparts often misread as delay.

### The Reckoning

Put the two side by side and the irony sharpens: the culture that prizes explicit agendas and punctual starts (the UK) is also the one where almost nothing said in the room can be taken at face value, while the culture stereotyped by outsiders as opaque and indirect (China) is, within its own hierarchy, remarkably clear about who has authority and what a nod does and does not commit you to. British ambiguity is linguistic; Chinese ambiguity, where it exists, is structural — located in the gap between the meeting and the decision, not in the words used inside it.

Both systems, from the outside, look inefficient. Both participants would insist, correctly, that their system produces exactly the outcome intended — it merely front-loads the negotiation (China, before the meeting) or back-loads the decoding (UK, after it). The genuinely global manager's task is not choosing which style is "more honest" but recognising that both are honest by their own internal logic, and that a British "perhaps" and a Chinese "we'll consider it" are, functionally, cousins.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Blind — a poster in a tech-industry thread noted that Chinese-born managers consistently joined video calls five or more minutes after the scheduled start, every single meeting, a pattern other commenters debated as cultural habit versus a deliberate assertion of seniority.*

> *Blind — someone describing life under a Chinese manager warned that a "yes" in a meeting often just means "I understand you," not agreement, and that silence after a proposal should be read as caution or quiet disagreement rather than consent — a distinction that cost them a project timeline before they learned it.*

> *Quora — a respondent answering a question on British office etiquette flagged the opposite culture shock for newcomers from more formal environments: it wasn't the indirectness that surprised them, it was the casual swearing colleagues dropped mid-meeting without anyone reacting.*

> *Quora — someone recounting their experience doing business with Chinese counterparts as a foreigner advised that patience with the internal consultation process mattered more than negotiating skill, since decisions routinely required consensus among people who were never actually in the room.*

### Conclusion

The single distinction that matters most for anyone parachuted into either system is this: in the UK, the meeting is where opinions get filtered through politeness on their way to a decision that was probably already forming; in China, the meeting is where a decision — already substantially formed elsewhere — gets ratified through hierarchy. Mistake one for the other and you will either push too hard for an answer that was never going to arrive in the room (China) or take a polite non-answer as genuine openness (UK).

If a friend were heading to either posting, the honest advice over a drink would be this: in London, listen for what isn't quite being said; in Shanghai, watch who is being deferred to and worry less about what's being said at all. Neither is more forthright than the other — they've just moved the honesty to different parts of the process.
