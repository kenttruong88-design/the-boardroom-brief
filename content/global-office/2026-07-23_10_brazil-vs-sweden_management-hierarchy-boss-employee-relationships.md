# Brazil vs. Sweden: The Boss Who Decides Everything and the Boss Who Insists You Decide Together

🇧🇷 **Brazil** · 🇸🇪 **Sweden**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

In a São Paulo office, an employee who publicly questions the boss's decision in a meeting has committed something close to a social offence — the correction, if it comes at all, happens quietly afterward, over coffee, without witnesses. In a Stockholm office, an employee who does not question the boss's decision in a meeting has usually misunderstood the assignment — silence is not taken as agreement so much as an absence of the input everyone was explicitly invited to provide. Brazil scores 69 on Hofstede's power distance index; Sweden scores 31. Numbers rarely translate this cleanly into daily behaviour, but in this case they do: one culture built its org chart around the boss, the other built its org chart to make the boss as invisible as politely possible. Anyone weighing a move between the two is really choosing between two entirely different definitions of what a manager is for.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇧🇷 Brazil

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Invest time in small talk and personal rapport with your boss before getting to business | Treat the relationship with your manager as purely transactional; trust here is personal before it is professional |
| Follow instructions as given, even if you have reservations, and raise concerns afterward, privately | Question or contradict your boss's decision openly in a meeting, even diplomatically |
| Expect your boss to take a personal interest in your life outside work — this is normal, not intrusive | Interpret a paternalistic, hands-on manager as a lack of trust in your competence |
| Show loyalty to your direct manager, not just the company; relationships often outlast org charts | Assume a decision reached by consensus in a meeting is final — a senior director can still overrule it later |
| Use formal titles and last names until your boss signals it's appropriate to switch | Address a senior manager too casually before that signal has been given |
| Accept that hierarchy is generally stable and visible; it tells you where you stand | Try to bypass your direct manager to get decisions made faster; it reads as disrespect, not initiative |

#### 🇸🇪 Sweden

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Use your manager's first name from day one, regardless of their seniority | Use titles, formal address, or defer to rank in ways that signal you expect hierarchy |
| Offer your opinion in meetings, even to senior staff — it is expected, not tolerated | Wait to be asked before speaking up; silence is read as disengagement, not respect |
| Expect decisions to take longer, because consensus is genuinely being built, not performed | Push for a fast, top-down call; it will likely be seen as skipping a step everyone else values |
| Approach your manager as a resource and coach, not an authority to be managed carefully | Expect your manager to simply tell you what to do; that's viewed as a failure of your own initiative |
| Take fika (the daily coffee break) seriously as a genuine, hierarchy-flattening ritual | Skip it to keep working; it can read as opting out of the team, not as diligence |
| Commit fully to a decision once consensus is reached, even if you privately still have reservations | Continue re-litigating a decision in later meetings after the group has already agreed to move forward |

### Brazil: The Manager as Benevolent Authority

Brazilian management style is often described, accurately, as paternalistic — a term that in most Western business contexts carries some baggage, but in Brazil describes something closer to a functioning social contract. Per Commisceo Global's management guide, successful Brazilian executives are expected to combine clear, top-down direction with genuine personal interest in their employees' wellbeing; the boss who asks about your family and remembers your child's name is not overstepping, he is doing the job correctly. The Cultural Atlas notes that subordinates in Brazilian workplaces are often more loyal to their direct manager than to the organisation itself — a distinction that matters enormously when managers change jobs and staff quietly follow them.

None of this softens the hierarchy itself. Brazil's power distance score of 69 shows up in concrete behaviour: instructions are carried out without extended debate, and any debate happens privately rather than in front of the group, to avoid undermining the boss publicly. Decision-making concentrates at the top, and — a detail that regularly catches newcomers off guard — a consensus reached in a meeting is not necessarily binding; a senior director can revisit and overturn it later, and the team absorbs the change without treating it as a betrayal. The standard workweek, codified under Brazil's CLT labour code, runs 44 hours, and within it, knowing exactly who holds the authority to make a call is treated as basic professional literacy, not office politics.

### Sweden: The Manager as Facilitator

Sweden inverts nearly every one of these assumptions. Commisceo Global's Sweden management guide describes local managers as coaches rather than commanders — present to remove obstacles and support the team, not to issue instructions from on high. Any employee, in principle, can walk into the managing director's office; positions of authority are earned through demonstrated competence rather than granted permanent deference once achieved, and displays of status are, if anything, quietly discouraged. Statistics Sweden puts actual average working hours for adults aged 25 to 54 at 31.1 hours a week in 2023 — well under the country's own 40-hour statutory standard, and a reminder that Swedish workplace culture treats results, not hours logged or rank held, as the real currency.

The mechanism for all this is consensus, and it is not a courtesy — it is the actual decision-making process. Accounts of Swedish corporate culture describe input genuinely sought from every level before a decision is finalised, taking longer than a directive from the top but producing buy-in a directive rarely does. Fika, the daily coffee break, plays a structural role too: a deliberately hierarchy-flattening ritual where a new graduate and the managing director have, for fifteen minutes, the same conversation about the weather. Edelman's 2025 Trust Barometer put trust in employers at roughly 74 percent in Sweden — a figure a flatter, consultative management style plausibly helps sustain, even as it makes decisions slower than outsiders expect.

### The Reckoning

The clearest illustration of the gap between these two systems comes from Brazilian managers who have actually made the move to Sweden. One documented case describes a Brazilian project manager, accustomed to a directive style where decisions from above were followed without extended discussion, taking over a Swedish team and finding her authority functionally dissolved: staff openly questioned her proposals in meetings, carried out tasks their own way without checking in first, and kept offering opinions even after she had announced a decision, as though the matter were still open. When she raised the friction with her own Swedish manager, he did not step in with directions — he coached her toward finding her own solution, which was, in itself, a second and more disorienting lesson in how Swedish authority actually functions.

The counterintuitive part is that both systems function exactly as designed — the friction only appears when a manager socialised in one crosses into the other. Brazil's model produces fast decisions with a built-in escape hatch for the person at the top; Sweden's produces slower decisions that are harder to reopen, since reopening means re-litigating a consensus everyone worked to reach. A Brazilian employee dropped into a Swedish team may initially read the lack of directive management as a lack of leadership. A Swedish employee dropped into a Brazilian team may read a paternalistic boss's warmth as a precursor to later being simply told what to do.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Blind (teamblind.com) — A widely shared post from an engineer who moved to Sweden after years in a much more hierarchical tech industry described the shift bluntly: no real hierarchy to navigate, colleagues logging off on time without guilt, and a workplace where mental health was treated as a legitimate factor in decisions rather than a weakness to hide. The contrast with a "boss messages you at 10pm and you answer" culture was, in their account, the single biggest adjustment — in a good way.*

> *Quora — Answering a question on Brazilian workplace norms, one respondent noted that a decision agreed on in a meeting is not always the decision that survives the week — a director above the room can quietly revise it later, and the team is simply expected to adapt without treating the reversal as a broken promise.*

> *Fortune (workplace culture commentary) — A Finnish professional working in Stockholm offered a less flattering read on Swedish consensus: endless discussion, genuine reluctance to take individual responsibility, and a culture where, in their words, everyone talks and it can be surprisingly hard to get anyone to simply commit to finishing something.*

> *InterNations (São Paulo/Rio community) — Advice repeated across several threads for newcomers working under Brazilian managers: build the personal relationship first, and only once real trust exists does raising a concern or pushing back on a decision become something the manager receives as loyalty rather than insubordination.*

> *Quora — One respondent describing years working under a Brazilian boss noted that the paternalism, initially uncomfortable, became one of the better parts of the job: their manager tracked family emergencies, quietly adjusted workloads around personal problems, and treated the team's wellbeing as his own responsibility in a way no manager in a flatter, more procedural workplace ever had.*

### Conclusion

The decision-relevant question for anyone choosing between these two countries is not which management style is more "modern" — both are simply optimised for different things. Brazil offers speed, clarity about who holds authority, and a manager who is likely to treat you as a person worth personally investing in, at the cost of a hierarchy you cannot openly challenge and decisions that can be revised over your head. Sweden offers genuine input into decisions that affect you, a manager who treats you as a peer worth consulting, and slower, more durable outcomes, at the cost of a process that can feel, to anyone raised on faster hierarchies, like nobody is actually in charge.

If you need to know exactly where you stand and who to ask when something goes wrong, Brazil's system will tell you immediately. If you need to feel that your opinion was genuinely weighed before a decision was made, Sweden's system was built for you, assuming you can wait the nine months it might take to get there. Pick your boss accordingly — and if you want to know how each version treats you when you make a mistake, know before you go: one absorbs it personally, the other schedules a meeting about it.
