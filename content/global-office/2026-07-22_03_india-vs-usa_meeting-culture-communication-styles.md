**In Delhi, "Yes" Can Mean Four Different Things. In Chicago, It Means Yes.**

**🇮🇳 India · 🇺🇸 USA**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

An American manager asks an Indian report if the deadline is achievable, hears "yes," and is stunned two weeks later when it isn't — because the actual answer was closer to "I don't want to be the one who says no in this meeting." Hofstede Insights puts India's power distance at 77 against the US's 40, and that gap explains more workplace friction than any language barrier: it isn't about vocabulary, it's about who in the room is allowed to disagree with whom, and how.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇮🇳 India

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Listen for softened refusals — "I'll try" often means no | Don't take a "yes" in a meeting as a firm commitment without follow-up |
| Address seniors formally and route questions up the hierarchy | Don't publicly challenge a manager's decision in front of the team |
| Build relationships through regular calls, not just email threads | Don't expect strict adherence to a meeting's stated agenda |
| Expect meetings to run long and start late | Don't schedule back-to-back meetings assuming punctual starts |
| Read silence from a junior colleague as deference, not agreement | Don't assume no pushback in the room means no objections exist |

#### 🇺🇸 USA

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| State disagreement directly and expect the same in return | Don't interpret American directness as personal disrespect |
| Come prepared to speak up regardless of your rank in the room | Don't wait to be invited to share an opinion — it may never come |
| Expect meetings to start and end on time, agenda intact | Don't assume relationship-building precedes business talk |
| Use first names with managers and executives alike | Don't over-formalize your address — it reads as distant |
| Treat a stated deadline as a real commitment | Don't say "yes" to a timeline you don't believe is achievable |

India's meeting culture runs on relationship infrastructure that the agenda barely reflects. Commisceo Global's research on US-India cultural differences notes Indian workplaces lean on indirect communication to preserve harmony and avoid causing a colleague or superior to "lose face," a habit that traces directly to the country's high power-distance score and its collectivist leanings — Hofstede puts Indian individualism at just 48 against the US's 91. In practice, this means the real content of a meeting often lives in the sidebar conversations before and after it, not in what gets said around the table, and refusal is communicated through hedges — "let me check," "I'll try" — rather than a flat no.

American meeting culture, by contrast, treats the meeting itself as the primary information channel: state your position, expect pushback, resolve it in the room. The US's individualism score of 91 — among the highest Hofstede measures anywhere — corresponds with an expectation that junior employees speak up regardless of seniority, and that a stated deadline is a real commitment rather than an opening position. Global Indian Network's comparison of the two work cultures notes Americans are "usually frank and direct," a trait that reads as efficient inside the culture and abrasive outside it — several of the Indian-origin professionals quoted in Commisceo's research described American colleagues as "too blunt," while Americans described Indian colleagues as "evasive."

**The Reckoning.** The genuine trap for anyone moving between these two systems isn't rudeness on either side — it's that both cultures believe they are being clear. An American stating a blunt objection thinks they're being transparent; an Indian colleague softening a refusal thinks they're being polite and constructive. Nobody in either country believes they're being difficult to read. The practical fix, reported consistently by people who've worked both systems, is to stop treating "yes" as a data point in India and start treating silence as one — and to stop treating American directness as hostility and start treating it as the actual content of the message.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — Someone answering why Indian managers at US software firms often come across as "rude" argued it wasn't rudeness at all, but the transfer of a domestic Indian directness — appropriate toward juniors back home — into an American context where it read very differently across a flatter hierarchy.*

> *Quora — A respondent explaining why Indians say "yes" when they mean "no" or "maybe" traced it to upbringing: saying no outright to a superior or elder is discouraged early in life, so professionals default to hedged, softened language that Americans routinely miss.*

> *teamblind.com — An engineer who moved from an Indian product company to a US tech firm described the adjustment as needing to "just say the disagreement out loud," since staying quiet in a meeting — the safe move back home — was read by American colleagues as having nothing to contribute.*

> *teamblind.com — A poster considering a move back to India after over a decade in the US said the hardest recalibration wasn't work hours but re-learning to read hierarchy again — deferring to a senior's phrasing rather than expecting the flat, say-it-directly norm they'd gotten used to in American meetings.*

### Conclusion

If you're moving from the US to India, the skill to build is patience with ambiguity — learn to hear "I'll try" as information, and stop expecting the meeting's agenda to survive contact with the room. If you're moving from India to the US, the skill is the opposite: say the disagreement out loud, on the record, in the meeting, because silence there isn't read as respect, it's read as absence. The honest version I'd give a friend: in India, listen for what isn't said; in America, say what you actually think, because no one else is going to guess it for you.
