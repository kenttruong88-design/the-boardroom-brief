# In Singapore, the Meeting Is Where Decisions Get Announced. In Canada, It's Where They Get Pre-Approved.

**🇸🇬 Singapore · 🇨🇦 Canada**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

A Singaporean colleague may leave a 15 to 30 second pause before answering a direct question in a meeting — not hesitation, but a sign of careful, respectful consideration. A Canadian colleague will rarely raise a genuinely contested idea in the meeting itself at all, because per workplace guidance, Canadian meetings are "rarely where robust debate happens" — that debate has already occurred quietly beforehand, and the meeting exists to build visible consensus around a decision that's functionally settled. Both cultures will tell a newcomer the meeting is where things get decided. Neither of them quite means it.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇸🇬 Singapore

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Read silence as consideration, not agreement — a pause before answering is respectful, not evasive | Fill every silence immediately — interrupting a considered pause can read as impatient or disrespectful |
| Follow up privately with junior colleagues after a meeting to surface real objections | Assume nodding or "we will try" means a firm yes — both often signal politeness over commitment |
| Let seniority determine who speaks first, and defer accordingly in group settings | Push a junior colleague to openly contradict a senior one in front of the group — raise disagreements one-on-one instead |
| Treat consultation before the meeting as genuine input-gathering, not box-checking | Mistake indirect language ("it may be difficult") for a soft maybe — it usually means no |
| Watch for what isn't said as closely as what is — tone and hesitation carry real information | Take the absence of questions as a sign of full understanding or consensus |

#### 🇨🇦 Canada

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Build consensus in one-on-one conversations before the meeting — that's where the real negotiation happens | Wait for the meeting itself to raise a strong objection — you'll likely be the only one doing so, and it will land badly |
| Frame disagreement diplomatically and privately with the relevant person ahead of time | Publicly challenge a proposal in the meeting without having floated concerns beforehand |
| Expect authority to be exercised through influence and involvement, not directive top-down instruction | Assume a manager's suggestion in a meeting is optional feedback — it's often already a soft decision |
| Treat visible collaboration and being easy to work with as seriously as technical output | Prioritize being right in the room over being seen as a good collaborator — Canadian workplaces weigh both |
| Use polite, indirect phrasing, but say what you actually mean underneath it | Assume Canadian politeness means the message isn't serious — it usually is, just delivered gently |

### Singapore: Hierarchy, Harmony, and the Art of the Considered Pause

Singapore's meeting culture is shaped by a workplace hierarchy that, per multiple business culture guides, remains genuinely present despite the country's modern, efficient veneer: reporting lines are clear, decisions often flow from the top, and seniority frequently determines who speaks first and how directly feedback can be delivered. Layered on top of that hierarchy is an indirect communication style — disagreement is rarely voiced openly in group settings, "yes" doesn't always mean agreement, and phrases like "we will try" function as a polite decline rather than a genuine commitment.

What makes this workable rather than paralyzing is a parallel norm of private consultation: input is often gathered from team members individually before a decision is finalized, meaning the public meeting functions more as ratification of an already-consulted position than as the forum where minds actually change. Newcomers who don't know this tend to either over-read silence as agreement, or under-read a senior colleague's pause as confusion rather than the deliberate, respectful consideration it actually represents.

### Canada: Consensus Theater, Negotiated in Advance

Canadian meeting culture runs on a similarly indirect logic, dressed in different cultural clothing. Business guidance on Canadian workplace norms consistently notes that meetings are rarely the venue for real debate — that happens in prior one-on-one conversations — and that Canadian workplace culture "strongly avoids public conflict or disagreement." Decision-making is described as consensus-driven, with managers expected to involve team members and create space for input, and authority exercised through influence rather than direct instruction.

The practical result is a meeting that looks, on the surface, like open collaborative discussion but functions closer to a presentation of pre-negotiated conclusions, with visible buy-in performed for the room. Canada's Hofstede uncertainty avoidance score (48) sits meaningfully higher than Singapore's famously low 8, which shows up here as a slightly greater Canadian appetite for process and structure around decision-making, even though both cultures share a deep discomfort with visible, public disagreement.

### The Reckoning

The surprising overlap: despite vastly different power distance scores (Singapore high, Canada comparatively low), both cultures independently arrived at the same solution to conflict avoidance — move the real disagreement out of the room and into private, informal conversation, then use the formal meeting to display consensus rather than build it. A newcomer who assumes either country's meetings are the actual decision-making venue will be reliably surprised when the "discussion" produces a decision that was clearly settled beforehand.

The difference is in who's licensed to speak first and how visibly. Singapore's hierarchy openly structures who talks and when, and the norm is legible once you know to look for it. Canada's egalitarian, consensus-driven self-image obscures a similar pre-negotiation dynamic behind a veneer of open collaborative discussion, which paradoxically makes it easier for a newcomer to misread Canadian meetings as more genuinely deliberative than they are.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — A foreign professional who spent nearly a decade in Singapore wrote that the hardest communication skill to learn wasn't the accent or the vocabulary, it was recognizing that a 20-second pause before a colleague's answer was a sign of respect and careful thought, not confusion or disagreement, and rushing to fill that silence early in their tenure had cost them credibility.*

> *r/singapore — An expat manager described junior team members consistently saying "we will try" to deadlines they had no intention of meeting, and only after months realized this phrase functioned as a polite, culturally understood decline rather than the soft commitment it sounded like in English.*

> *Internations Singapore — A European expat noted that the biggest adjustment was realizing that raising a disagreement directly in a group meeting, something normal in their home country, made junior Singaporean colleagues visibly uncomfortable, and that the same feedback delivered one-on-one afterward was received far better.*

> *r/Canada — A newcomer from Asia observed that Canadian meetings felt oddly scripted once they paid attention, since proposals presented as open questions for the room were almost always met with immediate, uniform agreement — a pattern that only made sense once a colleague explained the real conversation had already happened over coffee the day before.*

> *Quora — A Canadian professional who worked in Singapore for two years advised newcomers to treat a lack of questions after a presentation as a signal to seek out one-on-one conversations afterward, since silence in the room was far more often confusion than comprehension.*

### Conclusion

If you're moving from Canada to Singapore, learn to read hierarchy and pauses explicitly — the structure is more visible than in Canada, but it requires you to actively seek private feedback rather than expect it in the room. If you're moving from Singapore to Canada, don't mistake the room's easy, collaborative tone for the actual site of decision-making — the real negotiation in Canada happens quietly, before the meeting you're sitting in even starts.

The honest version, over a drink: in both countries, the meeting is a performance of a decision, not the decision itself. Learn where the real conversation happens — a hallway chat, a coffee, a one-on-one — and you'll understand far more about what's actually going on than anyone will ever say out loud in the room.
