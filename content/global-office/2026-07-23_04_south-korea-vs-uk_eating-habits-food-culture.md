**[HEADLINE]** Desk Sandwich or Second Round: How South Korea and the UK Actually Eat at Work

**🇰🇷 South Korea · 🇬🇧 United Kingdom**

***By Priya Mehta, The Global Office***

A British office worker treats lunch as a personal, faintly guilty forty minutes over a supermarket meal deal and a keyboard; a South Korean office worker treats lunch as a mandatory group activity that, by dinnertime, may have escalated into a mandatory group activity involving soju. Sixty percent of UK employees eat at their desks, largely alone; in South Korea, eating alone at your desk gets quietly noted. Both countries insist they have a food culture. Only one insists you attend it.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇰🇷 South Korea

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Join the team lunch at the company cafeteria (sanae sikdang) daily, even week one | Eat a packed lunch alone at your desk regularly — it reads as opting out of the team |
| Let the most senior person start eating first | Start eating before elders or seniors do |
| Attend at least some hoesik (after-work dinners) — declining every one is noticed | Pour your own drink — pour for others, let them pour for you |
| Use two hands receiving a drink or dish from someone senior | Stick chopsticks upright in rice — it evokes a funeral ritual |
| Pace your drinking at hoesik; refusing outright is harder than sipping steadily | Assume hoesik is optional just because HR policy says so — team norms often override it |

#### 🇬🇧 United Kingdom

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Expect to eat lunch alone at your desk most days — nobody inviting you isn't a snub | Take offense at colleagues eating separately — it's the default, not a rejection |
| Bring a homemade sandwich or grab a meal deal — genuinely the norm, not a budget signal | Expect a long, social lunch hour; most take well under an hour |
| Say yes to the occasional after-work pub trip — the closer UK equivalent of team bonding | Assume the pub trip is mandatory or that declining is held against you |
| Offer to make colleagues tea — small, real social currency | Skip office snack or biscuit kitties — minor, but noticed over time |
| Ask directly if unsure; colleagues will explain, mildly bemused you asked | Expect restaurant lunches daily — eating out is an occasional spend, not routine |

---

**South Korea** treats the workday meal as an extension of the org chart. Research on office workers in urban South Korea finds hierarchy and group harmony visibly shape everyday food choices, a pattern rooted in the same Confucian collectivism captured in Hofstede Insights' data: South Korea scores just 18 on Individualism against a comparatively high 60 on Power Distance, figures that land directly on the lunch table, where the most senior colleague eats first and the group decides where and when. Statistics Korea data shows the collectivism extends to spending too — in 2023, households eating out together spent roughly 137,300 won a month on average, rising with income and household size, evidence that group dining is a budgeted, routine institution rather than an occasional treat.

Where this gets demanding for newcomers is hoesik, the after-work team dinner that often extends into a "second round" of drinking or karaoke. Corporate reform campaigns like "119" (one type of alcohol, one venue, finish by 9pm) exist specifically because unwritten hoesik obligations got treated as extensions of the workday — a tension sharpened by OECD figures showing South Koreans still average around 1,872 working hours annually, well above the UK's 1,531, leaving little appetite for an evening that is technically off the clock but functionally isn't.

**The United Kingdom** built its food culture around the opposite instinct: minimizing the meal's claim on the day. ONS Living Costs and Food Survey data puts average UK household spending on food and non-alcoholic drinks at £63.50 a week, with restaurant spending at £40.50 weekly — modest against Korea's dining-out norms once adjusted for the sheer frequency of Korean group meals. Survey data on British lunch habits found 42% of office workers make their own lunch, usually a sandwich, and 60% eat it at their desk rather than take a proper break — a habit The Conversation's coverage of "sad desk sandwich" culture notes Britons themselves recognize as slightly bleak, and which visibly puzzles European colleagues used to a sit-down midday meal.

This tracks with Hofstede's UK score of 89 on Individualism against South Korea's 18 — among the starkest contrasts the model offers between two developed, expat-heavy economies. The upside for newcomers is real: nobody in a UK office will notice or question you eating alone, skipping lunch, or staying at your desk. The downside is fewer built-in opportunities to bond with colleagues over food — they exist, mainly around the office kettle and the occasional Friday pub trip, but they're opt-in and easy to miss if you're not paying attention.

**The Reckoning:** In South Korea, food is infrastructure for hierarchy and belonging — skipping the team lunch carries social consequences roughly proportional to skipping a team meeting. In the UK, food is a private errand slotted into a day organized around not bothering colleagues. A Korean employee eating alone daily for a month would likely be quietly discussed; a British employee doing the same would, at most, be mildly noted as "keeps to themselves" and otherwise left alone.

The counterintuitive part is which country offers more genuine connection through food versus more food-adjacent obligation. South Korea's structure guarantees regular meals with colleagues, building real familiarity fast, but bundles in drinking expectations many newcomers find taxing. The UK guarantees nothing, restful for the food-shy, but workplace friendships form more slowly and require actively seeking them out — nobody organizes it for you.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *r/korea — a foreign hire at a Seoul firm described their first hoesik running past midnight across two venues, and later realizing that declining a second round cost nothing socially, but skipping the first hoesik entirely early on visibly cooled how the team treated them for weeks.*

> *r/AskUK — a commenter new to a London office, used to a proper sit-down lunch, described genuine culture shock at colleagues eating cold sandwiches at their desks mid-spreadsheet, and taking weeks to stop feeling awkward about being the only one who left the building at lunchtime.*

> *Quora — a Korean office worker, answering whether Koreans should "learn to eat lunch alone like Westerners," pushed back firmly, arguing eating together isn't a burden to escape but genuinely one of the workday's better parts — a reply that surprised commenters expecting resentment.*

> *Blind (teamblind.com) — engineers at Korean offices of multinational tech firms described companies formally adopting hoesik-limiting policies like "finish by 9pm, one venue," with managers still attending in person specifically to normalize leaving early, since junior staff wouldn't leave first on their own.*

> *An expat dining-etiquette guide for newcomers to Korea flagged one easy-to-miss rule: use the spoon, never chopsticks, for rice and soup, and wait for the eldest at the table to lift theirs first — small enough to overlook, awkward enough to be remembered if you get it wrong in front of a new team.*

### Conclusion

The practical decision point is less about which cuisine you prefer and more about how much of your social life you want your job to organize for you. South Korea hands you a ready-made circle of dinner companions whether you asked for one or not, at the cost of evenings and, occasionally, a hangover you didn't plan for. The UK hands you nothing, asks nothing, and leaves you to build lunch friendships from scratch, entirely optional.

Neither is obviously the kinder system — one over-includes, the other under-includes, and which failure mode bothers you more is worth knowing before you sign a contract. If a friend asked me over a drink, I'd say: go to Korea if you want colleagues who'll notice if you vanish, and go to the UK if you'd rather nobody noticed at all — just budget for either a second round of soju or a very sad sandwich, because you're getting one of the two.
