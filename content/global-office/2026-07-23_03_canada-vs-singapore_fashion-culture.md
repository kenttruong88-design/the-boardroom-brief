**[HEADLINE]** Parka or Blazer: What Canada and Singapore Actually Expect You to Wear to Work

**🇨🇦 Canada · 🇸🇬 Singapore**

***By Priya Mehta, The Global Office***

In Toronto, a director can show up to a client pitch in a fleece vest and nobody blinks; in Singapore, the same fleece vest would prompt someone to gently ask if you were feeling unwell. One country has spent a decade quietly abolishing the suit; the other air-conditions its offices to nineteen degrees specifically so the suit can survive. Both call themselves modern, global economies. Only one means it about your wardrobe.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇨🇦 Canada

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Buy a proper -20°C winter coat before your first January | Assume "business casual" means suit-adjacent — outside Bay Street law and banking, it means jeans and a sweater |
| Layer aggressively; buildings run hot, streets run cold | Wear outdoor boots at your desk all day — keep indoor shoes in a drawer |
| Watch what your director wears in week one, then calibrate down | Wear shorts to a client meeting, even in a 30°C summer |
| Keep one blazer on a hook for client visits and reviews | Assume Vancouver-tech casual applies in Bay Street finance, or vice versa |
| Ask HR directly if unsure — it reads as sensible, not naive | Wear strong perfume or cologne — many offices are informally scent-free |

#### 🇸🇬 Singapore

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Default to a long-sleeved shirt and slacks for first meetings in finance, law, or government-linked firms | Wear sleeveless tops, shorts, or open-toed sandals to a bank or ministry, even at 32°C |
| Carry a light jacket — air conditioning is set aggressively cold | Assume "smart casual" invites jeans — conservative sectors still mean tailored trousers |
| Choose tropical wool, linen, or cotton blends that hold a crisp line | Show up underdressed to a client lunch — neatness reads as seriousness |
| Confirm dress code before assuming your tech or creative employer is casual | Wear flip-flops or beachwear outside weekends — buildings will stop you at reception |
| Keep a spare shirt at your desk for the commute-sweat problem | Loosen your tie before senior colleagues do — small deference gestures matter |

---

**Canada** has spent recent years dismantling its own dress code. According to Indeed's Hiring Lab Canada, employers have embraced casual dress at a pace that outstrips comparable G7 markets, with jeans, sneakers, and hoodies now standard even in white-collar settings once reserved for the sports jacket. Randstad Canada frames this as a structural shift, not a pandemic hangover: employers have concluded that policing hemlines does little for retention and a lot for making the office feel unwelcoming. Robert Half's 2026 guidance for Canadian job-seekers is almost anticlimactic — dress slightly more formal for the first two weeks, then match what you observe, because no single national standard remains to memorize.

The exceptions are instructive. Law firms and financial institutions on Bay Street remain formal holdouts, suits intact, while Vancouver's tech sector treats a blazer as an eccentricity. What actually unites the country isn't a dress code so much as a climate code: from November to April, what you wear to survive the commute matters more than what you wear once inside, and every office implicitly budgets for a coat closet, a boot tray, and the ten minutes colleagues spend unwrapping themselves each morning.

**Singapore** has taken the opposite bet, and the logic deserves more credit than "fussiness." In finance, law, and the government-linked corporations anchoring a large share of the economy, business attire stays conservative by design — full sleeves, slacks, closed shoes — despite year-round humidity that makes the whole proposition faintly punishing. Business-etiquette guides for newcomers, including the Expat Association Singapore's briefing, frame dress as a visible marker of the hierarchy and harmony that Hofstede Insights' cultural-dimensions data captures numerically: Singapore scores 74 on Power Distance against Canada's 39, and 20 on Individualism against Canada's 80 — a meaningfully more hierarchical, group-oriented culture, of which dress is one legible expression. The Singapore Fashion Council notes the domestic apparel market has grown past US$2.5 billion, evidence the appetite for looking put-together extends well beyond the office.

Where Singapore surprises newcomers is the gap between industries. Tech, media, and creative firms have pulled toward casual dress, sometimes more aggressively than Canadian counterparts, because they compete for the same global talent. The confusion isn't "how formal is Singapore" but "which Singapore did I land in" — a puzzle no first-week trick solves as neatly as in Canada, since everyone is equally air-conditioned indoors and sweating outdoors regardless of sector.

**The Reckoning:** Canada's dress code is set by the thermometer; Singapore's by the org chart. A Canadian's sartorial anxiety peaks fifteen minutes before leaving the house, guessing between -15°C and -25°C; a Singaporean's peaks on walking into a meeting room and gauging, from the seniority present, exactly how buttoned-up to be. Canada's casualization is a genuine social flattening — the CEO in the hoodie isn't performing modesty, the norm has simply moved. Singapore's formality is a deliberate signal, maintained despite the climate arguing hard against it, because looking prepared still reads as respect owed to the room, not a personal comfort choice.

The counterintuitive twist is which country is harder to dress for. Canada looks easier, but its informality is unevenly distributed by industry and city, tripping up newcomers who assume uniformity. Singapore looks harder, suits in the tropics and all, but is in practice more legible: conservative sectors publish the rules, casual sectors are casual on purpose. You will misjudge Canada by assuming; you will misjudge Singapore only by not asking.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *r/singapore — a new arrival from a European tech firm described turning up to their own bank's branch midweek, in shorts and slides to sort out a work-permit account, and being politely turned away at the door by security until they went home and changed — a rule nobody had written down anywhere.*

> *Blind (teamblind.com) — a tech-industry thread on how formally people dress for work surfaced a recurring split: engineers at Singapore offices of global tech firms reported jeans and t-shirts as completely normal, while colleagues one floor up in the same building, working for a bank tenant, wore full suits — same weather, same elevator, opposite expectations.*

> *Quora — someone relocating for work from a tropical part of Asia to Canada described packing almost entirely wrong: shirts and slacks suited for air-conditioned tropical offices, no real winter layer system, and buying an entire practical wardrobe from scratch within the first two Canadian weeks.*

> *Expat Association Singapore's business-etiquette briefing for newcomers noted that dress there reads as a proxy for preparation and respect, not personal style — its advice to incoming professionals is to dress one notch more formal than seems necessary until someone senior explicitly signals it's fine to relax.*

> *r/toronto — a commenter new to a Bay-Street-adjacent employer described overdressing in a suit for their first week, only to realize every colleague under 40 was in dark jeans and a knit sweater, and spending the rest of the month feeling like the only person who hadn't gotten a memo that was never sent.*

### Conclusion

Choosing between the two purely on dress, Canada will cost you more in outerwear and Singapore will cost you more in attention. Canada's wardrobe budget goes toward surviving a six-month winter; its social budget for reading dress codes is minimal, because the codes have mostly dissolved outside a few formal sectors. Singapore's wardrobe budget is comparatively cheap — a handful of breathable shirts and trousers will do — but it demands you actually notice who outranks whom in the room, because the clothes are doing quiet organizational work Canadians stopped asking of theirs years ago.

Neither is more "professional"; they've simply chosen where to spend their formality — Canada on the coat, Singapore on the collar. If a friend asked which to pack for, I'd say: bring the parka to Canada, the spare shirt to Singapore, and in both places resist dressing like you already know the rules on day one — in one country nobody will correct you, and in the other, someone will.
