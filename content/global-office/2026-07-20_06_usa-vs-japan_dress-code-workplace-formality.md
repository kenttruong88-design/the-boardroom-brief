# The Suit Is Dead in One Country and Load-Bearing in the Other

**🇺🇸 USA · 🇯🇵 Japan**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

Only 3% of American workers now wear a suit to the office, according to Gallup polling, down from 7% in 2019, and 43% report having no formal dress code at all. In Japan, meanwhile, a wrinkled suit or scuffed shoes is still described by career guides as a failure of the most basic professional standard, regardless of how much the suit itself cost. The gap isn't really about clothing. It's about what clothing is doing: in one country it signals almost nothing, and in the other it's a uniform that visually declares your alignment with the group, a concept guides trace to the Japanese idea of *wa*, or harmony.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇺🇸 USA

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Check with HR or a future colleague before your first day — norms vary wildly by company | Assume "business casual" means the same thing at a bank and a startup |
| Lean into quiet, elevated basics if unsure — cashmere and tailored knits read as put-together | Overdress dramatically on day one; it can read as trying too hard |
| Wear what lets you do your job comfortably at most tech and creative firms | Assume client-facing roles allow the same casualness as internal teams |
| Notice what leadership wears — it's often more telling than any written policy | Trust the employee handbook over what people actually wear |
| Keep one polished outfit ready for client meetings or investor pitches | Assume casual dress-down culture applies to every single meeting |

#### 🇯🇵 Japan

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Wear a dark, pressed suit your first weeks regardless of stated policy | Assume a "casual" note in the job posting means jeans are fine day one |
| Switch to Cool Biz lighter attire only once colleagues visibly do (May–Sept) | Remove your tie before your manager or senior colleagues do |
| Keep shoes and shirts immaculate — grooming is judged as closely as the garment | Wear a wrinkled shirt even under an expensive suit |
| Observe how strict your specific company is before relaxing your own dress | Assume international or startup offices follow traditional norms |
| Shop Uniqlo, Aoyama, or AOKI for reliable, affordable business basics | Wear black head-to-toe outside mourning contexts — it carries funereal association |

### The USA: Clothing as Personal Branding, Not Institution

American dress code has been in structural decline for years, and the data shows it accelerating rather than stabilizing. Gallup's polling puts suit-wearing at a historic low, while separate research found formal dress codes have fallen from 1.2% to just 0.2% of US offices over four years. What's replaced hard rules isn't total chaos, though — a TK Maxx-cited 2026 survey found 45% of workers, and 67% of younger millennials specifically, say their work wardrobes have actually become more formal over the past year, suggesting a swing back toward "quiet luxury" basics rather than sweatpants. Dress code has also become a retention lever: 44% of workers say they'd consider switching jobs for a company whose dress code better matched their personal style.

The upshot for a new arrival is that American workplace dress functions more like a personal signaling system than an institutional one. What you wear says something about you as an individual — polished, laid-back, trend-aware — rather than your loyalty to the organization.

### Japan: The Suit as Uniform, Not Choice

Japanese business dress remains built around uniformity as a value, not a constraint to route around. The core standard — navy or charcoal single-breasted suits, crisp white or light-blue shirts, muted ties, skirts hemmed to the knee for women — persists most strongly at large, established companies and in any client-facing role, according to multiple business-attire guides aimed at foreign professionals. The one seasonal relief is Cool Biz, a government-endorsed campaign running May through September that permits removing ties and jackets to cut air-conditioning use, though even then the standard is "muted and professional," not casual.

Crucially, the rules are unwritten as often as they're written, and guides consistently advise newcomers to dress formally their first week and calibrate downward only after observing actual colleague behavior rather than trusting a stated policy. Smaller companies, startups, and international firms do relax considerably, but even there, neatness itself remains non-negotiable in a way American casual culture doesn't demand.

### The Reckoning

The real divergence isn't formal versus casual, it's what the clothing is communicating. American dress increasingly optimizes for individual comfort and personal brand, consistent with a culture that Hofstede Insights scores at 91 on individualism against Japan's 46. Japanese dress optimizes for group cohesion and non-distraction — the suit-as-uniform framing means standing out through clothing is closer to a minor transgression than a style choice, an idea almost inverted from how younger American workers now treat office style as self-expression.

The ironic convergence point is Cool Biz itself: a top-down, institutionally mandated permission to relax slightly, which is a very different mechanism from the organic, bottom-up erosion of American dress codes, even though both produce "you can skip the tie now" as the practical outcome.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — One respondent explained that Japanese office dress varies enormously by company age and size, noting that older, larger firms and any customer-facing sales role still treat a dark suit as non-negotiable even as newer companies relax considerably.*

> *japanlivinglife.com (expat guide) — A newcomer's account described the advice to "dress formally the first week, then watch and adjust" as the single most useful piece of guidance they received, since the written policy and the actual office norm rarely matched.*

> *Internations Tokyo — An American engineer at an international Tokyo firm noted the pleasant surprise that his office skewed far more casual than expected, but said the neatness standard, no wrinkles, no scuffed shoes, remained strict even in jeans and a blazer.*

> *Quora — A longtime Tokyo resident described the practical frustration of shopping for suits as a larger foreign body type, recommending Uniqlo's smart trousers and blazers as reliable alternatives to traditional suit chains that don't always stock larger sizes.*

> *Quora — One American who relocated for a startup role in the US described the opposite culture shock returning from a client-heavy consulting job: colleagues treated his daily blazer as oddly formal, a reminder that "business casual" now reads differently depending on which American office you're in.*

### Conclusion

If you're packing for a move, the practical advice inverts depending on direction: arriving in Japan, over-dress and de-escalate slowly once you've read the room; arriving in the US, assume less is expected of you than you think, but keep one sharp outfit in reserve for the meetings that still matter. The line I'd give a friend packing a suitcase: in Japan, the suit is doing a job for you before you've said a word, so let it; in America, nobody's watching your clothes nearly as closely as you assume, so relax.
