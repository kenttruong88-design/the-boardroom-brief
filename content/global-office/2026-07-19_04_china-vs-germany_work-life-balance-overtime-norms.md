# The 72-Hour Week vs. the 11-Hour Rule: China and Germany Can't Agree on What a Day's Work Even Is

🇨🇳 China · 🇩🇪 Germany

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

A German employee who works more than 10 hours in a single day is, technically, breaking the law. A Chinese employee who works 72 hours in a single week is, technically, also breaking the law — and yet one of these facts surprises absolutely nobody. According to the OECD, Germany logs roughly 1,340 average annual working hours per worker, among the lowest in the world; China's National Bureau of Statistics, by contrast, has recorded enterprise employees averaging around 48 hours a week, which annualizes to somewhere north of 2,300 hours — a gap of nearly a thousand hours a year, or about six extra months of German-paced labor. Both countries have working-hours laws on the books. Only one of them is regularly enforced by the people it protects.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇨🇳 China

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Expect the workday to run well past 6pm in tech, finance, and manufacturing — plan your evening accordingly | Assume your contract's "standard hours" clause reflects actual practice |
| Track your overtime hours yourself; legal caps exist (3 hrs/day, 36 hrs/month) but enforcement is uneven | Sign anything implying you "voluntarily agree" to 996 — such clauses are unenforceable and a red flag |
| Budget for a large annual bonus (often 3–8 months' salary) as part of total compensation, not a surprise | Expect that bonus to arrive without a full year of unclocked extra hours behind it |
| Watch how colleagues handle "invisible overtime" — after-hours messages that never get logged as work | Reply to work chat messages at 11pm just because everyone else does; it resets the baseline for you too |
| Note that some major firms (Midea, DJI) are now enforcing earlier clock-outs — ask about this specifically in interviews | Assume every company has followed suit; the crackdown is uneven and sector-dependent |

#### 🇩🇪 Germany

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Leave when your contracted hours end — staying late by default reads as poor time management, not dedication | Check email or Slack after Feierabend; nobody will answer, and you'll look anxious |
| Log your hours precisely — employers are now legally required to record full daily working time | Treat "flexible working time" as an invitation to simply work more; it caps at 10 hrs/day, 48 hrs/week |
| Take your full 20+ statutory vacation days (most employers offer 25–30) — not taking them raises eyebrows | Schedule non-urgent meetings during someone's known lunch or the 11-hour rest window between shifts |
| Negotiate overtime compensation (pay or time off) explicitly if your contract requires it | Expect overtime to be an unspoken badge of honor — colleagues will read it as inefficiency |
| Get comfortable with direct, low-context communication about workload limits | Soften a refusal to take on extra work; German colleagues generally prefer the blunt "no" to a vague maybe |

### China

China's labor law caps the standard workweek at 40 hours with overtime limited to three hours a day and 36 a month, compensated at 150–300% depending on timing. The gap between that statute and lived experience is the entire premise of the "996" debate — nine to nine, six days a week, a 72-hour schedule that the Supreme People's Court and the Ministry of Human Resources jointly ruled illegal back in 2021, a ruling reaffirmed by a 2025 State Council action plan. The National Bureau of Statistics' own surveys still put average enterprise-employee hours at roughly 44 to 48 a week, well above the legal ceiling, which suggests the ruling has done more to generate headlines than to generate free evenings.

There are signs of movement. According to the South China Morning Post, several major firms have begun enforcing earlier departures — Midea now sends staff home by 6:20pm, and DJI, long associated with late-night offices, has started clearing its buildings by 9pm. But SCMP has also documented the flip side: younger workers responding not with confrontation but with what it terms quiet, almost bureaucratic resistance — slower paperwork, longer bathroom breaks, deliberately unhurried output, a protest conducted entirely within the bounds of showing up on time. Meanwhile a separate strand of reporting on "invisible overtime" — after-hours messages and calls that never get logged as billable work — suggests the boundary between work and non-work in China is less a wall than a suggestion.

Compensation culture complicates any simple verdict. Annual bonuses at large firms, often three to eight months' salary, function as a kind of retroactive payment for a year of unlogged extra hours — a bargain some employees find acceptable and others find galling, depending largely on whether the bonus arrives on schedule.

### Germany

Germany's Arbeitszeitgesetz sets a default 8-hour day (extendable to 10 only if the six-month average holds at 8), mandates an 11-hour uninterrupted rest period between shifts, and — as of recent enforcement guidance — requires employers to record the entirety of daily working time, not merely the overtime portion. Destatis reports full-time employees averaging 39.9 hours a week in 2025, continuing a slow decline from 40.5 hours a decade earlier, while the overall workforce average, pulled down by a rising share of part-time work (31.9% of employees, disproportionately women), sits at 34.0 hours. Violations of the Act are administrative offenses carrying fines up to €30,000 in serious or repeated cases — a level of enforcement infrastructure China's system, on paper stricter in its overtime caps, notably lacks in practice.

The cultural mechanism doing the real work here is Feierabend — the idea, described in workplace guides aimed at newcomers, that the end of the contracted day is a genuine boundary, not a suggestion. Overtime exists and is legally regulated, requiring either explicit contractual agreement or a collective bargaining provision, and must be compensated through pay or time off. But the InterNations Expat Insider Survey found 74% of expats in Germany rated their work-life balance favorably, against 68% globally — a gap that tracks less with GDP than with the simple fact that German managers tend to interpret staying late as a scheduling failure rather than a virtue.

### The Reckoning

Here is the irony Hofstede's data half-explains and half-obscures: Germany scores high on individualism (67) and uncertainty avoidance (65), which should, in theory, produce a culture obsessed with control and self-interest — yet it's China, scoring low on individualism (43) and uncertainty avoidance (30), that has built the more chaotic, boundary-free relationship with the workday, where "invisible overtime" bleeds into evenings precisely because the group, not the contract, sets the norm. Germany's individualism produces a workforce that treats the contract as sacred; China's collectivism produces one where showing solidarity with the team's late night matters more than what the contract says.

The deeper irony is regulatory: China's overtime law is, on paper, more protective than Germany's — a hard cap of 36 monthly overtime hours versus Germany's more permissive "as long as the six-month average holds" formula. But Germany enforces its (looser) law with time-tracking mandates and real fines, while China's (stricter) law competes against a bonus system that quietly rewards ignoring it. A law nobody enforces is a suggestion; a suggestion somebody enforces is a law.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Blind (Tencent company review) — an employee flagged the tradeoff plainly: solid compensation, undermined by having to take late-night calls timed to China business hours, which quietly cancels out any nominal "flexible schedule."*

> *Quora ("What is it like for foreigners to work in China?") — a respondent described how tightly clock-in and clock-out is monitored, down to lunch breaks, and how leaving early or arriving late can require sign-off from four or five separate people — a bureaucratic layer that has nothing to do with the hours themselves and everything to do with visibility.*

> *InterNations Expat Insider respondents — repeatedly cited genuinely disconnecting on vacation and colleagues who don't check email after hours as the most unexpectedly pleasant discovery of relocating to Germany, well above what they'd been led to expect from stereotypes about German rigidity.*

> *The Local (thelocal.de) German workplace guide — newcomers are warned that the culture shock isn't the hours, it's the bluntness: direct, unpadded feedback in meetings that can read as coldness to anyone used to a more indirect style, even as the actual working hours stay humane.*

> *A Reddit thread recapped by the Daily Dot — a German professional who relocated to the US described genuine disorientation at discovering some employers offer under two weeks of paid leave, contrasting it with the 5–6 weeks and mandatory sick pay he'd taken for granted at home — a reminder that Germany's baseline expectations are, globally, the outlier, not the norm.*

### Conclusion

If you are choosing between these two systems, the question isn't which country works harder — China clearly does, by nearly any measure — but which country's rules you can actually trust to describe reality. Germany's laws are strict, enforced, and boring in the best sense: the workday ends when it says it ended, mostly because someone is required to write down when it ended. China's laws are, on paper, comparably strict, but sit alongside a bonus culture and a "the group is still online" norm that quietly overrides them, leaving individual willpower — or a spot on a very short list of newly reformed employers — to do the enforcing instead.

So if a friend asked me, over a drink, which one to pick: take the German contract, take the Feierabend literally, and don't feel guilty about it — guilt is an import, not a local custom.
