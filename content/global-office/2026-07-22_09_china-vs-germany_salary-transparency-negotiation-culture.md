**Germany Is Being Legislated Into Salary Openness. China Got There First, Informally, Through Job Sites That Just Post the Number.**

**🇨🇳 China · 🇩🇪 Germany**

*By Priya Mehta, The Global Office*

Germany must transpose the EU Pay Transparency Directive into national law by June 7, 2026 — a legal mandate forcing employers to disclose salary ranges before interviews and banning them from asking candidates their current pay, in a country where, per Hogan Lovells' research, "pay secrecy" clauses have historically been written directly into employment contracts. China needed no such directive: Michael Page's research on the Chinese mainland notes major job platforms already actively encourage or mandate salary disclosure, and offered ranges at mid-senior levels are explicitly anchored to a candidate's previous salary as a negotiation starting point. Two very different roads arrived at similar destinations, and neither means what an outsider might assume.

### Do's & Don'ts

#### 🇨🇳 China

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Check major job platforms for posted salary ranges before applying | Don't assume salary is a private topic — disclosure is often expected upfront |
| Expect your previous salary to be used explicitly as a negotiation anchor | Don't undersell your salary history if you want leverage on the new offer |
| Be prepared for requests for payslips or bank statements during hiring | Don't be alarmed if this is requested — verify the request is from a legitimate employer first |
| Treat directness about compensation as normal, not rude | Don't import Western salary-discussion taboos into conversations with local colleagues |
| Negotiate early — ranges tend to be less flexible once an offer is formalized | Don't wait until after acceptance to raise compensation concerns |

#### 🇩🇪 Germany

| ✅ Do | ❌ Don't |
|---|---|
| Know that as of the 2026 EU directive, employers can no longer ask your current salary | Don't volunteer your current salary in an interview — you're not required to |
| Request the posted salary range before or during your first interview | Don't assume the older "pay secrecy" culture is still fully in force — the law is shifting it |
| Bring documented evidence and preparation to negotiations, not just an ask | Don't use aggressive tactics — German negotiation culture rewards factual, calm cases |
| Ask HR about your right to request comparable-role pay data post-hire | Don't assume coworkers will openly share their salary — informal taboo persists even as law changes |
| Expect large employers (100+ staff) to face new gender pay gap reporting rules | Don't assume small companies are covered the same way — thresholds matter |

---

China's salary openness is functional rather than cultural in the Western "sharing is caring" sense — it's closer to a negotiation tool than a transparency value. Michael Page's research frames disclosure as something platforms actively encourage precisely because it speeds hiring and gives both sides an anchor point, and one widely discussed Quora thread on Chinese cultural norms around personal information notes that broader reticence about privacy simply doesn't extend to money the way it does in much of the West. The practical effect for a foreign hire is that your previous salary isn't a private historical fact — it's an active input the employer will reference, so understating it going in can cost you real leverage.

Germany's shift is legal, recent, and running against a genuinely older grain. Hogan Lovells' multijurisdictional research on pay transparency describes German employment contracts as having historically codified discretion through formal pay-secrecy clauses — discussing your salary with a colleague wasn't just socially awkward, it was sometimes contractually discouraged. The EU Pay Transparency Directive changes the legal floor dramatically: posted salary ranges before interviews, a ban on asking candidates their current pay, and post-hire rights to request comparable pay data broken down by gender. But Ogletree's coverage of the transition notes companies face real gaps in preparation, and the informal taboo — Quora threads explicitly ask "why is discussing salary still taboo in Germany even when it's not forbidden" — is expected to outlast the legal change by years, the same way social habits usually lag legislation.

**The Reckoning.** The irony is that China, without any comparable legal mandate, has produced more functional day-to-day salary transparency than Germany currently has on the books, even as Germany's incoming law is, on paper, more comprehensive and rights-based. China's transparency serves the employer's negotiation process as much as the employee's; Germany's incoming transparency is explicitly designed to correct structural pay gaps, particularly by gender. If you're negotiating in China, expect the openness but use it strategically. If you're negotiating in Germany post-2026, use your new legal rights actively — because the cultural habit of staying quiet about it will not disappear just because the law changed.

### The Part the Brochure Left Out

> *Quora — Someone asked why discussing salary remains taboo in Germany even though it isn't explicitly forbidden, and responses pointed to a long-standing cultural association between financial openness and rudeness — a norm strong enough that even new legal transparency rights are expected to go underused at first.*

> *teamblind.com — A widely cited tech industry data point found over 60% of workers had been told directly by management not to discuss salary with colleagues, despite this being legally unenforceable in many jurisdictions — illustrating how strongly informal pressure can outweigh formal rights on this specific topic.*

> *Quora — A respondent addressing why Chinese colleagues might ask about someone's salary directly explained that in Chinese social norms, financial questions aren't treated with the same privacy weight as in Western culture, and were more often read as friendly interest than intrusion.*

> *Quora — Someone navigating a Chinese company's hiring process described being asked to provide bank statements and payslips as part of an offer, and advised verifying the request came from a legitimate HR process before complying, since the practice — while not unusual there — differs sharply from Western hiring norms.*

### Conclusion

If you're negotiating a role in China, come prepared with your actual salary history documented and ready to discuss, because it will be used as an anchor whether or not you volunteer it. If you're negotiating in Germany after mid-2026, know your new rights under the EU directive and actually use them — ask for the range, request comparable pay data, don't stay quiet out of old habit. The honest version: in China, the number gets discussed early and used against you if you're not ready; in Germany, the law now says you can ask — the harder part will be believing it's actually fine to.
